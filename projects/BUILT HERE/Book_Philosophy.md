# BUILT HERE — BOOK PHILOSOPHY

## Why This Book Exists

Built Here exists to answer one question:

**Why has Cedar Rapids repeatedly produced extraordinary entrepreneurs, and how did those entrepreneurs create a culture that continued producing builders long after they were gone?**

This is not a history of Cedar Rapids.

It is a history of builders.

---

## The Reader's Experience

The reader should forget they are reading history.

They should feel they are spending time with remarkable people making difficult decisions under uncertainty.

History should emerge naturally from those lives.

---

## The Protagonists

The protagonists are always people.

Every entrepreneur wants something.

Every entrepreneur risks something.

Every entrepreneur changes something.

---

## The City

Cedar Rapids is not the protagonist.

It is the opportunity, the constraint, and the inheritance.

The city shapes its builders.

Its builders reshape the city.

---

## The Pattern

Every chapter answers:

**What did this entrepreneur leave behind that another builder could use?**

The inheritance may be:

- a company
- a technology
- a market
- a workforce
- an institution
- a lesson
- a culture
- a second chance

---

## Historical Integrity

Never invent facts, dialogue, events, or motives.

Write confidently when the evidence strongly supports a conclusion.

---

## Story Before Timeline

Chronology organizes the narrative.

It never gives the narrative meaning.

Meaning comes from human decisions.

---

## Every Chapter Must Earn Its Place

If removing a chapter does not weaken the book's central argument, the chapter does not belong.

---

## The Human Test

Readers should remember people before dates.

If they cannot explain why the entrepreneur could not walk away from what they were trying to build, the chapter is unfinished.

---

## The Ending

The book begins with possibility.

It ends with inheritance.

Readers should leave believing that builders inherit opportunity—and have a responsibility to leave something useful for those who come next.

---

## The Standard

Great cities are not built by history.

They are built by ordinary people who repeatedly choose extraordinary responsibility.
