# Chapter 19: The Machine Was Only the Beginning

Paul Lattner had a machine to sell and a market still waiting to be built.

An automobile could reach Cedar Rapids by rail, gleam behind glass, and draw a crowd. Then the real trouble began. Its owner needed fuel or electricity, tires, batteries, repairs, replacement parts, storage, and eventually a buyer for the used vehicle. Every missing service weakened the original sale. Every breakdown became an argument against the machine.

Paul and his brother Joe committed themselves before that supporting system was complete. In October 1909, they commissioned a building for the Cedar Rapids Auto Company at 212–216 Fourth Avenue. The record does not preserve the project’s price or final cost, but the business decision is unmistakable: they put fixed space behind an uncertain market.

The premises came before any surviving sales history that could justify them. Paul was betting that curiosity could become ownership—and that ownership would create enough recurring problems to sustain a business.

He was not alone. By 1909, a city directory counted eight automobile-related concerns, six described as dealers. A later claim that the Lattners founded Cedar Rapids’s first dealership in 1902 cannot be settled against the surviving contemporary record, and accounts disagree over whether the city’s first automobile appeared in 1898 or whether W. G. Haskell’s horseless carriage arrived in 1900. Clarence Miller, William G. Dows, and an Olds-related seller also appear in the early history.

Paul did not need to win the argument over who was first. He had chosen the harder distinction: being early enough to confront everything that did not yet work.

## Selling the Unfamiliar

In March 1910, Cedar Rapids newspapers reported an automobile show. The event left no reliable count of paid deliveries, but its purpose is clear. Dealers brought unfamiliar machines together so the public could see and compare them. The automobile did not sell itself. It required explanation, demonstration, and confidence.

Paul’s later sales language reveals the objection he was trying to overcome. In 1915, he argued that “the savings in repair expense” were the important savings. It was a seller’s claim, not a verified customer result, but that is what makes it revealing. He was asking buyers to look past the price in the window and consider the continuing economics of ownership.

That pitch admitted the vulnerability at the heart of the trade. A vehicle would be judged again whenever it failed, needed a part, or consumed money. A dealer’s reputation depended on far more than the initial transaction.

The market multiplied around those needs. By 1913, a directory synthesis counted twenty businesses under automobiles and garages, in addition to concerns listed for repairs, supplies, and tires. The categories overlap, but the direction does not: Cedar Rapids was developing several businesses around one durable machine.

The heavy equipment that built roads had made movement possible. Paul’s task began where the road builders stopped. A route was useful only if someone could put a dependable vehicle on it and keep that vehicle moving.

## A Building for Promise and Trouble

Automobile commerce gathered east of downtown in the district that became Automobile Row. The businesses changed rapidly; the useful architecture lasted.

At 616 Second Avenue SE, Barton-Ford appeared in 1912 and Abbott-Detroit in 1913. Battery Equipment Company followed in 1914. P. M. Lattner Sales Company occupied the address from 1917 through 1919. Moore Auto Sales, Carringer Auto Company, J. W. Smith’s Central Auto, and Russell Cadillac operated nearby. In 1917, Central Auto and Russell Cadillac occupied newly constructed district buildings.

Those structures joined aspiration to failure. Broad windows displayed the new machine to the street. Open spans made room for automobiles instead of shelves. Vehicular bays led from showrooms to repair areas. Warehouses held parts. Corner stations received cars from the street. Alleys, lots, and parking became working space.

The showroom presented freedom. The service bay acknowledged what freedom would cost.

Paul’s move into the district placed him inside a commercial system that no proprietor had designed as a whole. His company offered the Milburn Light Electric in a 1919 advertisement. At the same address, a battery concern had preceded him. The combination preserves an early fork in the road: Cedar Rapids automobile commerce did not yet mean gasoline alone.

Selling an electric car meant selling confidence in the support around it. Battery service was part of the proposition, just as Paul’s promise of lower repair expense was part of it. The vehicle and the system behind it had to be sold together.

Gasoline vehicles created their own surrounding trades. Filling and service stations supplied energy. Tire and radiator businesses specialized around components that wore out or failed. Parts wholesalers and retailers connected standardized goods to local repairs. Garages kept vehicles in service. Used-car lots handled the machines that came back.

No one business controlled the entire ecology. Each unresolved problem gave another entrepreneur a reason to enter.

That was Paul Lattner’s great business insight, whether or not he ever stated it: a technology becomes a market only when builders create everything around it.

## When the Street Became an Asset

For Paul and the dealers around him, the street was part of the business.

From 1913 through 1921, the Lincoln Highway followed First Avenue near Automobile Row. From 1922 through 1938, it ran along Second Avenue through the district. Through traffic bypassed downtown beginning in 1939, after construction of the Eighth Avenue bridge in 1938.

No surviving record says Paul chose his location because of the highway, and no sales series measures how many travelers became customers. Yet the geography explains the commercial logic. Dealers needed visible frontage, room for turning and parking, bridge access, and proximity to fuel, parts, and repairs. When a transcontinental route passed a showroom, the pavement outside became part of the sales apparatus.

The risk was equally plain. A public routing decision could move commercial advantage without moving a single building.

Maps from 1913 and the revised 1949 record the larger consequence. Blocks that had been substantially residential became heavily occupied by automobile uses. The maps do not reveal why particular residents left, what owners were paid, or whether every dealer prospered. They show what the market Paul entered eventually demanded: houses giving way, parcel by parcel, to showrooms, service spaces, warehouses, stations, parking, and open lots.

The automobile did not remain an object inside the city. It began changing the city to fit itself.

Yet the bypass did not end Automobile Row in one blow. Schamberger received a permit in November 1952 and opened at 719 Third Avenue in the spring of 1953. Kadlec opened at 803 Third Avenue that year. Automobile operations remained in the district through 1965.

Commercial geography changes on several clocks. A route can move while a lease continues. A franchise can pass to a successor. Service can remain after new-car sales shift. The district’s advantage faded gradually because it had never belonged to one firm alone.

## The Car That Came Back

The used automobile exposed the second half of the dealer’s business.

Rapids Chevrolet appeared at 601 Second Avenue beginning in 1924. Culver Motors later occupied the address and maintained a separate used-car lot at 704 Third Avenue, visible in a photograph from about 1950. New automobiles belonged behind showroom glass. Trade-ins and used inventory required another kind of ground.

That lot embodied the vehicle’s commercial afterlife. A car leaving the showroom would someday return as something to appraise, repair, price, and sell again. A dealer could not think only about the first owner. The business had to prepare for the second sale before the first machine came back.

Culver’s ending shows how untidy that process could be. Its occupancy at 601 Second Avenue ends in 1956 in one compiled record, while a later open letter places its final new-car sale on August 31, 1957. McGrath Pontiac appears at the address from 1957 through 1965. Occupancy, last sale, franchise succession, inventory transfer, and legal closure did not have to occur together.

Addresses outlived names. At 616 Second Avenue, a dealer gave way to a battery concern and then to Paul’s company. At 601, one automobile business followed another. The district’s deepest asset was not the permanence of a proprietor. It was the ability of its buildings and neighboring trades to keep answering the same family of problems.

Finance may also have entered that apparatus. Phoenix Finance Corporation occupied space at 612 Second Avenue in 1932, though its address alone does not prove that it financed automobile purchases. The larger threshold was real: a customer could want a machine without having the cash to buy it. Credit could transform price into payments, mobility into obligation, and a sale into a longer exposure for both borrower and lender.

Paul had faced the earlier version of that uncertainty. His documented decisions ran from a purpose-built premise to public promotion, from an operating-cost argument to an electric-car offer, and from a scattered young trade into Automobile Row.

No surviving balance sheet says whether those choices made him wealthy. His legacy does not depend on it.

Paul Lattner entered while the commercial form of the automobile was still unfinished. He committed space before demand was secure. He sold the continuing cost of ownership, not merely the machine. He offered one propulsion technology while the market was still choosing among them. And he helped establish the premise on which Automobile Row grew: every vehicle sold would create future work.

Mechanics, attendants, parts handlers, clerks, bookkeepers, cleaners, drivers, and lot workers made the system function. The promise in the window depended on bays, bins, pumps, ledgers, and hands behind it.

That is what Paul helped build. Not simply a dealership, and not a disputed claim to being first, but a local market sturdy enough to support the machine after the novelty wore off.

The next builder would challenge a different system built around machinery. Milo Barta saw that factories had organized tasks, risks, and authority with great precision. His question was whether workers could organize themselves with equal durability—and change the bargain from inside the gate.
