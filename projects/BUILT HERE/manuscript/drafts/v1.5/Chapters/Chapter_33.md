# Chapter 33: Nathan Boelkins and the Center That Moved

Nathan Boelkins led a business from Cedar Rapids whose ultimate center was no longer in Cedar Rapids.

In 2025, Collins Aerospace identified him as the leader of Avionics, one of two strategic business units based in the city. Generations of Collins engineers and workers had made Cedar Rapids an aerospace center. The Avionics organization still carried that inheritance. But the old headquarters at 400 Collins Road NE now belonged inside RTX, a corporation headquartered elsewhere and operating through hundreds of sites.

Boelkins did not need to prove that Cedar Rapids could build avionics. His problem was harder to see and impossible to solve once: keep the city’s accumulated capability consequential inside an enterprise with competing claims on capital, programs, people, and authority.

This was the risk of inheritance. Boelkins received a concentration of skill without receiving final control over its future.

Cedar Rapids remained a center. It was no longer the only center that mattered.

## When Headquarters Became Heritage

The modern form of Boelkins’s challenge began with an apparent homecoming.

On June 29, 2001, Rockwell International distributed all outstanding Rockwell Collins shares to Rockwell shareholders. The shareholder notice identified the newly separate corporation at 400 Collins Road NE. Rockwell Collins’s final standalone annual filing would still describe Cedar Rapids as company headquarters and present a portfolio spanning commercial and military aviation and communications.

For sixteen years, Cedar Rapids again held the headquarters of a public aerospace company bearing the Collins name. Yet independence did not restore Arthur Collins’s founder era. Shareholders owned the corporation. Customers, regulators, suppliers, and global markets shaped its choices. The city was the center, but it was never the whole world around the company.

Then came the agreement that changed the address without moving it.

On September 4, 2017, Rockwell Collins agreed to a transaction under which it would survive as a wholly owned subsidiary of United Technologies. The acquisition closed on November 26, 2018. UTC combined Rockwell Collins with UTC Aerospace Systems to form Collins Aerospace.

The scale was immense: 70,000 employees, 300 sites, and $23 billion in pro forma annual sales, with more than $500 million in projected run-rate pretax cost savings by the fourth year. Those were global ambitions, not Cedar Rapids results. But every promised synergy carried a local question. Which systems would merge? Which suppliers would remain? Which functions overlapped? Which programs would receive investment? Where would the decisive approval sit?

By the time Boelkins appeared in the 2025 leadership structure, the transformation could be read in the titles. Avionics was based in Cedar Rapids. Mission Systems, led by Heather Robertson, was based there too. Troy Brunk led Collins Aerospace. RTX sat above them.

A strategic business unit is not a branch office. It can hold leadership, engineering, programs, and customer relationships. But “based in Cedar Rapids” does not mean every staffing, investment, supplier, bid, and portfolio decision ends there.

Boelkins’s title marked what the city retained. The corporate chain marked what it had surrendered.

## The Business That Left Collins but Stayed Here

The clearest test of Cedar Rapids’ aerospace strength came through a forced separation.

When United Technologies and Raytheon prepared to combine in 2020, federal antitrust action required the sale of UTC’s military-GPS business. The government defined that business around military receivers and anti-jam products and required a broad transfer of assets and personnel associated with the work.

The legal boundary revealed how difficult a technical enterprise is to move cleanly. Two Iowa facilities—855 35th Street NE in Cedar Rapids and 2855 Heartland Drive in Coralville—were expressly excluded from the facilities being divested. Buildings, equipment, intellectual property, people, and work did not form one indivisible object.

The hold-separate order treated expertise itself as vulnerable. It required the maintenance of staffing, training, working conditions, and technical knowledge while the sale proceeded. Federal lawyers were not protecting nostalgia. They were protecting a viable business from losing the people who made it work.

BAE Systems became the successor associated with the military-GPS operation. Cedar Rapids later described a new BAE facility as a 200,000-square-foot, $173 million project tied to 650 retained jobs. Other public descriptions used different figures, but they agreed on the central consequence: a substantial aerospace operation moved outside Collins ownership and remained rooted in Cedar Rapids.

That outcome split Boelkins’s inheritance. Collins retained Cedar Rapids-based Avionics and Mission Systems. BAE held a local navigation and sensor operation descended from work separated during the merger process. The city lost organizational unity without losing all the capability that unity had gathered.

Ownership mattered. It changed reporting lines, capital access, systems, badges, benefits, and the power to close, sell, or expand. Geography mattered too. If the work and people had scattered, Cedar Rapids could have lost the teams and routines from which the next program would be staffed. By remaining concentrated, the divested business gave BAE an operating base and left Collins beside a labor market no merger agreement could manufacture.

For Boelkins, the new competitor was also proof. Part of the local inheritance could leave the Collins organization without leaving the city. The deepest asset was not the sign above one door. It was the practiced ability of people to do difficult work together.

## Winning Work in a Company of Centers

That capability still had to earn the next assignment.

In March 2025, RTX announced an $80 million contract for Black Hawk avionics upgrades and said the work would take place in Cedar Rapids and Huntsville. The announcement did not divide tasks or dollars between the cities. Its importance was narrower and stronger: Cedar Rapids was named as a place where current work would be done.

In January 2026, RTX announced a $438 million FAA radar-system replacement contract from a Cedar Rapids dateline. The dateline alone did not establish where design, manufacturing, installation, certification, or support would occur. A December 2024 announcement of a $95 million MAPS Gen II order likewise could not be folded into the BAE military-GPS story. Collins and BAE were now separate companies with separate product boundaries.

These awards and announcements showed customers entrusting Collins with complex work. They did not settle Cedar Rapids’ question: how much of that work would be built here?

That question sat inside Boelkins’s role. Avionics being based in Cedar Rapids gave the city an operating claim, not a claim to every task. Large-company entrepreneurship arrives as work distributed across sites. A local leader must make the inherited center valuable enough that the wider company chooses it again.

Collins’s resources offered extraordinary opportunity. Its 2025 fact sheet reported more than 80,000 employees, including more than 20,000 engineers, along with $28.3 billion in adjusted 2024 sales and $4 billion in annual investment. RTX later reported more than $30 billion in 2025 Collins segment sales and a $42 billion backlog. None of those totals belonged specifically to Cedar Rapids. They described the scale of the system in which Boelkins competed.

That scale could feed the local center or bypass it. A large backlog might sustain current work without creating jobs. A global investment budget might transform an Iowa operation or go elsewhere. The Collins name could rise while the city’s share narrowed.

Boelkins’s task was therefore not custodial. He could not preserve the inheritance by admiring it. Cedar Rapids had to remain useful to customers and difficult for RTX to replace.

## The Asset That Can Walk Away

Aerospace capability sounds abstract until it begins to leave.

It lives in systems engineers and software developers, but also in technicians, assemblers, inspectors, laboratory employees, maintenance workers, program managers, supply-chain staff, and customer-support teams. It lives in routines: how a discrepancy is found, a configuration controlled, a supplier qualified, a test completed, and a product accepted.

The antitrust order recognized this when it required expertise, training, and staffing to be maintained. A technical business can be damaged before a building closes. Enough departures can hollow out an address while the sign remains lit.

That made Boelkins’s inheritance larger than a campus and less secure than one. A building can be sold. A business unit can be reorganized. A contract can end. Capability persists only while people perform consequential work, teach others, and win the next chance to use what they know.

Cedar Rapids had reasons for confidence. Two Collins strategic business units remained based there. BAE had made a separate local commitment linked to divested military-GPS work. A recent Collins announcement explicitly placed work in Cedar Rapids alongside Huntsville.

The city also had reason to resist complacency. Ultimate ownership sat elsewhere. Public totals did not reveal local payroll, supplier spending, layoffs, capital, or decision rights. A heritage center can become an interchangeable site if the larger company no longer needs what makes it distinct.

Boelkins stood at that intersection: locally based, globally embedded, rich in inherited capability, exposed to distant choice.

## Keeping a Center Alive

Nathan Boelkins did not found Collins Aerospace. His significance lies in the unfinished problem founders leave behind.

Arthur Collins had made Cedar Rapids a place where advanced communications could be imagined, engineered, and produced. Later generations expanded that capability into avionics and mission systems. Corporate combinations then gave the enterprise reach and capital Arthur Collins never possessed while moving final authority farther from the city.

The military-GPS divestiture supplied the pivotal lesson. A capability could be cut from one owner, survive under another, and remain part of Cedar Rapids. The city’s aerospace legacy was no longer one corporation. It was a concentration of people and practice capable of outlasting the corporate chart.

But inheritance is not permanence. Boelkins and the people around him had to keep earning work important enough to shape the larger enterprise.

A place keeps an industry only while its skills remain harder to abandon than its headquarters.

That was the consequence of the center moving: Cedar Rapids could no longer rely on corporate identity to guarantee local significance. It had to rely on capability—and on leaders willing to renew it.

The next entrepreneur would confront the same problem without the protection of a global parent. In 2020, Cedar Rapids shopkeepers and small-business owners carried rent, payroll, inventory, debt, and personal savings into a pandemic. Then the derecho arrived. Boelkins had a worldwide system around his local center. John Pinkerton’s earlier lesson now returned under harsher conditions: the size of a bet determines how many bad turns a builder can survive before the next decision disappears.
