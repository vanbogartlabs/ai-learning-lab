# Chapter 34: John Pinkerton and the Size of the Bet

John Pinkerton understood before disaster made the lesson brutal: survival often begins with the size of the first commitment.

When That One Cupcake Place entered NewBo City Market in 2012, Pinkerton described the appeal as “low risk.” The phrase did not mean cupcakes without consequence. His business still needed equipment, ingredients, licenses, insurance, labor, and cash. It meant the stall asked less of the company at once than a stand-alone storefront would.

Two years later, the business opened a separate store. It remained at NewBo for nearly six years before leaving. Pinkerton’s path showed risk being increased in stages—test, learn, commit again.

In 2020, Cedar Rapids businesses faced the opposite problem. They already had obligations when the world changed the odds. The pandemic altered customer demand and the legal terms of operation. Then, on August 10, a derecho struck a business community carrying months of disruption. Owners could not return their leases, payrolls, inventories, debts, and personal commitments to the counter and begin again with a smaller bet.

Pinkerton’s earlier choice became the lens for a harsher question: when revenue, power, communications, and time fail together, how much obligation can a business carry before it loses the ability to choose?

## Obligations Outlive Customers

By April 6, Iowa’s rules divided commerce category by category. Restaurants and bars remained closed to the general public through April 30, though carryout, drive-through, and delivery were allowed. Fitness centers, salons, barbershops, theaters, casinos, specified retailers, and other establishments faced continued closures. Gatherings of more than ten were prohibited.

The rules changed what was legal. They did not create customers.

A restaurant allowed to sell carryout still had to generate enough orders. A shop outside a closed category could lose traffic, workers, contracts, or suppliers. “Open” and “closed” became weak descriptions of business condition because the real pressure sat behind the door: rent, payroll, debt, utilities, insurance, inventory, and the owner’s household.

Pinkerton had chosen a smaller doorway because it left room to discover whether demand could support those obligations. Existing owners in 2020 had already made larger commitments. They were trying to carry yesterday’s cost structure through a market that no longer behaved as expected.

Federal assistance offered time to some businesses, but it came through a sequence: learn of a program, qualify, apply, provide records, receive a decision, obtain money, and meet later terms. Nationally, early Paycheck Protection Program lending tilted toward larger businesses within the program’s broad small-business universe. That does not establish what happened in Cedar Rapids. It does reveal why the existence of help and an entrepreneur’s ability to use it are different facts.

By summer, local owners were no longer choosing whether to take a first risk. They were deciding how long they could carry accumulated ones.

Then the wind arrived.

## The Afternoon That Changed the Equation

The August 10 derecho brought unusually long, destructive winds across Linn County. The National Weather Service identified a damage swath consistent with intermittent winds of 100 to 130 miles per hour and estimated a maximum near 140 miles per hour at one southwest Cedar Rapids apartment complex. Those estimates do not describe every business address. They establish the force of the event that crossed the city.

For an owner, wind speed was not the final measure. A roof, window, machine, refrigerator, sign, internet connection, or power line could determine whether commerce continued. The municipal response itself operated through extended outages, failed primary communications, and ongoing pandemic restrictions. A later Linn County report described widespread losses of electricity, cell service, and internet, while stakeholders said the pandemic multiplied the impact and reduced organizational capacity.

The two emergencies did not merge into one neat cause. A company could have lost customers before suffering storm damage. It could have received pandemic assistance but lacked electricity. It could have escaped physical damage and still lost communications or customer access. It could reopen while carrying new debt.

What joined the emergencies was time. Every interruption allowed obligations to keep arriving while the transactions meant to meet them slowed or stopped.

Pinkerton’s low-risk stall had purchased time to learn. The derecho took time away.

## One Hundred Eleven Answers

Cedar Rapids tried to count the pressure quickly. On August 27, the city posted a business storm-loss questionnaire and asked for responses by September 30. One hundred eleven voluntary respondents reported $133,387,720 across the survey’s categories. That included $69,360,465 for structures and real property, $41,473,778 in lost sales, and $9,166,133 in lost payroll.

The total was not a final citywide bill. Large employers represented 37 percent of respondents, and the city described the amount as a minimum rather than a census of every establishment. But the responses exposed the anatomy of business risk. Buildings were damaged. Sales vanished. Payroll was lost. Obligations continued while the ability to produce cash had been interrupted.

Among respondents, 50.5 percent expected insurance payments. Expecting payment was not receiving it, and receiving it was not the same as being made whole in time. Another 28.7 percent reported taking on additional debt in 2020. The survey did not divide that borrowing cleanly between pandemic losses, storm damage, and other needs. It showed risk changing form. Broken property could become a claim. Interrupted trade could become a loan. Missing revenue could become a smaller paycheck or an owner’s foregone income.

The sharpest numbers measured time and liquidity. More than one fifth of respondents selected less than four months of adequate working capital. Among service and retail businesses, the share was 32.8 percent. Meanwhile, 22.6 percent reported more than a week of lost operating time, and 32.1 percent reported two weeks or more.

Those numbers cannot be matched firm by firm. Their collision still explains the danger. Two weeks is not merely fourteen days to a company with little working capital. It is fourteen rounds of choices among repair, payroll, inventory, debt, and the next attempt to reopen.

Every day can force a decision. Pay for a roof or protect cash for workers. Replace spoiled inventory or wait for insurance. Reopen with incomplete communications or lose another day of sales. Borrow against a future that has become harder to see.

The survey did not tell those stories by name. It recorded the pressure points where business judgment was being squeezed: premises, sales, payroll, debt, insurance, and working capital.

## When the Next Choice Narrows

A small firm is not a miniature corporation.

It may have no alternate site, no internal transfer system, no specialized finance department, and no second division to absorb a loss. The owner may be the emergency planner, loan applicant, customer contact, purchaser, and person responsible for the damaged premises. Size does not determine courage or outcome. It determines how many options may remain when several systems fail.

That is why Pinkerton belongs at the center of this chapter even though his documented NewBo trajectory is not a claim about what happened to his business in the 2020 emergencies. His decision makes the hidden variable visible. He began in a structure designed to leave room for another move.

The pandemic and derecho tested whether established businesses could preserve the same room.

Power mattered because it kept equipment and payments working. Communications mattered because owners needed customers, workers, lenders, insurers, and current information. Credit mattered because repair and payroll arrived before recovery. Insurance mattered only to the extent that coverage fit the loss and payment arrived in time. Public assistance mattered at the moment of receipt, not the day of announcement.

A federal disaster declaration followed on August 17. By October 28, more than $19 million in Small Business Administration disaster loans had been approved statewide for Iowa homeowners, renters, and small businesses. The figure does not identify Cedar Rapids firms or separate business borrowers. It does reveal the scale of a system trying to move money toward people already spending time they did not have.

For an entrepreneur, each application step consumed capacity. Information had to be found while communications were impaired. Documents had to be recovered while homes and workplaces also required attention. Decisions had to be awaited. Money had to arrive before the obligation it was intended to cover became irreversible.

Assistance announced too late can be accurate and useless at the same time.

## Survival Is Another Bet

A reopened door is not a recovered business.

Revenue may still be low. Workers may have lost hours. Repairs may be deferred. Insurance may remain unsettled. New debt may replace immediate danger with a longer burden. An owner may preserve the company by transferring costs into a household or future year.

This is where the popular language of resilience becomes too easy. Resilience is not the ability to look restored from the street. It is the ability to keep the next decision possible.

Pinkerton’s stall had preserved choices: continue, expand, remain, or leave. The emergencies of 2020 put thousands of choices under pressure at once. A healthy entrepreneurial system cannot promise that every firm will survive every shock. It can keep power, communication, finance, insurance, customers, and assistance available long enough for business judgment to matter.

The city’s 111 answers could not produce a final score. They did something more urgent. They showed what was at stake when a business lost time: not just today’s sale, but tomorrow’s freedom to decide.

John Pinkerton’s lesson had grown larger than his stall. The best first bet leaves room for a second. The best recovery protects the same thing.

The final builder in this book would make that second move visible. Sydney Rieckhoff began Almost Famous Popcorn in a booth at NewBo and, after about eighteen months, moved into a shop across the street. Her journey would return the story to its opening promise. Builders inherit what others leave—but inheritance becomes a future only when someone chooses to cross the distance.
