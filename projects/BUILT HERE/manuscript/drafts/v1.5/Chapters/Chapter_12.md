# Chapter 12: Greene Powers the Schedule

W. J. Greene’s railway problem began before any passenger read a timetable. The cars could not keep a schedule unless the power plant could support one.

A December 1907 engineering article identified Greene as manager of the lighting department of the Cedar Rapids & Iowa City Railway & Light Company. It connected him to an improved boiler operating in the company plant and to an order for another. The account does not define his full authority or show how costs were divided between lighting and railway operations. It does reveal the business he was helping to manage: electricity and transportation occupied the same corporate and physical system.

Greene’s immediate challenge was integration. A public schedule looked simple—places and times printed in rows. Behind it stood generation, overhead systems, track, rolling stock, repair, crews, bridges, and dispatch. The promise made at the station depended on machinery the passenger rarely saw.

Cedar Rapids had been learning that lesson for decades. A preservation history places the organization of the Cedar Rapids and Marion Street Railway Company in 1879. It reports steam cars running between Marion and Twelfth Street in 1880, with horse cars continuing downtown. By 1882, another line ran through downtown along Third Street. Electrification followed in 1891.

The sequence moved from steam and horse power to electricity in little more than a decade. Each change enlarged the operating problem. Track alone was never enough. Service required motive power, schedules, fares, transfers, maintenance, and permission to use the streets. By 1910, Cedar Rapids reportedly had more than thirteen miles of local track, though the figure does not distinguish every route, siding, or double-tracked segment.

Greene’s world became more demanding when the local network met the ambition to connect Cedar Rapids and Iowa City. Competing interurban proposals appeared by 1902. A government study reports that Iowa Railway & Light Company constructed the corridor in 1903 and 1904 and began electrified passenger and freight service on August 13, 1904 over twenty-seven miles. Contemporary accounts used the name Cedar Rapids & Iowa City Railway & Light Company. The legal identities changed, but the operating proposition endured: one corridor would serve two very different businesses.

Passenger service sold time and access. Freight service sold movement of goods. They could share track and power without sharing the same economics.

From the beginning, freight was not an afterthought. A 1907 professional account said the company had immediately handled less-than-carload freight in a baggage car and moved carloads by electric locomotive. Another account that year described a dispute over joint cereal and livestock rates with the Chicago and North Western. Evidence was reportedly heard in Cedar Rapids on January 29 and 30.

The rate fight exposed the scale of the freight ambition. The line was described as about twenty-eight miles long, with a private right-of-way one hundred feet wide for nearly the entire distance and construction to steam-railway standards. It reportedly handled 450 livestock cars in the prior year. The underlying Interstate Commerce Commission decision is not available, so those figures remain reported evidence rather than final regulatory findings. They still make one fact unmistakable: this was not merely a streetcar stretched between two cities.

For Greene, mixed operations made power a strategic resource. A boiler problem could threaten more than illumination. Capacity had to serve a company whose assets supported passengers, freight, shops, and lighting. An improved boiler—and the order for another—represented a pivotal investment in the hidden system behind public service.

The operating discipline extended to labor. In 1907, company motormen reportedly spent four weeks each year in the Cedar Rapids shops, divided into two-week periods, while receiving their usual motorman wages. The purpose was to keep them familiar with the equipment and to assist with repairs. Driving and maintenance were not treated as entirely separate worlds. The people responsible for movement were brought into the work of keeping movement possible.

That practice captures the culture Greene’s system required. Reliability could not be created at the moment a car was due to depart. It had to be built beforehand—in machinery, maintenance, familiarity, and reserve capacity.

The opportunity was reach. Early service between Cedar Rapids and Iowa City reportedly took seventy-five minutes and offered thirteen daily round trips. The company used a downtown passenger station near Second Street SE and Fourth Avenue SE and maintained facilities west of the river off Rockford Road. The exact timetable and its variations do not survive in the available account, but the intended business is clear. The railway sold a repeated connection, not a one-time journey.

The risk lay in confusing offered reach with usable reach. A line on a map did not tell a worker whether the first car matched a shift. A station did not reveal whether a fare fit a household budget. A timetable did not prove that the car ran reliably or that a traveler could make the necessary transfer. The infrastructure created possibility; operations determined whether the possibility could be used.

That distinction also governed freight. The same corridor could survive in one market and fail in another. Freight customers and passenger riders demanded different schedules, equipment, revenue structures, and connections. Shared assets created efficiency, but they also forced managers to decide which service justified continued investment.

The city’s local streetcars reached their reported high point during the 1920s. In 1929, Cedar Rapids adopted its quadrant street-direction system, complicating later efforts to align addresses across directories, maps, and timetables. The larger change came in 1937, when buses replaced local streetcars on what one account called similar routes. Track was removed or covered, and overhead lines came down.

“Similar” did not mean identical. A bus could follow the same corridor while changing stops, frequency, capacity, transfer rules, or the hour of the last trip. The record establishes a change of mode, not an equal exchange of access. Nor does it support a Cedar Rapids version of a generalized national streetcar-conspiracy story. The local business decision must stand within the evidence available: rails and wires gave way to tires, while the consequences for particular riders remain unknown.

CRANDIC passenger service continued. In 1939, six secondhand cars built for the Cincinnati and Lake Erie in 1930 entered service in Iowa. Their arrival represented another effort to sustain the passenger business with different equipment. The available operating records do not measure their effect on speed or reliability.

Wartime use gave the passenger operation its reported peak. In 1944, CRANDIC reportedly ran seventeen interurbans in each direction each day, nearly hourly from about five in the morning until midnight. The company reported 573,307 patrons in 1945. An October 1946 timetable shows a dense pattern of stations and frequent service.

The number was large, but it was not a count of unique people, and it did not reveal why they traveled. It could not by itself establish crowding, profitability, or lasting preference. By 1952, reported patronage had fallen to 188,317. A later study attributed the decline to the popularity of automobiles and hard-surfaced roads, but fares, service changes, maintenance, buses, management, land use, and freight priorities remain possible parts of the explanation.

Corporate structure changed as passenger use fell. A current company history states that railroad operations separated from the electric company on January 1, 1950, forming the present corporation. The railway-and-light structure Greene had helped manage in 1907 no longer defined the business. Power and transportation had been separated institutionally just as the passenger and freight futures were diverging.

By May 1953, the divergence had become final. The *Daily Iowan* announced that Train No. 3 would leave Iowa City at 10:25 on the morning of May 30 for the last CRANDIC passenger run. Six sections were expected to carry about three hundred reservation holders. Senator Bourke B. Hickenlooper and railroad officials were named among the participants. Alfred N. Scales was identified as a passenger on the first trip in 1904, and Jack Hageman as its engineer. Souvenir tickets and band music were planned.

The newspaper appeared before the run, so it records expectation rather than every completed detail. A later account identifies car 120 and an 11:15 arrival in Cedar Rapids, while the contemporary article names Train No. 3 and six sections. The exact final movement remains unsettled. The ending date does not. Multiple accounts converge on May 30, 1953, as the close of scheduled passenger service.

Buses were expected to begin carrying CRANDIC passengers the following day. Freight continued.

That survival completes the business story Greene’s boiler had foreshadowed. A mixed system could share capital and infrastructure for decades, then divide when its markets no longer justified the same operation. Passenger service disappeared. The freight railway endured.

Greene’s legacy is not that one boiler built the interurban or saved it. The evidence supports no such claim. His significance lies in what his work reveals: the visible service depended on an invisible operating system, and a schedule was only as credible as the machinery, labor, and management behind it.

The memorable lesson is this: infrastructure creates possibility; operations create reach.

The next builders would apply that discipline to a different flow of goods. Instead of powering a timetable, they would coordinate grain, milling, packaging, and distribution—and turn oats into a national business.
