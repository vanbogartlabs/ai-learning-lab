# Chapter 26: Betting on the Edge of Town

James Faulkes became part of a retail gamble before Cedar Rapids had finished moving toward it.

The gamble was Lindale Plaza. Announced in February 1956, the proposed shopping center would cover seventy-two acres, cost a reported $10 million, and gather Younkers and Sears at an outlying site. Faulkes was among the named participants in its land and development history. The plan assumed that retailers, roads, housing, parking, and customers would converge on a place where no such market yet existed.

Downtown already concentrated commerce. Lindale proposed to manufacture a new kind of commercial center beyond it—one built around the automobile rather than forced to accommodate it.

The risk was not construction alone. Land had to be assembled. Anchor stores had to commit. Capital had to wait through years of preparation. And when the doors finally opened, customers had to make the trip.

Faulkes and the Lindale group chose not to follow a proven market. They chose to give a moving market an address.

## Five Thousand Places to Park

The customer Lindale expected was already changing.

In 1944, the Cedar Rapids and Iowa City Railway ran seventeen interurban trains in each direction every day, almost hourly from early morning until midnight. It carried 573,307 patrons in 1945. By 1952, the reported count had fallen to 188,317. Passenger service ended on May 30, 1953, although freight continued.

No single explanation accounts for each rider who stopped boarding. Roads and automobiles had become more important, but fares, service, buses, management, and postwar conditions also shaped the decline. For Faulkes, the business implication mattered more than a clean theory: customers were no longer moving through the region in the same way.

Lindale’s design answered that change without ambiguity. At opening, the center advertised parking for five thousand cars.

Five thousand spaces were a business model rendered in pavement.

A downtown store benefited from neighboring businesses, offices, sidewalks, and established travel patterns. An outlying center had to create its own concentration. Anchor stores supplied drawing power. Smaller shops made the trip more useful. Roads expanded the distance from which customers might come. Parking solved the first problem every driver brought to the property.

The same abundance of land that made the plan possible made failure visible. An empty acre produced no sale. A half-filled center looked less like a destination than an unfinished promise. Lindale needed enough retailers to make the property coherent and enough customers to prove that distance had become an advantage.

The project was announced in 1956. Construction did not begin until September 1959.

For more than three years, Faulkes’s wager remained attached to land and expectation.

## A Market That Had Not Settled

The easiest way to tell Lindale’s story is to make it a declaration that downtown was finished. The actual business opportunity was subtler.

Younkers had sought a downtown site before joining Sears in the outlying center. Sears, Younkers, and Killian’s each brought its own property and store strategy. Lindale did not need them to agree that the old commercial core had no future. It needed them to believe that another location, organized around road access, could succeed.

That was the insight behind the project. New markets do not always replace old ones in a clean sweep. Sometimes they appear in the widening space between where business is established and where customers may go next.

Cedar Rapids had already seen commercial value move with access. From 1939 through 1954, the U.S. 30/Lincoln Highway alignment bypassed the downtown Automobile Row district. Automobile businesses moved toward 16th Avenue SW, and the last two dealerships left the older district in 1965. The bypass did not dictate each decision, but it demonstrated a principle no retailer could ignore: a route could change the value of an address.

Lindale applied that principle on a larger scale. Instead of one dealership seeking a better road-facing property, Faulkes and the other participants assembled an entire retail destination around the expectation that customers in cars would travel to a coordinated place.

The critical risk was borne before the result became public. Land and development had to move ahead. Anchor participation had to hold. Construction had to turn years of promise into an operating center. On September 15, 1960, Lindale Plaza opened with thirty stores and three anchors: Killian’s, Sears, and Younkers.

The original announcement had envisioned fifty stores. Opening with thirty did not make the project a failure. It revealed the distance between a proposal and a business. Lindale had reached the only test that finally mattered: customers could now decide whether the wager was right.

## Building Ahead of the Map

The year Lindale opened, regional officials completed the first multi-jurisdictional arterial plan for Cedar Rapids and Marion, looking ahead to 1980. Public planners and private developers were responding to the same emerging reality: the inherited map no longer contained the city’s ambitions.

Cedar Rapids covered 29.26 square miles in 1957, forty-five in 1962, and forty-eight in 1967. Its reported population rose from roughly 62,000 in 1940 to 92,000 in 1960 and 110,000 in 1970. Annexation contributed to those city totals, but the commercial field was plainly expanding—more households, more roads, more possible customers.

A 1966 account imagined the Cedar Rapids trade area stretching 140 miles north, sixty east, one hundred south, and sixty west. The claim was promotional rather than a measure of sales, yet it expressed a genuine commercial ambition. Cedar Rapids increasingly saw customers as regional. Lindale gave that ambition physical form.

Faulkes’s legacy lies where the public and entrepreneurial maps met. Government could plan routes and expand boundaries, but it did not choose Lindale’s tenants. Private capital could assemble land and stores, but it could not create regional mobility alone. Faulkes and the other builders made a commitment inside that uncertainty. They placed a destination ahead of a settled transportation map.

Lindale had already been open for a decade when construction began on the southern end of I-380 in 1970. The later interstate did not create Faulkes’s decision. His wager depended on the broader rise of road and automobile access—and on acting before the city’s most visible highway was complete.

That timing is the sharpest measure of the venture. Faulkes did not wait for the future to become obvious.

## The Unequal Price of Access

Lindale’s accomplishment had consequences beyond its property lines. A center designed for thousands of cars served drivers most directly. A person dependent on public transportation encountered a different city. Cedar Rapids took public ownership of its transit system in 1966 and began subsidizing it the following year, but access was never distributed as evenly as pavement on a plan.

Elsewhere, the neighborhood remembered as Little Mexico became part of project R-9. Roughly 150 lots were acquired. Residents including Margaret Pena Meier, Jimmy Vasquez, and Lydia and John Rodriguez were connected to the place. Public resolutions in 1964 and 1966 and individual deed transactions marked the movement of land into redevelopment.

The entire relationship between R-9 and the later I-380 corridor cannot be reduced to one project line. Nor can every transaction be judged as a uniform bargain. But the contrast is essential. Faulkes and his partners assembled land to create a commercial future. Other people experienced the remaking of land through acquisition and relocation.

Access created value. The power to choose the terms of movement was not shared equally.

That truth does not erase Lindale’s entrepreneurial achievement. It gives the achievement weight. Developers selected land. Retailers selected sites. Public bodies chose plans, routes, boundaries, transit ownership, and property programs. Households navigated the results with unequal mobility and power. A road could carry customers toward one business while bypassing another. A parking lot could make shopping easier for drivers while doing nothing for someone without a dependable car.

Faulkes’s business was not separate from those consequences. Location was the product.

## Where Customers Would Go Next

Lindale made northeast Cedar Rapids more commercially legible. It showed retailers, competitors, planners, and developers that a coordinated center with large anchors and vast parking could operate outside the old downtown pattern. The edge of town was no longer merely land awaiting development. It had become a place capable of drawing trips toward itself.

The market did not simply move. Builders gave it somewhere to move.

That is the memorable truth in Faulkes’s story: a market does not shift because an arrow on a map predicts it will. It shifts when entrepreneurs commit real property and capital to where they believe customers will go next.

But roads could create opportunity without guaranteeing survival. By the 1980s, Cedar Rapids had spread plants, stores, homes, and obligations across a much larger geography. Access could bring workers and products to a property. It could not secure orders, settle labor conflict, or keep credit flowing.

Keith Barnes would learn that limit inside one of the city’s largest industrial plants. In 1984, he headed Cedar Rapids Meats as it agreed to buy the Wilson packing plant and operate it as Farmstead. Faulkes had wagered that customers would follow retailers outward. Barnes would wager that workers, production, customers, and credit could be held together inside a troubled plant its corporate owner was ready to leave.
