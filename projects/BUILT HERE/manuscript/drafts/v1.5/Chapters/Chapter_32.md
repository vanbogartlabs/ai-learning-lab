# Chapter 32: Chris Cuddy and the Choice Inside the Mill

Chris Cuddy faced a brutal industrial fact: the market no longer wanted the same things in the same proportions.

In April 2020, Archer Daniels Midland announced that it would temporarily idle ethanol production at its Cedar Rapids dry mill. About ninety workers received furlough notices. At the company’s wet mill, ADM chose another response. It reduced the amount of corn ground for ethanol and shifted emphasis toward industrial alcohol and starch used in containerboard.

Cuddy, the ADM leader quoted in the announcement, became the public voice of the split decision.

One mill would stop. The other would change what it made.

The distinction was more than technical. It revealed the entrepreneurial engine inside Cedar Rapids’ modern processing economy. Continuity did not mean producing the same thing forever. It meant preserving enough usefulness—in facilities, equipment, receiving systems, utilities, transportation, and skill—to answer a changed market with another product.

Yet flexibility had a price. ADM preserved options across two plants. Roughly ninety workers received notices that made the adjustment personal.

Cuddy’s problem was entrepreneurship at corporate scale: when an asset loses its immediate purpose, what can it become before it loses its value?

## Two Mills, Two Fates

ADM had created the distinction it used in 2020.

In 2006, the company announced plans for a dry corn mill beside its existing Cedar Rapids corn-processing plant. The proposed operation had an initial annual capacity of 275 million gallons of ethanol, subject at the time to government approval. It was a huge commitment to one way of turning corn into value.

The dry mill and wet mill were separate facilities, not two names for the same factory. Iowa environmental records identify the wet-mill operation at 1350 Waconia Avenue SW and the ADM-owned Vantage Corn Processors dry mill at 1425 60th Avenue SW. Different processes gave Cuddy different choices when demand shifted.

Dry-mill ethanol production could be idled. The wet mill could reduce its ethanol grind while emphasizing other outputs. The corn remained useful; its most valuable destination had changed.

That choice punctured Cedar Rapids’ comfortable identity as a “food city.” Industrial alcohol was not breakfast. Containerboard starch was not dinner. The same processing base could serve fuel, packaging, papermaking, feed, ingredients, and finished-food markets. Its strength lay not in one product label but in the ability to redirect material through a system built to transform it.

The system could adapt only partly. The company expected the dry-mill stoppage to last about four months, depending on market conditions. The later worker outcomes are not established. In the moment, Cuddy’s announcement showed both sides of flexibility: one asset changed its mix; another went quiet; employees carried the uncertainty between them.

This was not the romance of an inventor in a garage. It was entrepreneurship as allocation—choosing which asset to run, which product to favor, and where the interruption would land.

## A City of Transformations

Cuddy could make that choice because Cedar Rapids held more than a corn mill. It contained a broad processing platform built across generations.

A 2024 Iowa State University report identified activity involving corn, oats, soybeans, yeast and fermentation, and processed foods. It named Quaker Oats, General Mills, ADM, Ingredion, DuPont Industrial Biosciences, and Cargill. The report estimated roughly $2 billion in corn, oat, and soybean inputs, about 4,000 manufacturing workers, and $1.48 billion in nominal sector output for 2022. Those estimates describe scale, not a single integrated company. The plants shared a city, not necessarily one operating plan.

Each chain carried its own version of Cuddy’s problem.

Quaker passed into PepsiCo in 2001. When the 2008 flood closed the Cedar Rapids facility, PepsiCo redirected some production and warned of service disruption. The plant mattered to a network beyond the city, while the larger network gave its owner other places to send work. Later equipment changes included cereal conveyance, storage, and screening systems and an automated oat-hull truck loadout that replaced one rail loadout. The familiar name remained while ownership, equipment, and one logistics function changed.

At 1001 First Street SW, Ingredion inherited another line of continuity after acquiring Penford in 2015. The company identifies a wet corn mill there and publishes truck receiving hours, a local merchandiser, and a maximum inbound-corn moisture level of 16 percent. That specification is a quiet expression of industrial power. Corn does not enter because it reaches the gate. It enters because it meets the buyer’s system.

In 2025, Cedar Rapids described a proposed $50 million Ingredion redevelopment involving three buildings and a starch flash dryer. The project was expected to add four jobs, retain eight, and reach completion in October 2026. The intended products were industrial starches for packaging and papermaking. Like Cuddy’s 2020 shift, the proposal tied the future of a legacy site to a use beyond the ordinary meaning of food. It remained a proposal moving through development and permitting, not a finished result.

Cargill’s Soybean East Plant added another crop. Red Star’s operation added yeast manufacturing; a contractor reported completing a five-story facility with ten fermentation vessels in 2006. General Mills added finished foods and a different form of leverage: more than five hundred workers ratified a first union contract in 2019 and another three-year agreement in 2025.

Together, the operations made Cedar Rapids capable of processing at scale. They did not make consequences equal. An ADM furlough, a General Mills contract, an Ingredion investment proposal, and a Quaker equipment change represented four different bargains for four different groups of people.

Cuddy inherited the platform. He still had to choose what to do with it.

## What “Still” Really Means

Old industrial addresses invite a dangerous illusion: if the plant remains, the business must have endured unchanged.

National Oats shows otherwise. A preserved environmental assessment traced an oat mill at 1515 H Avenue NE to 1904, its sale to National Oats in 1910, a complicated ownership and operating arrangement in 1993, and a Ralston Foods transition in 1994. Then the chain breaks. The site’s later disposition cannot be carried forward by assumption.

Even where a present operation is clear, continuity must be earned decision by decision. PepsiCo’s acquisition of Quaker does not prove that every Cedar Rapids line or job survived. Ingredion’s acquisition of Penford does not create a seamless chain through every Penick & Ford asset and employee. A permit identifies regulated equipment, not actual annual production. A company sign names an owner or brand, not the person who can approve the next investment.

Cuddy’s 2020 announcement mattered because it named an operating choice. It showed two assets being treated differently under the same corporate umbrella. The wet mill’s shift demonstrated flexibility in action. The dry mill’s idling revealed flexibility’s limit.

By 2026, regulators still described the Waconia Avenue operation as ADM’s wet-mill ethanol facility and Vantage as the wholly ADM-owned dry mill. A dry-mill proposal sought authority connected to non-denatured ethanol. The proposal did not prove production or sales. It did show the continuing search for another use.

An idle asset is an option, not an answer. It can be restarted, repurposed, sold, or abandoned. Time makes none of those decisions wisely.

The continuity that matters is the capacity to choose again.

## The Machinery Beyond the Fence

No processor creates that capacity alone.

Grain must arrive. Products and by-products must leave. Water and energy must enter. Wastewater must be treated. Roads, rail, laboratories, maintenance trades, permits, and emergency systems all shape what a plant can profitably make.

Cedar Rapids’ wastewater accounting separates total flow, industrial contributors, domestic flow, and plant return. It does not assign a complete account to each company, but it makes the shared dependence visible. Cuddy’s product flexibility relied partly on public infrastructure extending beyond ADM property.

Freight carried the same interdependence. The former National Oats property had rail spurs. Quaker changed one oat-hull function from rail to automated truck loadout. Ingredion maintains a truck receiving interface. A decision inside a plant can move consequences outside it—onto a siding, a road, a utility system, or a public schedule.

Environmental authority follows the product. Iowa notices distinguish wet and dry mills and describe proposed modifications. Ingredion’s flash-dryer notice addressed a higher short-term nitrogen-oxide limit without increasing permitted particulate matter. The 2026 Vantage notice concerned proposed authority for non-denatured ethanol. A new use must be physically possible, commercially worthwhile, and legally permitted.

That web of dependence made Cuddy’s decision local even though ADM’s reach was global. Cedar Rapids gave the company grain access, industrial sites, infrastructure, and accumulated skill. ADM brought markets and capital beyond the city. Workers supplied the human capability no pipe or permit could replace.

But those parties did not hold equal options.

## The Price of an Option

Approximately ninety furlough notices gave Cuddy’s decision its sharpest edge.

A corporation with two mills could stop one process and redirect another. A worker attached to the idled operation could not shift a household as easily as ADM shifted its product mix. At General Mills, collective bargaining gave more than five hundred workers a form of scale of their own. An individual receiving a furlough notice had neither a diversified plant network nor a bargaining unit’s collective leverage by virtue of that notice alone.

The lesson is uncomfortable and memorable: flexibility belongs to whoever has options.

Cedar Rapids gave processors options through facilities, transport, utilities, and skill. Corporate ownership added options through broader customer and product networks. Workers’ options depended on jobs, contracts, transferable skills, and whether another employer needed them. The city’s options depended on the continuing usefulness of plants it did not ultimately control.

Cuddy’s choice therefore carried two consequences. It showed why Cedar Rapids remained valuable to a global processor: the local system could turn agricultural inputs toward more than one market. It also showed why adaptation cannot be praised without asking who absorbs the change.

His legacy is not one gallon of ethanol or one box of cereal. It is a decision that exposed the real durability of an industrial city. A factory survives not by refusing to change, but by preserving something worth changing into.

The next builder inherited an even less tangible asset. Nathan Boelkins led a Collins Aerospace business based in Cedar Rapids after the city’s signature aerospace company had been folded into a larger corporate chain. Cuddy could point to separate mills and choose what corn should become. Boelkins had to protect accumulated skill inside an enterprise whose ultimate center of power had moved away.
