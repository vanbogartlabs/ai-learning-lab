# Chapter 17: Howard Hall’s One-Piece Bet

Howard Hall had a foundry. What he needed was a problem large enough to justify another company.

He found it wherever roads and quarries pushed into new ground. Rock had to be crushed, screened, and moved, but the machinery that did the work was cumbersome to transport and assemble. The job kept moving. Too often, the plant could not.

Hall and his partner John Jay already controlled the Cedar Rapids concern that became Iowa Steel & Iron Works. Its beams, boilers, pulleys, and architectural ironwork gave them a base in casting and fabrication. In 1923, they incorporated Iowa Manufacturing Company from or around the former Bertschey Engineering Company. Hall then brought in an engineer remembered as Guy Frazee in one account and Guy Franzee in another, along with a team.

Their ambition was practical and audacious: combine crushing, conveying, and screening in a portable outfit.

The precise division of invention and the exact year remain unsettled. One account calls the machine the “One Piece Outfit.” Another presents it as the first truly portable aggregate plant. An equipment-industry profile centers Hall and dates a first portable crusher to 1924. No surviving design record supports a lone-inventor myth or an unqualified claim to being first.

The business decision is clearer—and more important. Hall did not merely add another product to a foundry catalog. He organized a company around making heavy processing equipment mobile. He bet that road builders and quarry operators would pay to bring an integrated plant closer to the work.

Portability turned geography from an obstacle into a market.

## Reorganizing the Customer’s Work

Calling a crushing plant portable created an unforgiving chain of problems.

The crusher, screen, and conveyor had to function together after transport. The equipment’s dimensions and weight had to permit movement. Components had to survive the trip. A customer needed to erect, power, maintain, and repair the outfit at a place chosen for rock or road work rather than factory convenience.

Early measures of throughput, setup time, transport cost, and customer savings have not survived. The product concept still reveals Hall’s entrepreneurial logic. He was not trying to sell more stationary iron by the pound. He was reorganizing the customer’s job.

Roads consumed aggregate in changing places. A plant that could follow construction reduced the tyranny of a fixed processing site. Combining stages made Iowa Manufacturing responsible for more of the production sequence. That enlarged the opportunity—and the consequence of failure. If one integrated outfit broke down, several functions could stop together.

Hall’s Cedar Rapids base made the wager plausible. He and Jay were associated with both Iowa Steel and Iowa Manufacturing. One business possessed foundry and fabrication capability; the other sold finished machinery. Their common control created the possibility of coordination, though the purchase orders and intercompany accounts that would show the exact supply chain have not survived.

Possibility was not enough. The One Piece Outfit still had to earn its way through manufacture, sale, shipment, setup, and service.

That was Hall’s pivotal decision: stop treating crushing, screening, and conveying as separate stationary problems and see the customer’s entire task as the product.

## A Capability That Could Compound

Iowa Manufacturing widened its line. A successor history dates hot-mix asphalt plants to 1929. Later accounts connect the company to wartime road or landing-strip equipment and an Army-Navy E award in 1944. After the war, the company moved further into paving equipment for the highway era.

Each expansion followed Hall’s original logic. Crushing and screening prepared aggregate. Asphalt equipment moved closer to paving. Mobility remained valuable because the work kept changing location.

Hall’s first product did not have to predict every future market. It had to create a capability that would remain useful as markets changed.

Other Cedar Rapids companies were solving adjacent physical problems. Universal Crusher made aggregate and mining equipment and became Universal Engineering during the Second World War. LaPlant-Choate developed earthmoving machinery; a 1940 patent named Ellsworth W. Austin as inventor of an “Earth mover” and the company as assignee. A later patent covered controls for earthmoving equipment and was cited by Caterpillar. Highway Equipment, established in Cedar Rapids in 1939, became associated with spreaders and road-maintenance products. Speeder and Link-Belt Speeder made cranes, shovels, and excavating equipment. A Fruehauf operation made trailer parts in the city.

Together, the firms gave Cedar Rapids an extraordinary range: machines that broke rock, moved earth, prepared paving material, spread loads, lifted weight, and supported transport.

The city’s advantage did not lie in one machine. It lay in the density of people attempting to solve related physical problems.

## Naming the Capital

Howard Hall attached a formidable claim to that concentration: Cedar Rapids was the “road machinery capital of the world.” A later government-sponsored history preserved the attribution and placed the city among three large American road-equipment centers, alongside Peoria and Milwaukee.

No common table of employment, shipments, sales, or market share proves the ranking. The phrase survives more strongly than its denominator.

That does not make it empty. It reveals Hall’s instinct for another kind of entrepreneurship.

Entrepreneurs do not only design products. They define categories in which their companies can matter. “Road machinery capital” gathered foundries, crusher makers, earthmovers, spreaders, and crane builders into one market story. It told recruits that Cedar Rapids valued machinery skill. It told customers the city was not home to one isolated maker but to a concentration of equipment businesses. It made a trip to eastern Iowa legible as a trip into a center of construction technology.

In 1947, a later account reported, 108 foreign engineers came to study engineering and road-building equipment. The surviving record does not preserve their itinerary, but the remembered visit shows the force of the identity Hall helped create. Cedar Rapids wanted to be seen as a place where the machines of modern construction could be understood.

A slogan could not make the companies cooperate. Universal, LaPlant-Choate, Highway Equipment, Speeder, and Iowa Manufacturing had different owners, products, risks, and customers. They may have shared suppliers, employees, and ideas; they also competed for talent and orders.

The phrase traveled farther than the paperwork. That did not make it historical proof. It made it entrepreneurial positioning.

## Public Opportunity, Private Risk

Hall and the other machinery builders sold into markets shaped partly by public decisions. The Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956 authorized an enormous road-building program. Roads required earth to be moved, rock crushed, aggregate screened, asphalt prepared, materials spread, and structures lifted.

For Cedar Rapids manufacturers, the law created possibility. It did not create a purchase order.

Between federal authorization and a machine leaving a local plant stood project selection, specifications, bids, contractors, dealers, financing, production, inspection, delivery, and payment. Yet Hall had positioned Iowa Manufacturing for exactly the kind of work the program multiplied. Portability had been central decades before the interstate era. Asphalt and paving equipment extended the company’s reach toward the road surface itself.

This is how entrepreneurial bets compound. A strong first decision builds a capability that becomes more valuable when the world changes.

The machinery concentration may have thickened the labor market, widened the city’s technical vocabulary, and made Cedar Rapids more visible to buyers. Its existence is clear even where every exchange among firms is not. By 1965, the city held a differentiated equipment economy with Hall’s Iowa Manufacturing near its center.

Founders, however, do not build machines alone. Portable aggregate plants required engineers, draftspeople, patternmakers, foundry workers, machinists, welders, assemblers, inspectors, painters, purchasing agents, carriers, salespeople, and field-service technicians. Customer failures could force redesign as surely as an engineer’s calculation. A patent could name an inventor and assignee; production remained collective.

Hall’s name endured because his decision gives the story a center. He and Jay moved from a foundry concern to a machinery company, recruited technical help, and pursued a portable system that answered a logistical problem. He did not single-handedly create Cedar Rapids’s machinery concentration. He joined existing industrial capability to a new business model.

His achievement was not merely a machine. It was a way of seeing the market: the strongest industrial businesses do not sell one part of a problem; they reorganize the work around the customer.

That is Howard Hall’s lesson: **build a capability around the customer’s whole job, and one product can become a platform for markets not yet visible.**

One Cedar Rapids company would carry that logic into a larger and more dangerous wager. George T. Ronk’s Speeder had already moved twice in search of room. In Cedar Rapids, it would join Link-Belt, build capacity for a national construction market, employ thousands—and learn what happens when the demand a factory was built to capture suddenly disappears.
