# Chapter 21: The Store Behind the Name

Anna Jacobs’s business survived in the least useful way: as a name.

Before the First World War, Jacobs & Kurik Drygoods operated in the Jacobs Building at 1121 Third Street SE. Anna Jacobs and Anna Kurik ran the store. In the same building, Anna Lesinger leased space and worked as an undertaker.

That brief account places Anna Jacobs inside a genuine commercial enterprise and withholds almost everything an entrepreneur would want to know. The firm’s name survives, but not its agreement. The building bears Jacobs’s name, but no recovered deed establishes Anna’s title. The word operated confirms responsibility without revealing capital, inventory, supplier credit, employees, sales, profit, or why the concern ended.

Anna’s problem was not invisibility. It was partial visibility—enough to be remembered, not enough to be measured.

She needs neither promotion into an owner the evidence cannot prove nor demotion into a footnote. She helped operate a dry-goods store. Goods had to be selected, obtained, arranged, priced, sold, accounted for, and replenished. Customers had to choose this counter over another. Those obligations were not ceremonial. They were the business.

Anna’s pivotal decision was to accept the burden of making a useful place work.

## What Operation Means

The Jacobs Building stood in the Bohemian commercial district, with shops below and four flats above. One government preservation report dates its completion to 1912; a later National Register nomination says it was built in 1914, replacing two frame dwellings. The exact year remains unsettled.

Anna’s role is clearer than the date. She and Anna Kurik operated Jacobs & Kurik Drygoods.

Operation was a chain of decisions repeated until the business either sustained itself or did not. The surviving record does not disclose how the two women divided those decisions, whether they contributed equally, or whether their authority changed. The firm’s shared name prevents Anna Kurik from being absorbed into Anna Jacobs’s story. Both women receive the same operating verb.

Nor does operation prove ownership. But incorporation does not open a store each morning. A deed does not select stock, serve a customer, or balance an account. Legal title and daily enterprise are different forms of power, and cities depend on both.

Anna Lesinger’s undertaking work makes the distinction sharper. Her service required a different set of capacities from dry goods: authorization, equipment, transport, suppliers, care, records, and payment. She worked from leased space in the building, although no completed service or ownership instrument survives in the available record.

Three named women thus appear in one small commercial address: two operating a store, one providing a specialized service. Retail, professional work, and residential use occupied the same roof.

Anna Jacobs’s immediate financial outcome is unknown. Her consequence is visible. She helped turn architecture into economic infrastructure. Without operation, the building was only rooms.

## Work Beyond the Sign

Another woman’s record reveals how much a business title can conceal.

Ella McDannel left selected diary entries from 1910 through 1914. They record childcare, clothes work, packing, shampooing Irene Douglas, dining-room work, cleaning, and mending. They also record travel, church, and visits to the YWCA. The entries are a narrow selection, but they preserve work as actions rather than as one occupational label.

McDannel was not an entrepreneur in Anna’s sense. She did not operate the documented store. Her diary matters because it shows labor crossing categories that an employer’s roster might flatten. Care, clothing, personal service, food service, cleaning, and repair could belong to one working life.

A National Park Service history names at least twenty-eight employees connected to the Douglas household and estate. Selected account pages include salary entries for McDannel and four others, though the pay periods, hours, room and board, and deductions are unclear. Brucemore’s current history also identifies Eliza Brown, Anna Dustal, Mary McCord, and Mary O’Connor as servants listed in the Sinclair household in the 1880 census.

These women must not be turned into an imagined staff for Anna’s store. Whether Jacobs & Kurik employed anyone else or relied on family labor is unknown. McDannel’s diary teaches a stricter lesson: unrecorded work should neither be assumed absent nor invented into existence.

A business never operates through the authority printed on its sign alone. Anna’s achievement lies in the documented work of operation, not in a fictional payroll created to make that work seem important.

## Building an Institution

Women also created operating capacity through association.

A preservation report traces a Bohemian Young Women’s Union established in 1891, formal organization of a Cedar Rapids YWCA in 1893, a downtown cafeteria opened in 1898, an $80,000 community campaign in 1911, and programs for women factory workers. The legal continuity of the organizations, the campaign’s receipts, and the individuals behind the work remain unresolved. The operating demands do not.

A cafeteria needed premises, food, equipment, purchasing, workers, prices, customers, hours, cash control, and maintenance. A campaign needed a goal, solicitors, donors, records, and decisions about the use of money. A program for factory workers needed people capable of making the institution function.

Social purpose did not excuse any of those efforts from business discipline.

The institution’s name survived more clearly than the women who performed its daily work. That is the reverse of Anna’s case. Her name remained attached to firm and building while her ledger disappeared. In the YWCA history, the organization retained the milestones while most individual builders receded.

Different records remember different kinds of power. Buildings preserve addresses. Firms preserve trade names. Diaries preserve selected days. Institutions preserve milestones. None preserves the whole enterprise.

## Money in Motion

Other women left clearer traces of individual decisions.

In 1925, Brucemore’s history says Irene Douglas paid Grant Wood $182 to design and install a sleeping porch. The underlying check and contract are not available here, but the account preserves a payer, a supplier, an amount, a year, and a defined project.

The transaction shows authority in motion. Irene commissioned work; money moved toward design and installation; a decision became a physical alteration.

That clarity throws Anna’s missing ledger into relief. Irene’s case retains a price and a named contractor. Jacobs & Kurik retains no known inventory invoice, rent payment, supplier account, or sale. Anna’s role is broader in duration but thinner in documentary detail.

Caroline Sinclair’s record presents a larger act with disputed attribution. Brucemore’s current history credits her with purchasing the property in 1884 and commissioning the mansion. An earlier preservation account credited T. M. Sinclair, who died in 1881. The conflict cannot explain why the attribution changed, but it proves why attribution matters: a building can remain while historical credit moves from one person to another.

Margaret Douglas Hall appears as one of five donors and original board members when the Hall Foundation formed in 1953. Institutional histories also connect her to an inheritance and a later estate transfer in 1981. Donor, board member, inheritor, and testator were distinct forms of authority. The surviving summaries do not disclose her votes or separate every asset decision.

None of these women should lend Anna borrowed certainty. Their records reveal several mechanisms by which a woman could shape Cedar Rapids: operating a store, commissioning paid work, acquiring property, serving on a board, or transferring an estate. Wealth made some acts easier to preserve. It did not make them representative.

Anna’s role remains powerful because it rests on the practical verb operated.

## The Limits of One Name

Women also worked in Cedar Rapids factories. A later newspaper account names Jessie Taken, Marjorie Blevins, and Helen Persson as former Wilson or Farmstead workers. It places Taken’s start at Wilson in 1957 and preserves retrospective accounts of work by all three. Their rates, shifts, seniority, union roles, and departures are not available.

Their names widen the economic world around Anna without turning it into one story. A store operator could influence daily commerce without proven title. A domestic employee could perform varied work without governing the household. A factory worker could earn wages without controlling the line. A donor could transfer wealth without managing the institution that received it.

Employment was not entrepreneurship. Operation was not ownership. Neither was insignificance.

The record also fails to supply a documented principal-period Black woman’s business or institution-building case. The African American Museum of Iowa’s history says unnamed members of Mt. Zion Missionary Baptist Church founded the museum in 1993 and that the church supplied meeting space and money. That later account neither names a woman nor fills the earlier gap. Silence cannot be repaired by assigning a role to someone the evidence does not identify.

Anna Jacobs cannot stand for every woman, immigrant, retailer, or entrepreneur whose papers disappeared. She is one documented builder whose story opens the door without pretending the room is complete.

## What Anna Jacobs Built

No surviving balance sheet proves that Anna Jacobs made a profit. No partnership agreement establishes her share. No deed proves that she owned the building carrying her name. No closing file explains how the business ended.

What remains is enough.

Anna Jacobs operated Jacobs & Kurik Drygoods with Anna Kurik. Their concern occupied a mixed-use commercial building in a neighborhood whose stores and institutions carried commerce beyond downtown factories and monuments. Anna Lesinger provided undertaking services from leased space under the same roof. The building gathered retail, professional service, and residences into one working place.

Anna helped make that place useful.

Her work changed Cedar Rapids at the scale on which cities are actually lived: a counter where goods could be obtained, an address where services could be found, rooms made productive by repeated human decisions. Cedar Rapids, in turn, gave her a neighborhood commercial district in which to operate.

Her name survived on a firm and a building. The papers that would measure her authority did not. That imbalance is not a reason to look away. It is a reason to stop confusing fame with proof, property with work, or silence with absence.

A city is built not only by the people who own its institutions, but by the entrepreneurs who make useful institutions function.

The next builder would work at a scale Anna never claimed. Grant Wood would take a public commission from design to full-size study and into an international fabrication chain. Anna’s store showed how a building acquires life through operation. Wood’s window would show how an idea becomes buildable only after it passes through other people’s hands.
