# Chapter 20: The Counterweight

Milo Barta faced a business problem no worker could solve alone.

Management could divide production into tasks, change staffing, introduce machinery, grant benefits, and decide who entered the plant. One employee could object, leave, or endure. None of those choices created lasting power over the terms of work.

On August 23, 1933, Barta and Lewis Clark formed Federal Labor Union 18530 at Wilson’s Cedar Rapids packinghouse. A union number did not bring recognition. Recognition did not bring a contract. A contract did not enforce itself. The two men were building an institution inside an enterprise that already possessed buildings, capital, managers, lawyers, and control of the paycheck.

Their product was leverage. Their capital was participation. Their immediate risk belonged to people who could lose their income.

Barta’s pivotal decision was not to stage one protest. It was to create something that might survive the grievance that summoned it.

## The Terms Already Written

The factory bargain had been assigning risk to particular bodies for decades.

John Kelley died in 1887 in an event associated with a cauldron. Frank M. Stanz died after an 1891 event involving a shaft while oiling it. Frank Kratochvil died in 1892 in a boiling-vat event. Their complete circumstances, family losses, compensation, and any corrective actions are not preserved in the available account. Their names establish what abstraction can hide: industrial production placed men close enough to heat and machinery for failure to become final.

By 1915, a published description presented slaughter as a moving sequence through nearly fifty men, each performing one part of a process said to take twenty minutes. The division made volume possible. It also divided knowledge and authority. The product moved; each worker remained at his assigned point.

Mechanization changed that arrangement without ending the bargain. A later history said new machinery and electrical systems reduced hand labor and increased speed. A machine could eliminate one manual task while binding the remaining workers more tightly to pace. Management decided how capital would reorganize labor.

Workers had contested that authority before Barta. A reported walkout in January 1903 followed a change in hog slaughtering that reduced staffing. An organizing effort in 1921 involved the American Meat Cutters and Butcher Workmen and an employer lockout. Both disputes placed access to work at the center of the conflict. The company could assign labor—and withhold it.

Management also built institutions of its own. The Sinclair history reported a pension fund in 1917, a welfare service and dispensary in 1919, and a cafeteria. The dispensary was said to treat as many as one thousand minor injuries in a month. A sprinkler system installed in 1907 was credited with reducing production interruption and insurance expense.

Those measures could provide real value while leaving management in control of their terms. A dispensary treated injury; it did not give workers authority to remove a hazard. A pension promised future value without revealing who qualified or what could be lost. A sprinkler protected people, property, production, and the insurer’s interest at once.

This was the system Barta inherited: divided work, recurring danger, changing machinery, employer benefits, and earlier resistance that had not created durable local standing.

He chose to build the other institution.

## From Protest to Structure

Federal Labor Union 18530 affiliated with the Amalgamated in November 1933. In 1935, the organization became independent as the Midwest Union of All Packinghouse Workers. In 1939, it received a CIO charter as Packinghouse Workers Local Industrial Union 3. An action in 1941 was associated with recognition. A master agreement followed in 1943.

Compressed into dates, the sequence looks administrative. In business terms, each step answered a different survival question.

Could workers establish a local organization? Could it gain the resources of a larger body? Could it leave that body and endure? Could it secure a place in a new industrial federation? Could it compel the employer to deal with it? Could it turn recognition into written obligations?

Barta appears at the first hinge, beside Clark. Ted Lubacek appears later as an organizer and translation lead. No one man can be credited with the whole sequence. That limitation strengthens rather than weakens Barta’s story. The organization he helped create had to outgrow him. If its power remained personal, it would fail the people meant to inherit it.

The changes in name and affiliation were not cosmetic. Independence offered one kind of control; federation offered reach and resources. Recognition opened bargaining. A master agreement could carry terms beyond the memory of a meeting or the force of one picket line.

The institution still had to persuade on two fronts. Workers had to believe collective action was worth its immediate price. Management had to confront enough organized power to negotiate. A charter alone could compel neither side.

Barta’s achievement was to begin converting vulnerability into structure. One worker could face company authority with something behind him.

## The Cost of Leverage

A union’s power came from withholding the labor its members needed to sell.

Cedar Rapids packinghouse workers participated in the national strike of 1948. The available history reports a local count and a national duration, but not the complete local vote, demands, relief accounts, settlement, or household budgets. The central exchange needs no embellishment. A halted plant threatened the company’s production. A missing paycheck threatened a family’s ability to continue.

Collective leverage carried private costs.

By 1959, the contest over authority had moved beyond wages and work rules to the meaning of being seen.

On November 20, H. S. Amalong sent Wilson executives in Chicago photographs of automobiles participating in a United Packinghouse Workers of America parade. He wrote that a similar set had gone to company attorney V. C. Shuttleworth. Company copies of strike photographs carried worker names and, in some cases, the departments in which they worked.

The act required no dramatic confrontation. A public image became an employment record. A person outside the plant was identified, connected to a department, and returned to management’s attention.

The factory gate no longer marked the boundary of company knowledge. A camera could extend it.

Workers had built enough organization to parade, picket, and bargain. Management used its own organization to observe, classify, and circulate information. Both sides turned people and events into records that could be acted upon.

The conflict did not end on one clean date. One account describes a June 1959 walkout, 109 days, and an agreement in February 1960. Another describes Wilson lockouts beginning in June, picketing into the following March, and arbitration agreements in March. Lockout, walkout, settlement, return, and arbitration were different stages. The disputed chronology reveals the nature of the struggle: the bargain changed in pieces because authority itself had several layers.

## A Procedure Strong Enough to Say No

The union did not abolish company power. It built procedures that could sometimes check it.

A scholarly account of the 1959–1960 conflict describes a case in which film was used against a worker. An arbitration board reversed the result, and the employee returned. His name, the personnel action, and the award are not available for this account, but the documented sequence is enough: management acted; the action was challenged; an outside procedure reversed it; the worker came back.

That was the long consequence of Barta’s founding choice.

The surviving trace of John Kelley ends with a cauldron and death. By 1959, at least one worker facing company action could reach an arbitration board and return to the job. The cases are not equivalent, and arbitration did not end physical danger. Together they show how the architecture of contest had changed.

Barta’s organization had grown into a larger system of charters, affiliations, agreements, grievances, lawyers, photographs, and arbitration. Rules could now stand between an individual employee and the company.

Power changes when protest becomes an institution, and an institution becomes a procedure that can alter an outcome.

That lesson was not a promise of victory. A worker might win reinstatement only after suffering the loss that made reinstatement necessary. A grievance could fail. Coverage could be unequal. The union could contest discipline while leaving layoffs, hierarchy, and hazards intact. Counterpower did not abolish risk. It changed where some risks could be challenged.

## What Barta Built

Milo Barta did not reorganize every Cedar Rapids workplace. The packinghouse local cannot stand for women filling packages at Quaker Oats, railroad employees working around roundhouses and car shops, or workers in starch and machinery plants. Their tasks, rules, hazards, and bargaining systems were different.

Industrial costs also crossed the factory gate. The municipal sewage plant built in the 1930s was reported to receive six million gallons of domestic sewage and 1.5 million gallons of packinghouse waste each day. Private production had created a flow large enough to become part of public infrastructure.

Barta’s accomplishment was narrower and more durable. He helped workers at one large plant create a vehicle for contesting some of the authority that came with industrial scale.

Cedar Rapids made the venture possible in the same way it made it necessary. A large plant concentrated management power, specialized tasks, and worker vulnerability. It also concentrated enough workers around one employer for organization to become imaginable.

In return, the institution Barta helped begin changed the city’s industrial culture. A management decision could provoke not only individual reaction but organized response. A public parade could become evidence in a company file. An arbitration board could return a worker to the payroll.

Barta and Clark formed Federal Labor Union 18530. Others affiliated it, made it independent, entered the CIO, won recognition, negotiated agreements, struck, picketed, and invoked arbitration. The founder’s best measure was that the organization no longer needed to remain his.

He had built a counterweight.

The next entrepreneur worked on a smaller commercial stage, but her problem was also one of authority. Anna Jacobs’s name survived on a dry-goods concern and a building while the documents that would measure her ownership and power disappeared. Barta’s institution became visible by enduring. Anna’s enterprise would have to be recovered from the single decisive fact that she helped operate it.
