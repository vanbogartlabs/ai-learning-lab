# Chapter 29: Thirty-One Feet

Gail Naughton had time to move two truckloads.

That was the margin between warning and inundation at the National Czech & Slovak Museum & Library in June 2008. Staff carried out two truckloads of objects before the Cedar River entered the building. On June 17, four days after the crest, Naughton still had not been inside. She knew what had escaped. She did not yet know what the water had taken.

Her problem was immediate: preserve an institution whose building, collections, public mission, and surrounding district had all been placed at risk at once.

The flood had expanded the same problem across Cedar Rapids. Homes, businesses, factories, cultural institutions, municipal departments, and transportation facilities stood in the water’s path. No builder could wait for the full loss to be counted before deciding what to save, where to operate, and whether returning to the same place made sense.

Naughton’s first decision was measured in truckloads. The consequence would be measured in years.

## The Number That Broke the Plan

At 10:15 on the morning of June 13, the U.S. Geological Survey recorded a river stage of 31.12 feet at its Cedar Rapids gauge. Thirty-one feet was not the depth of water across the city. It was a reading at one defined point. But it marked the scale of the force bearing down on Naughton’s museum.

The river stood 19.12 feet above the National Weather Service flood stage and 11.12 feet above the previous recorded maximum from 1929. USGS called it the largest known flood at the gauge since operations began in 1903.

Even the flow could not be measured directly at the city gauge. Hazards and obstructions forced USGS to estimate 140,000 cubic feet per second using a measurement taken 9.9 miles downstream. The river had been above flood stage since June 8 and would remain there through June 21.

The crest was not one violent instant. It was the peak of a long emergency.

Nor had the flood come from a single storm. Above-normal fall rain, heavy winter snow, and saturated spring soil prepared the watershed. More rain fell in early June and again as the river was already carrying record flow. The water that entered Naughton’s museum had been accumulating as risk for months before it became visible at the door.

## The Institution Inside the Emergency

Cedar Rapids had spent generations placing homes, workplaces, public offices, transportation, and culture along and across the river. The flood found those investments together.

The city estimated that water affected more than ten square miles—14 percent of Cedar Rapids—and 1,126 blocks. It placed 18,623 people inside the affected area and estimated that 10,000 residents were displaced. Six major public operations were damaged and forced out: City Hall, Central Fire Station, Animal Control, Public Works, the Ground Transportation Center, and the main library. Firefighters conducted 423 boat rescues.

Government was not managing the emergency from a safe distance. Its own buildings and systems were in the water.

That mattered to Naughton. A museum is a cultural institution, but it is also an operating enterprise: employer, customer, property owner, public destination, steward of inventory, buyer of specialized services, and raiser of capital. Its survival depended on roads, utilities, public authority, financing, conservation expertise, and the future of Czech Village. Saving the collection could not be separated neatly from rebuilding the city around it.

Small businesses faced the same continuity problem with fewer resources. On July 16, the City Council allocated $3 million to the Chamber for small-business recovery grants. The action recognized a central fact of the flood: business survival was itself part of recovery infrastructure. A storefront that waited for every permanent decision might not live long enough to see one.

Naughton could not yet enter the museum when the city began making choices around it.

## Four Days After the Crest

On June 17, the same day Naughton described the two truckloads and the unopened building, the City Council adopted six recovery goals and expanded the work of the Sasaki/JLG planning team.

The goals included flood protection, affordable workforce neighborhoods, business vitality, preservation of arts and cultural assets, historic heritage, and retention of a next generation of workers. Together they exposed the conflict ahead. Protection, housing, commerce, culture, and history could reinforce one another. They could also compete for land, money, and speed.

Naughton’s museum embodied every tension. It was a building, a collection, an employer, a destination, and part of a damaged district. Repairing the structure would not conserve the objects. Conserving the objects would not restore programs. Restoring programs would not answer whether the permanent building should remain where it had stood.

A month after the flood, the museum had been cleaned, disinfected, and stripped of wallboard and insulation. Naughton called it an empty shell. “It’s like starting over.”

But she was already discussing exhibits, store operations, and programs away from the permanent building.

That was her pivotal entrepreneurial decision: the loss of usable walls would not become the disappearance of the institution.

The choice did not produce instant recovery. It produced continuity under constraint. The two truckloads became a first inventory of survival. Temporary operations offered a way to keep serving the public while collection treatment, capital planning, and permanent construction moved on slower clocks.

Naughton had separated the organization from the building before she knew what the final building would become.

## Choosing Among Three Futures

Cedar Rapids faced the same fundamental decision. It could not simply put everything back where it had been and call that recovery.

Through the summer and fall, the River Corridor process tested three materially different approaches. One emphasized a floodwall. Another combined a levee with a parkway. A third proposed a naturalized floodplain. Each offered protection by accepting different consequences.

A wall could preserve more uses behind a hard boundary. A levee and parkway could join protection to transportation and open space. A naturalized floodplain could give more territory back to the river, but only by confronting what could no longer remain there.

The City Council selected a preferred strategy on October 1 and approved a combined framework on November 12. The choice turned engineering into a chain of entrepreneurial and civic questions. Which land would remain productive? Which neighborhoods would be protected? Where would displaced housing go? Which routes would reconnect the city? Which cultural assets warranted protection or relocation? What should return, and what needed a new form?

In 2009, those questions reached individual properties. A residential acquisition plan divided land among greenway, construction or study, and neighborhood-revitalization categories. Public facilities faced alternatives that included return as before, return with upgrades, relocation, co-location, new construction, and reuse of existing buildings.

Every option exchanged one risk for another. Repair could preserve continuity while retaining exposure. Relocation could reduce flood risk while changing access. New construction required time and capital. Reuse preserved an asset but imposed the costs of adaptation.

Naughton’s museum would eventually choose one of the boldest combinations: continue operating elsewhere, preserve the historic structure, move it, elevate it, and join it to an expanded permanent facility.

But in 2008, all of that depended on the first decision. The museum had to be understood not as one damaged building, but as a mission, collection, staff, programs, public identity, and physical assets capable of moving on different timelines.

## What Had to Continue

The flood’s famous number was fixed at 31.12 feet. Naughton’s task resisted such clean measurement.

Two truckloads were out. Other materials remained. The interior became an empty shell. Programs needed temporary form. The district faced its own uncertainty. Public plans offered alternatives without guaranteeing money, consensus, or speed.

That is what made her response entrepreneurial. Naughton could not wait until every damaged object, grant, engineering question, and public decision had been resolved. She had to act while the size of the loss was still unknown.

Her emotional journey is visible without imagined fear or private reflection. It begins in uncertainty: what survived beyond the two truckloads? It passes through loss: a gutted building. It reaches resolve through action: the museum would continue to exhibit, sell, teach, and plan even without a permanent home.

Rebuilding began when Gail Naughton separated what had to continue from the place where it used to happen.

The next phase of her story would turn that insight into an operating strategy. Records would travel to conservation labs. Books would be frozen. Exhibits and offices would move to temporary addresses. Capital would have to be assembled from many sources. The historic structure would itself become an object to save.

Thirty-one feet measured the river at one gauge. Naughton’s answer would be measured in boxes, labor hours, grants, temporary quarters, a building moved across the ground, and a public institution returned to life.
