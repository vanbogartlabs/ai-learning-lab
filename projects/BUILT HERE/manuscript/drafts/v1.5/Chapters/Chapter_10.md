# Chapter 10: Caroline’s Commission

In 1884, Caroline Campbell Soutter Sinclair made a decision that later history would give to her dead husband.

Thomas McElderry Sinclair died in 1881. The mansion later called Brucemore was begun three years afterward, when Caroline purchased the property and commissioned the house then known as Fairhome. Her immediate problem was not how to preserve her husband’s industrial system. It was how to exercise authority after his death—over property, capital, design, and the future of her household.

Yet a National Register nomination prepared in 1975 said the house had been built for him and even placed his death in 1908. The error did more than scramble a chronology. It gave a dead man credit for a living woman’s decision.

The exact route of every dollar is not recoverable. No available ledger ties a particular company share, dividend, inheritance, investment, or insurance payment to a construction bill. The commission itself is clear. Caroline directed resources associated with an industrial family into a major property under her name.

Construction extended from 1884 through 1886. Maximillian Allardt received the initial design attribution; Henry Josselyn and Eugene Taylor are credited with completing it. The family reportedly moved in during November 1886. Brucemore gives the project a cost of $55,000, though the surviving account does not divide that figure among land, construction, furnishings, changes, and professional fees.

Caroline’s pivotal decision created an asset that would long outlive its first household. But in 1884, its future as a cultural institution did not exist. Fairhome was a private estate, and the scale of the commission was an assertion of household authority. The industrial fortune had crossed the factory gate and become architecture.

That conversion mattered because wealth changes power when it changes form. Inside a company, it can buy machinery, inventory, land, and labor. Inside a household, it can buy property, employ workers, sponsor institutions, commission artists, or pass to heirs. Caroline’s house became the vessel through which several generations would make those choices.

Her giving extended the Sinclair name beyond the estate. Institutional histories connect the family to Sinclair Memorial Church, a chapel at Coe College, the YMCA, First Presbyterian Church, and foreign missions. Coe’s accounts place construction of Sinclair Memorial Chapel in 1910 and its dedication in 1911, and they report a $1,100 organ gift from Caroline in 1916. The memorial church and the college chapel were separate institutions, a distinction easily obscured by the repeated name.

The point is not that every gift can be measured by its later public effect. It cannot. The point is that Caroline repeatedly converted private resources into durable property and institutions. She chose forms that could carry a name, serve a purpose, and survive the donor.

Her most consequential transaction after building Fairhome came in 1906. Caroline and George and Irene Douglas exchanged residences. The Douglases transferred their Second Avenue mansion and paid an additional reported $28,500 for Fairhome’s house, barn, and ten acres. The agreement came first; renovation and the Douglas family’s move followed in 1907.

The exchange reveals the estate as an operating asset rather than a frozen monument. Caroline had commissioned it, occupied it, and then traded it. Industrial-family property moved from the Sinclair household to the Douglas household, carrying its scale and possibilities into a new fortune.

The Douglases changed the estate. Landscape architect O. C. Simonds was hired in 1907, and Irene Douglas remained in contact with him into the 1920s. The landscape expanded the household’s ambitions, but its operation depended on paid work.

Ella McDannel provides the clearest view of that work. She entered the Douglas household in 1909 and remained for roughly two decades; surviving accounts differ over whether her service ended in 1929 or 1930. Brucemore remembered her as a nanny. Selected diary entries show a role less easily contained. McDannel recorded childcare, putting away clothes, shampooing Irene Douglas’s hair, packing, cleaning downstairs, and mending. She also recorded walking, church, travel, and work shared with others.

Nothing in those selected pages permits an account of her feelings, usual hours, freedom, or treatment. Their force lies in their specificity. Fairhome’s grandeur depended on named people performing repeated tasks. A title such as nanny could cover childcare one moment and clothing, personal attendance, cleaning, or packing the next.

McDannel was not alone. A National Park Service roster identifies at least twenty-eight Douglas household workers. Among the long-serving employees were Albert Batten, listed from 1915 to 1937; Archie White, associated with the estate during part of 1921 and again from 1922 to 1937; and Alfred and Ivy Batten, both listed from 1926 to 1937.

A surviving salary transcription lists Bracha at $125, Guterich at $100, McDannel and Seay at $75 each, and A. E. Batten at $150. The pay period and compensation basis are not clear, and the reproduced page carries a 1924 date beside a caption referring to 1926. The figures cannot support a judgment about fairness. They establish that the estate’s beauty rested on a payroll.

Irene Douglas also used the estate as a place of artistic patronage. In 1925, she paid Grant Wood $182 to design and install a sleeping porch. The commission should not be inflated into a claim that she discovered Wood or caused his career. Its significance is more grounded and more useful: private taste became paid artistic work, and the estate became a site where commerce and culture met.

The limits of that conversion became stark after the Douglas Starch Works exploded on May 22, 1919. Surviving accounts disagree over whether forty-two, forty-three, or forty-four people died. No available record connects the family’s later patronage or philanthropy to relief, compensation, or a household response to the catastrophe. Later giving cannot be called restitution. Nor can silence prove that the family did nothing.

The disaster leaves Caroline’s original question in a harder form. What obligations follow a fortune out of the enterprise that produced it? A mansion can display wealth. A commission can distribute it. Neither answers by itself what is owed to the people who labored, suffered, or remained outside the gates.

After Irene Douglas died in 1937, her daughter Margaret inherited Brucemore and moved into the mansion with her husband, Howard Hall. In 1953, family resources acquired another durable form: the Hall Foundation.

The foundation’s history identifies Howard Hall, Margaret Douglas Hall, Margaret Lamey Hall, Irene Hall Perrine, and Beahl T. Perrine as original participants. The same history calls Howard a founder and principal donor while saying Margaret Lamey Hall initially funded the organization. Their precise roles in formation, governance, and contribution cannot be fully separated from the surviving account. What the incorporation accomplished is clear. Family allocation no longer depended on one household decision or one lifetime.

Later estate transfers strengthened that continuity. Hall-Perrine reports a transfer from Margaret Lamey Hall’s estate in 1958, followed by separate transfers associated with Howard Hall in 1971 and Margaret Douglas Hall in 1981. A foundation could continue choosing recipients after the original donors were gone. That durability was its power—and the source of its obligation to choose well.

Margaret Douglas Hall’s death in 1981 produced a second, distinct transfer. Brucemore passed to the National Trust for Historic Preservation and toward use as a cultural center or house museum. A portion of her estate also went to the foundation. The property transfer and the foundation transfer were not one ceremonial gift. They sent different assets into different institutions.

Two employees, Nial Dicken and Carolyn Shimek, reportedly received life tenancy in estate buildings. The legal terms are not available, so the provision cannot be made to prove ownership, a pension, or universal treatment of workers. It nevertheless joined the estate’s preservation to the continued residence of named people who had worked there.

Caroline could not have foreseen this institutional future. No claim about her intent is necessary. Her legacy lies in the chain of consequences set in motion by a documented act: she purchased property, commissioned Fairhome, and established the asset through which later families employed workers, hired artists, formed a foundation, and transferred a private estate into preservation.

The memorable truth is also the correction history required: Caroline built the house that made later choices possible.

But a mansion tells the story from the top of the ledger. Anna Vild’s much thinner record turns the page toward the other end of property—where acquiring one lot still left a household facing the price of the house it was required to build.
