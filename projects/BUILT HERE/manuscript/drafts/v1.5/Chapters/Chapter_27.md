# Chapter 27: The Plant Keith Barnes Tried to Keep Open

Keith Barnes stepped toward a plant its owner was trying to leave.

On May 9, 1984, Cedar Rapids Meats, headed by Barnes, agreed to buy Wilson Foods’ hog-slaughtering and processing plant for approximately $16 million. Wilson was in Chapter 11. Its conflict with union workers had already produced company-wide wage reductions and authorization for a strike at six plants. Yet Barnes was not buying an empty building. The agreement contemplated continued manufacturing and brokerage arrangements with Wilson and assumption of the contract with UFCW Local P-3.

Barnes expected to operate the Cedar Rapids plant at the same level as before.

That expectation was both the promise and the peril of the deal. Barnes was taking on a living industrial system: workers, livestock, customers, production lines, refrigeration, credit, pensions, and a large property whose age had been tempered by substantial modernization. Its value depended on keeping those parts connected. So did its danger.

Barnes chose continuity. The plant would not die with the seller’s bankruptcy. Cedar Rapids Meats would buy it, operate it as Farmstead, and try to turn an inherited industrial past into an independent future.

He was buying time. He could not yet know how many relationships would have to hold before time became survival.

## The Business Attached to the Building

The purchase was completed on July 2, 1984. Barnes gained an existing route into production rather than building one from bare ground: a recognized site, equipment, an experienced workforce, union arrangements, and customer relationships.

Every advantage arrived with an obligation.

A workforce brought knowledge and payroll. Capacity brought fixed costs. Pension commitments tied the new company to work performed before it existed. Manufacturing and brokerage arrangements could preserve business while leaving Farmstead dependent on organizations Barnes did not control.

Wilson had stopped beef and lamb slaughter at the plant in 1979. It entered Chapter 11 on April 22, 1983, before a union strike scheduled for June 4. Garry Zimmerman, identified at the time as a Cedar Rapids union local president, described exceptionally strong local support for the strike. Barnes therefore inherited more than machinery and real estate. He inherited a business in which wages, trust, production, and solvency had already collided.

His challenge was to separate a functioning plant from the failure around it without cutting the ties that made it function.

Across Cedar Rapids, another industrial property showed how quickly scale could become a burden. Link-Belt’s employment had fallen to 1,550 in July 1980, 700 in April 1982, and 380 by late 1983, with fewer than one hundred workers left in production. Reduced construction activity and orders, high interest rates, competition, and pressure for profit were all associated with the contraction. In 1985, FMC expected to close its Cedar Rapids crane and excavator plant.

A large factory could lose work far faster than its buildings could find another purpose.

Barnes was attempting the opposite maneuver at Farmstead. Instead of allowing a property to divide into equipment, claims, and land, he tried to keep it whole as a producing business.

For roughly six years, he did.

That fact matters. The wager did not collapse on contact. Farmstead continued operating until the relationships Barnes needed began to break together.

## When Credit Reached the Production Line

By early 1990, Farmstead’s crisis could no longer be solved on the factory floor.

On March 3, the Cedar Rapids operation laid off another 246 workers after 170 earlier layoffs. The company sought $4.6 million in concessions. Roughly 1,600 Cedar Rapids jobs were described as being at risk. Farmstead had also failed to reach a credit agreement after pork shipments to Doskocil and Wilson entities stopped.

The numbers made Barnes’s original bet visible in reverse.

A packing plant needed product moving through it. Product depended on customers and commercial agreements. Payroll and operations depended on cash. Cash depended on sales and credit. Labor concessions might reduce costs, but they could not restart stopped shipments or compel a lender to agree.

An analysis placed the average Cedar Rapids wage at about $8.90, above an estimate for a nearby IBP plant, and noted differences in technology as well as months of weak pork-packing conditions. None of that made wages the single cause of failure. Farmstead needed a labor answer, a customer answer, a financing answer, and an operating answer at the same time.

Credit had become production. Without it, physical capacity was only stranded machinery.

On March 8, Barnes announced closure and bankruptcy. Six days later, Cedar Rapids Meats filed for Chapter 11.

The distance between his 1984 declaration and the 1990 filing gives the story its emotional force. Barnes had entered publicly expecting to maintain operations. He ended amid layoffs, demands for concessions, interrupted shipments, failed credit negotiations, closure, and bankruptcy under the company he led.

He had preserved production for years. He had not made the plant independent of the network that could bring it down.

## When a Company Becomes Claims

Farmstead did not end in one motion.

Production stopped on one clock. The legal business continued on another. Cedar Rapids Meats remained in Chapter 11 until February 4, 1991, when the case converted to Chapter 7. A later court opinion recorded a $4.15 million union wage-priority claim and an allowed unsecured claim of at least $20 million, along with city water and sewer claims, Linn County tax liens, and postpetition taxes.

The plant that had once connected workers, the union, the city, the county, customers, and creditors through production now connected them through competing legal rights.

An equipment auction in 1991 pulled another thread loose. Machinery could leave while obligations remained. Once specialized equipment dispersed, restarting the operating system became harder even if the building still stood.

This is what the word closed often conceals. A factory can close as a workplace, continue as a debtor, sell its machinery, leave claims unpaid, and endure as real estate. Barnes’s company came apart under the different rules governing labor, credit, taxes, utilities, assets, and land.

The deepest consequences belonged to the workers, even though their individual paths are not visible here. The layoffs and union claims cannot tell us who found another job, retired, left Cedar Rapids, or accepted lower wages. They do show what Barnes had placed at stake. His decision to continue the plant kept hundreds of livelihoods and obligations tied to Farmstead’s survival. When the business failed, those ties did not vanish with the last production run.

## The Property Outlives the Enterprise

The former plant did not remain simply abandoned. Central States Warehousing acquired the property, and tenants used it after 1993. Storage and small businesses still occupied parts of the site when a city deal was announced in 2006.

The industrial past kept making demands. Cedar Rapids acquired the property in a transaction announced in 2006 and completed by 2007. Environmental assessment, hazardous-material removal, and cleanup followed. Part of the land became a levee and detention basin.

A private packing business had once held the property together. Bankruptcy separated its functions. Public custody gave part of the land a new purpose: flood control.

That later use did not replace the jobs lost in 1990 or settle the claims left behind. It showed that Barnes’s wager outlived him in physical form. Cedar Rapids had supplied the plant, workforce, infrastructure, and industrial memory that made his attempt possible. His company kept the operation alive after Wilson’s reorganization. When Farmstead failed, the city inherited part of the aftermath.

Entrepreneurs shape cities not only through success. Sometimes they leave obligations, land, and unfinished decisions that the city must carry forward.

## The Real Acquisition

Keith Barnes should not be remembered only as the executive present at Farmstead’s end. His defining decision came at the beginning: he bought a troubled but functioning plant and committed to keeping it alive.

The attempt mattered. Cedar Rapids Meats preserved production for years after Wilson entered bankruptcy. But Barnes had purchased more than a property. He had taken responsibility for a dense web of workers, agreements, customers, creditors, pensions, utilities, equipment, and fixed costs. When several strands weakened at once, the size of the plant offered no protection.

Capacity without credit could not move product. A demand for concessions could not substitute for a complete business solution. Buildings and machinery without a functioning network became assets, claims, and later uses.

Barnes’s lesson is as stark as the empty line: buying a plant is one transaction; keeping every relationship that makes it a business is the real acquisition.

Cedar Rapids’ entrepreneurial life, however, had never existed only behind the gates of its largest employers. While Barnes struggled to preserve a massive inherited system, Cecil Reed had built at a different scale and against a different barrier. Reed and his wife, Evelyn, opened Motel Sepia in a travel economy where Black customers could not trust every door to open. Barnes’s problem was how to hold an industrial network together. Reed’s began with a more elemental question: who gets in?
