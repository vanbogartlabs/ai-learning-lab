# Chapter 30: Starting Over Without Starting from Nothing

When Gail Naughton finally knew the condition of the National Czech & Slovak Museum & Library, uncertainty had hardened into damage.

The building had been cleaned, disinfected, and stripped of wallboard and insulation. She called it an empty shell.

“It’s like starting over.”

The sentence named the problem but not the resources still available to solve it. Two truckloads of objects had escaped before the flood. Collections remained that could be cleaned, frozen, repaired, or reassembled. The museum still had staff, programs, a recognized name, a public mission, and people willing to help. What it did not have was a functioning permanent home.

Naughton’s task was to keep the institution alive while its collections, staff work, public programs, capital, and building followed separate paths. Some objects would take years to treat. Exhibits and offices would move. The historic structure itself would eventually be lifted and carried to safer ground.

Starting over did not mean starting from nothing. It meant discovering what could still move—and building around it.

## The Museum Without the Museum

Naughton had already made the decision that governed everything after the flood: closure of the main building would not become closure of the organization.

A month after the water receded, she was planning exhibits, store operations, and programs elsewhere. From October 2008 through December 2009, a Lindale Mall storefront housed a temporary exhibit. Offices later operated from the Kosek Building, from April 2010 through May 2012.

A mall storefront was not a museum. An office was not a conservation lab. But each allowed part of the enterprise to function. Naughton separated permanent occupancy from public service and immediate continuity from long-term construction.

The African American Museum of Iowa chose a faster route back into its own building. About five and a half feet of water damaged its entire first level and many collection items. Yet it held a grand reopening on January 16, 2009, while collection recovery continued.

The doors reopened before the work ended.

That was not denial. It was an operating choice. Waiting until every object and room reached a final condition could have kept the public away indefinitely. The African American museum restored safe public functions and continued slower conservation behind them.

Naughton’s institution made the same separation across a longer interval. Temporary exhibits preserved public visibility. Offices sustained staff work. Outside laboratories handled materials that could not simply be dried and returned to shelves. The permanent building project could then proceed without forcing the institution’s entire mission to wait on engineering, fundraising, and concrete.

A reopening date measures access, not completion. Naughton understood that survival required many clocks.

## Saving, One Kind of Object at a Time

A museum collection could not recover as one unit because water had not damaged one kind of thing.

University of Iowa Libraries staff began cleaning the museum’s flood-damaged sound recordings in July 2008. In December 2009, close to 1,500 LPs and 45s returned after an estimated 750 hours of hands-on work. Another batch—1,580 ten-inch LPs and 78-rpm records packed in thirty boxes—required at least four hundred more hours.

The hours reveal what the word saved often hides. Each record had to be handled, cleaned, rehoused, boxed, and returned. Preservation was not a mood. It was production.

Books demanded another response. Roughly 7,500 frozen volumes were reported, and a nineteen-book sample was studied for possible treatment. The museum estimated that 20 percent of the books were beyond repair. Freezing did not restore them. It interrupted deterioration and bought time for later decisions.

A flood-damaged map arrived at conservation in thirty-two pieces and was reassembled. At the African American Museum of Iowa, the pages of a Bible with a failed binding were cleaned and the volume rebound.

These objects make Naughton’s problem concrete. Paper, bindings, maps, and sound recordings reacted differently to water. Each required its own expertise, sequence, cost, and measure of success. There could be no single heroic rescue because there was no single form of damage.

The stakes were also different from those in an ordinary inventory. A machine can sometimes be replaced by another machine of the same model. A museum object may carry information, memory, and association that no equivalent purchase can restore. Every hour spent cleaning a recording or rebuilding a binding preserved a particular route into the past.

Naughton was not merely saving things. She was deciding which connections to history could remain available.

## When the Building Became an Object

By 2010, the museum had proved it could operate outside its permanent home. The next question was what to do with the historic structure itself.

The organization chose neither abandonment nor a return to the building’s old condition. It would preserve the structure, move it, elevate it, and join it to an expanded permanent facility.

The container had become part of the collection.

In April 2011, the board approved a new mission, vision, and strategic plan for 2011 through 2015. Recovery and strategy were now one undertaking. Naughton was not simply trying to replace walls. She was rebuilding the operating system those walls would serve.

Patterson Structural Movers began moving the historic structure on June 8, 2011. The move ended on June 21 after a stated goal of 480 feet. The building was then raised onto a new foundation and parking garage. Contemporary and later accounts differ on whether the rise was eleven or twelve feet. The essential decision does not depend on choosing between them.

The museum refused to confuse faithfulness with stasis. It preserved the building by moving it.

That act required more than institutional resolve. Structural movers, engineers, architects, builders, conservators, public agencies, donors, staff, and volunteers all entered the chain. Rinderknecht Associates presented the building key to the museum on May 8, 2012—a milestone in a project whose collection work, exhibits, finances, and public operations continued on their own schedules.

Naughton also had to assemble capital around a divided problem. The museum announced a $175,000 National Endowment for the Humanities grant for post-flood preservation, cataloging, housing, and relocation. It announced a $147,351 Institute of Museum and Library Services grant for oral history. Federal support helped with the hazard-mitigation move. A capital campaign exceeded a reported $25 million, including $10 million from I-JOBS and $2.9 million from Vision Iowa.

The sums were not interchangeable. Collection care, oral history, hazard mitigation, construction, and expansion served different purposes. Naughton’s challenge was not to find one enormous pool of money. It was to align restricted sources of capital with distinct kinds of work closely enough that the whole institution could advance.

That is entrepreneurship in an institution: mission translated into projects, projects matched to resources, and every resource made to serve a coherent return.

## The Return

On July 14 and 15, 2012, the National Czech & Slovak Museum & Library held its public grand reopening.

The institution reported 191 volunteers contributing nearly eight hundred hours during the weekend. Attendance estimates ranged from 12,000 to 16,000. The exact count is less important than the business consequence. Four years after Naughton had described an empty shell, the museum again possessed a permanent public home.

The historic building had moved and risen. Temporary operations had carried the organization through the interval. Collections returned in batches even as other treatment continued. The reopening did not erase the flood. It gave the response durable form.

The museum survived because Naughton refused to define continuity as standing still. The old structure endured by changing location. The organization endured by working away from it. Collections survived unevenly through thousands of separate interventions. Return became possible because each part of the institution had been allowed to recover at the speed its problem required.

Other Cedar Rapids institutions chose other paths. The African American Museum repaired and reopened sooner, then continued conservation. The Douglas Mansion supplied temporary space to Theatre Cedar Rapids and Orchestra Iowa. Years later, The History Center adapted that mansion for its own use, beginning renovation in 2017 and opening to the public on October 13, 2018.

Repair, relocation, and reuse were not interchangeable answers. Together they enlarged the city’s repertoire of rebuilding.

## The Legacy of the Empty Shell

Naughton’s achievement does not depend on claiming that the museum single-handedly revived its district. One opening weekend cannot prove where visitors spent money or which nearby businesses survived.

Her legacy is more concrete.

She kept an institution operating when its permanent home could not. Under her leadership, the museum moved programs and offices into temporary quarters, sent damaged collections into specialized care, joined a new strategy to the rebuilding effort, assembled public and private support, moved and elevated a historic structure, and returned the mission to a permanent public home.

The emotional arc is visible in the enterprise itself. Two truckloads stood against unknown loss. An empty shell replaced a museum. Temporary operations proved that the mission could live outside its building. Conservation turned damage into work measured in boxes and hours. Construction made the building movable. Reopening turned endurance into return.

Gail Naughton’s lesson is simple enough to survive the flood: when the place that holds the mission fails, save the mission first—then build the place it deserves next.

That lesson became part of Cedar Rapids’ entrepreneurial vocabulary. A damaged building could be moved, raised, repaired, or given another use. But each transformation still had to answer the questions Naughton had faced: What must continue? Who will do the work? Where will the capital come from? What will exist after the celebration ends?

Ann Poe would take those questions into a factory shell. As the opening-period executive director of NewBo City Market, she helped turn a flood-damaged industrial building into a market and business incubator. Naughton had rebuilt a home for memory. Poe would test whether a recovered building could become a launching place for entrepreneurs strong enough to outgrow it.
