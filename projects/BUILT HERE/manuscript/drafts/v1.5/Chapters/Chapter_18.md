# Chapter 18: George Ronk’s Moving Target

George T. Ronk had already moved his company once. Now Cedar Rapids was asking him to move it again.

Speeder Machinery had begun in Leon, shifted to Fairfield, and remained small by the middle of the 1920s. Its Fairfield equipment list was modest enough to memorize: two lathes, a drill press, a power saw, and a forge. Then an October 1926 newspaper announced that Speeder would move to Cedar Rapids. The proposal called for a 35,000-square-foot plant and said 150 families would come with the company.

For Ronk, relocation was not an address change. It risked the continuity of a young business. Machinery had to be moved or replaced. Workers had to decide whether to follow. Production had to restart. A new building had to justify its cost. Customers waiting for excavating equipment would not be served by civic enthusiasm.

Speeder first operated in a streetcar barn, then built a permanent plant on former circus grounds. Later accounts report one hundred workers transferred, $40,000 spent on the new works, and about eighty-eight machines produced in the first Cedar Rapids year.

The numbers do not reveal Ronk’s private calculation. They reveal the gamble. He was moving from a shop-sized operation toward industrial scale.

Cedar Rapids offered metalworking firms, rail connections, power, builders, labor, and an emerging identity around road machinery. None guaranteed an order. Ronk’s company had to turn location into capability.

The move became the pivotal decision of his entrepreneurial life. Speeder survived, joined a broader product system, expanded through war and construction booms, and grew to employ thousands. It also became exposed to the fixed costs, outside control, and collapsing demand that scale can bring.

Ronk moved toward opportunity. His company spent the rest of its Cedar Rapids life chasing a market that never stopped moving.

## The Asset Ronk Created

Speeder’s early products addressed excavation. Later histories associate the company with the Tumblebug and a direct-cable-crowd design, though they do not establish a defensible claim to priority. The product logic fit the company’s name: construction equipment had to reach the place where dirt, rock, and structures needed to be moved.

Cedar Rapids gave Speeder room to become larger. A machinery plant needed space for incoming metal and parts, machining, fabrication, assembly, inspection, storage, and shipment. Those stages had to be coordinated around heavy products before revenue returned.

The first year remained an unfinished argument. The move looked decisive only because the company continued.

Ronk’s decision created an asset another business would value: a functioning Cedar Rapids machinery company with products at the smaller end of the excavating-equipment range.

In 1939, Link-Belt announced that it would combine its shovel division with Speeder. The resulting company became Link-Belt Speeder Corporation, with T. M. Deal as its first president. The transaction has been described as a combination, purchase, merger, and formation of a wholly owned subsidiary. Its strategic logic is clearer than its legal mechanics.

Speeder brought smaller machines. Link-Belt brought larger ones. A contemporary 1948 analysis called the ranges complementary and said the combination gave them “a much broader market to draw from.”

A broader line gave dealers more to sell and customers more reasons to stay with one organization as jobs changed in size. It also brought integration costs, overlapping products, and more distant authority.

Speeder’s move had made Cedar Rapids the home of an operation worth combining with a larger shovel line. Ronk’s local gamble became the foundation for a wider market—and control farther from the city.

## A Factory as Forecast

The Second World War tested whether the combined company could answer a different customer. Later company history reports a Cedar Rapids workforce of about five hundred and production of rubber-tired YC-9 Cargocranes for Construction Battalions and the Army Transportation Corps.

The wartime turn demanded more than a change in buyers. A company formed around civilian excavation and lifting had to translate military requirements into engineering, materials, production, inspection, and delivery.

Management did not wait for certainty after the war. Planning began in 1946 for a much larger factory-and-office complex. Completed in 1948, the project was designed by architect Milton Searle Carstens and structural engineer J. Kenny Johnson and built by Angot P. Larson. The new construction reportedly surrounded the 1926 plant.

The physical arrangement captured the entire wager. Ronk’s original works remained inside a larger industrial commitment. The company was not discarding its foundation. It was building around it.

The project’s reported value varies between $500,000 and $1.75 million, but the important fact is the commitment. Link-Belt Speeder enlarged the plant before it could know how postwar markets would develop.

Factories are forecasts made of steel and concrete. This one predicted enough demand to justify a step change in capacity.

## Who Shared the Growth

Management could expand the plant. It could not dictate the terms of work inside it.

At six in the morning on a July day in 1950, approximately seven hundred employees walked out, 675 of them members of UAW Local 299. The strike lasted a reported seventy-three days. A September 24 settlement brought wage increases and provisions involving vacations, pensions, and health insurance.

Link-Belt Speeder had built physical capacity. Workers controlled whether production moved through it.

Growth had created something worth bargaining over. Wages, time away from work, retirement, and medical costs were not side issues to the production story. They were the terms on which hundreds of people would continue making the machines.

The strike placed risk on both sides. Workers withheld labor and pay. Management faced idle capacity and interrupted orders. The settlement raised future labor costs while defining a bargain broader than the hourly wage.

Ronk’s relocation had been announced partly in families and transferred workers. By 1950, labor in Cedar Rapids was not merely an input drawn to the factory. It was organized power inside the enterprise.

The strike did not stop expansion. Link-Belt Speeder added space in 1953, 1956, and 1957. Product histories point to the Speed-O-Matic control system and the LS-98 crane as sources of confidence. The control system’s story spans a claimed 1936 introduction, a 1949 full-function design, and a 1952 trademark registration. The LS-98, introduced in 1954 according to later accounts, became one of the company’s enduring model lines.

The company was attempting to turn engineering control, lifting capacity, and a broad line into durable demand.

## David Lehti Chooses Capacity

In 1957, president David Lehti stated the company’s wager with unusual clarity. He wanted “the capacity to take full advantage of the substantial sales potential in the nation’s long-range building program.”

The crucial words were capacity and potential.

The Federal-Aid Highway Act had been approved the previous year. Public construction promised work for cranes, shovels, earthmovers, and aggregate equipment. A transportation photograph showed an LS-98 preparing a bridge footing for Interstate 80 near Bettendorf in January 1957.

Demand appeared to be coming. Capacity took time to design, finance, build, equip, and staff. If Lehti waited for every order, the company might miss the market. If he built too far ahead, Link-Belt Speeder would carry buildings, equipment, and payroll that buyers might not support.

He chose capacity.

Additions continued into the 1960s. A Bowling Street plant and design laboratories helped push the Cedar Rapids footprint above one million square feet, with employment approaching two thousand.

Construction equipment served more than highways. Link-Belt machines worked in mining, quarrying, utilities, industry, railroads, logging, pipelines, material handling, and general construction. Variety spread risk, but it could not eliminate the company’s fundamental exposure. Customers bought heavy machinery when they expected enough work—and affordable enough credit—to justify it.

Lehti’s sentence contained both the dream and the danger. Capacity was built in the present. Sales potential lived in the future.

## When Control Left Cedar Rapids

In 1967, FMC merged with Link-Belt Company. By 1972, the Cedar Rapids operation had become FMC’s Cable Crane and Excavator Division while the Link-Belt Speeder name remained on products.

The familiar brand concealed a critical change. The plant had become enormous, but decisions about capital and closure now sat inside a larger outside corporation.

Employment reached a reported peak in 1979, variously counted at about 2,100 or 2,300. Ronk’s small moving company had become one of Cedar Rapids’s major industrial employers.

Scale under outside control changed the wager. FMC could compare plants and products across a national organization. Cedar Rapids workers and managers remained close to the machines. Final authority no longer needed to remain close to Cedar Rapids.

The name could preserve customer recognition. It could not preserve local command.

## The Market Turns

The contraction came faster than the expansion.

The workforce fell to a reported 1,550 in July 1980, 700 in April 1982, and 380 by late 1983, fewer than one hundred of them in production. The direction was too severe to mistake.

Orders and construction investment had weakened. Interest rates were high. Demand and profits fell while competition pressed harder. The exact weight of each cause remains unresolved, but the trap was familiar. Heavy equipment was expensive. Customers depended on active projects and affordable credit. When construction slowed and borrowing became costly, purchases could be postponed.

A company built to capture a boom now carried the cost of capacity without the orders that justified it.

On February 22, 1985, a contemporary report announced that FMC expected to close its Cedar Rapids crane-and-excavator plant by midyear. Paired with a hydraulic-crane closing in Ontario, the action would affect nearly five hundred employees across the two facilities.

The property moved on. In 1986, FMC reportedly sold the nineteen-acre site at 1201 Sixth Street SW to the City of Cedar Rapids for $1.5 million.

The work did not remain with it.

## The Cost of Scale

George Ronk could not have known the full arc when Cedar Rapids courted Speeder in 1926. His decision brought the company to a city where it found room, workers, industrial capability, and a path into a much larger organization.

The permanent plant rose on circus ground. Link-Belt broadened the product line. War work tested the shops. Postwar management built around Ronk’s factory. Workers forced a wider bargain. Lehti committed capacity to a national building program. FMC took control. Employment climbed above two thousand and then collapsed.

The history does not prove that one highway law created the rise or that one recession caused the fall. It proves a more useful business truth. Specialized machinery companies live between engineering confidence and customer timing. They must invest before demand is certain, yet the capacity that captures a boom deepens the loss when orders disappear.

Ronk’s pivotal choice was relocation. It placed Speeder where the company could become far larger than the operation he moved. Its consequence was opportunity on a scale that eventually exceeded local control.

His lesson is unforgettable: **capacity turns opportunity into growth—and uncertainty into fixed cost.**

Cedar Rapids had learned to make machines that built roads, bridges, quarries, and industrial sites. The next entrepreneurs would work closer to the customer. As machinery remade movement, Paul Lattner began building a market around the automobile—not the machine that made the road, but the machine that persuaded ordinary people to use it.
