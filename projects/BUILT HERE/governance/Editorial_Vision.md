# Editorial Vision

**Project:** *Built Here: The Cedar Rapids Advantage*  
**Status:** Draft v1.0  
**Purpose:** Define the editorial identity every AI agent must preserve while researching, outlining, drafting, revising, and assembling the manuscript.

---

# 1. Editorial Mission

Produce a book that reads like the work of a disciplined historian and an exceptional narrative nonfiction writer—not like a collection of AI-generated summaries.

The reader should finish the book believing they understand **why Cedar Rapids repeatedly produced builders**, not simply knowing more facts about Cedar Rapids.

Every chapter must strengthen that understanding.

---

# 2. The Editorial Question

Every editorial decision should answer:

> Does this help explain why this place repeatedly produced extraordinary companies and entrepreneurs?

If not, it probably does not belong.

---

# 3. Desired Reader Experience

The reader should feel they are:

- discovering a mystery
- meeting remarkable people
- watching institutions evolve
- seeing connections emerge
- gradually recognizing a larger pattern

The experience should reward curiosity rather than overwhelm with information.

---

# 4. Voice

The voice should be:

- intelligent
- confident
- restrained
- curious
- analytical
- humane
- vivid
- specific

Avoid:

- hype
- boosterism
- clichés
- TED-talk language
- startup jargon
- inflated adjectives
- unnecessary sentimentality

The narrator should never sound impressed simply because a company became large.

---

# 5. Narrative Philosophy

Facts are necessary.

Narrative is essential.

Information should become story through:

- people
- decisions
- conflict
- uncertainty
- consequences
- institutions
- time

Whenever possible:

People → Decisions → Outcomes → Meaning

Not:

Facts → More facts → More facts.

---

# 6. Characters

Companies are not the protagonists.

People are.

Institutions are recurring characters.

The city itself is a character.

Readers should understand:

- ambitions
- incentives
- risks
- constraints
- relationships
- failures
- turning points

without fictionalizing history.

---

# 7. Structure

The book should continually alternate between:

- individual stories
- institutional development
- broader analysis

This rhythm prevents the work from becoming either biography or textbook.

---

# 8. Evidence First

Narrative must emerge from evidence.

Never reverse the process.

Do not construct stories and then search for supporting facts.

---

# 9. Intellectual Honesty

Agents are expected to challenge:

- popular narratives
- civic myths
- convenient explanations
- simplistic cause-and-effect

Complexity is acceptable.

Unsupported certainty is not.

---

# 10. The Cedar Rapids Standard

The city is neither hero nor villain.

It is the environment under investigation.

Praise and criticism should arise naturally from evidence.

---

# 11. Style Standards

Prefer:

- concrete nouns
- active verbs
- precise dates
- named people
- observable actions
- measurable outcomes

Limit:

- abstractions
- vague transitions
- repeated framing
- long introductions

Every paragraph should move the story or argument forward.

---

# 12. AI Writing Constraints

Agents must avoid recognizable AI habits:

- repetitive sentence rhythm
- excessive signposting
- empty summaries
- "It is important to note..."
- "In conclusion..."
- obvious moral lessons
- unnecessary bullet-like prose inside chapters

If a sentence sounds generic, rewrite it.

If a paragraph could appear in any business book, delete it.

---

# 13. Chapter Objective

Each chapter should accomplish one clear job.

Before drafting, every chapter brief must answer:

- Why this chapter exists.
- What question it answers.
- What changes for the reader.
- Why it cannot be removed.

---

# 14. Ending

The conclusion should not merely celebrate the past.

It should ask what kind of builders the next generation must become and why history creates responsibility rather than nostalgia.

The final emotion should be possibility grounded in evidence.

---

# 15. Editorial Test

Before approving any chapter, ask:

- Would an informed reader voluntarily keep reading?
- Does this chapter reveal something genuinely new?
- Does it advance the central thesis?
- Is every important claim supported?
- Does it sound like one author?
- Is it worthy of publication?

If any answer is "no," revise before proceeding.

---

# Governing Principle

The editorial standard is not "good for AI."

It is:

> Would this stand beside the best narrative business histories published today?
