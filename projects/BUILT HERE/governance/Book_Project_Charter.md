# Book Project Charter

**Project:** *Built Here: The Cedar Rapids Advantage*  
**Document Type:** Governing Project Charter  
**Status:** Draft v1.0  
**Authority:** Project Owner  
**Applies To:** All human participants, AI agents, models, workflows, repositories, and derived artifacts associated with the book project

---

## 1. Purpose

This charter defines the mission, scope, standards, authority, constraints, and completion criteria for the creation of *Built Here: The Cedar Rapids Advantage*.

The project has one primary objective:

> Produce the best possible nonfiction book explaining why Cedar Rapids repeatedly generated an unusual concentration of successful entrepreneurs, companies, and builders.

The book is the primary product.

The AI publishing system, agent architecture, workflows, schemas, and repository practices developed during the project are secondary products extracted from the real work of producing the book. The system exists to serve the book. The book does not exist to demonstrate the system.

---

## 2. Central Question

The book must answer:

> Why did Cedar Rapids produce, attract, and sustain an extraordinary concentration of successful entrepreneurs and companies?

Every major section, chapter, profile, historical episode, and analytical claim must contribute to answering that question.

The project must not assume the premise is true merely because it is compelling. The agents must test the premise, identify its limits, compare Cedar Rapids with relevant peer cities, and distinguish evidence from civic mythology.

---

## 3. Working Thesis

The initial working thesis is:

> Cedar Rapids became an unusually productive environment for builders because of the interaction among its industrial base, geography, civic institutions, immigrant and family networks, local capital, business culture, educational systems, social expectations, and repeated examples of entrepreneurial success.

This thesis is provisional.

Research agents are required to test, refine, narrow, complicate, or reject any part of it when the evidence demands. No agent may preserve the working thesis at the expense of accuracy.

Any material revision to the central thesis requires Project Owner approval before it becomes authoritative.

---

## 4. What the Book Is

The book is:

- A narrative work of business and civic history.
- An investigation into a place-based entrepreneurial ecosystem.
- A study of how people, institutions, industries, capital, culture, and historical conditions interacted.
- A commercially readable nonfiction book grounded in serious research.
- A unified narrative rather than a collection of unrelated profiles.
- A book about builders, decisions, institutions, risks, failures, consequences, and legacy.
- A forward-looking work connecting Cedar Rapids' history to the entrepreneurs who may build its next generation of important companies.

The book should read as a coherent work written in one consistent editorial voice, even though AI agents perform substantial portions of the research, planning, drafting, editing, and verification.

---

## 5. What the Book Is Not

The book is not:

- A general history of Cedar Rapids.
- A chamber-of-commerce promotional book.
- A collection of founder biographies.
- A directory of successful companies.
- An academic monograph written primarily for specialists.
- A motivational business book built on vague lessons.
- A celebration that ignores exclusion, failure, conflict, decline, or contradiction.
- A vehicle for unverified local legends.
- A claim that every successful Cedar Rapids entrepreneur succeeded for the same reasons.
- A hidden or falsely represented work of sole human authorship.

Biographical material, city history, institutional history, and company history may be included only when they advance the central inquiry or strengthen the narrative.

---

## 6. Intended Reader

The primary reader is an intelligent general reader interested in:

- entrepreneurship,
- business history,
- American cities,
- economic development,
- civic institutions,
- industrial history,
- founders and company building,
- and why certain places repeatedly produce consequential people and organizations.

The reader should not need prior knowledge of Cedar Rapids.

The finished book should also be valuable to:

- current and future Cedar Rapids entrepreneurs,
- business leaders,
- historians,
- economic-development professionals,
- civic leaders,
- educators,
- investors,
- and readers interested in the Midwest's role in American enterprise.

---

## 7. Reader Promise

By the end of the book, the reader should understand:

1. Whether Cedar Rapids truly produced an unusual concentration of entrepreneurs and companies.
2. Which founders, companies, institutions, and historical periods matter most.
3. What conditions made Cedar Rapids unusually productive for builders.
4. How those conditions reinforced one another across generations.
5. Where the city's entrepreneurial mythology differs from the historical record.
6. Which elements of the Cedar Rapids advantage were transferable and which were unique.
7. Whether the city's entrepreneurial engine persists today.
8. What the next generation of Cedar Rapids builders may inherit, recover, or need to reinvent.

The book must provide a satisfying answer without pretending that one simple explanation accounts for every success.

---

## 8. Narrative Ambition

The standard is not merely factual completeness.

The book must be:

- narratively compelling,
- structurally coherent,
- intellectually defensible,
- emotionally grounded,
- accessible,
- specific,
- and publication quality.

The preferred mode is narrative history supported by analysis.

Whenever the evidence permits, the book should present:

- consequential decisions,
- moments of uncertainty,
- conflicts,
- risks,
- institutional turning points,
- competitive pressure,
- failures,
- personal stakes,
- and long-term consequences.

The book must not manufacture scenes, dialogue, motives, emotions, chronology, or causal links.

When the historical record does not support scene-level reconstruction, the prose must remain transparent rather than filling gaps with plausible invention.

---

## 9. Geographic Scope

The primary geographic focus is Cedar Rapids, Iowa.

The project may include surrounding communities, regional institutions, national markets, migration patterns, investors, suppliers, competitors, and external events when they materially affected the Cedar Rapids entrepreneurial ecosystem.

The book must define when an individual or company qualifies as part of the Cedar Rapids story.

Possible qualifying relationships include:

- founded in Cedar Rapids,
- substantially built in Cedar Rapids,
- headquartered in Cedar Rapids during a formative period,
- shaped by Cedar Rapids institutions or networks,
- founded elsewhere by individuals formed by the Cedar Rapids ecosystem,
- or materially influential in the development of the city's business culture.

Borderline cases must be documented and resolved consistently.

---

## 10. Historical Scope

The research may extend as far back as necessary to explain the conditions that shaped the city.

The narrative should concentrate on periods that materially contributed to Cedar Rapids' entrepreneurial development.

The book must not force equal coverage across all eras. Historical periods should receive attention in proportion to their explanatory and narrative importance.

The final chapter should be forward-looking and connect the historical record to present and future builders.

Its working concept is:

> **The Next Builders**

The conclusion should challenge future Cedar Rapids entrepreneurs to build something worthy of inclusion in a future edition of the book.

This is a core creative principle unless later revised by the Project Owner.

---

## 11. Research Mandate

The research process must begin as an investigation, not as confirmation of a predetermined civic story.

Agents must:

- identify the relevant founder and company universe,
- establish an accurate historical timeline,
- map institutions and networks,
- investigate capital formation,
- test cultural explanations,
- study industries and infrastructure,
- identify failures and counterexamples,
- compare Cedar Rapids with relevant peer cities,
- distinguish correlation from plausible causation,
- document uncertainty,
- and challenge weak or convenient conclusions.

The system must actively search for evidence that contradicts the working thesis.

Research output must be captured in durable repository artifacts rather than retained only inside model context or chat history.

---

## 12. Evidence Standard

No factual claim may enter the manuscript without traceable support.

All claims must be connected to source records containing sufficient provenance to allow later verification.

The project should prefer:

1. Primary sources.
2. Contemporaneous records.
3. Archival materials.
4. Official documents and company records.
5. Recorded or documented interviews.
6. Reputable scholarly and journalistic secondary sources.
7. Later recollections, local histories, or unsourced accounts only when clearly labeled and appropriately treated.

Important claims should be corroborated whenever practical.

Conflicting accounts must be preserved and evaluated rather than silently resolved.

Agents must never invent:

- quotations,
- dialogue,
- events,
- dates,
- motives,
- relationships,
- financial figures,
- internal thoughts,
- scene details,
- or causal conclusions.

Uncertainty must be recorded explicitly.

Detailed requirements will be governed by `Evidence_Standard.md` and the approved source-record schema.

---

## 13. AI Authorship and Disclosure

This is intentionally an AI-produced book project.

AI agents and models may perform substantial work, including:

- source discovery,
- source processing,
- research synthesis,
- chronology building,
- claim extraction,
- comparative analysis,
- chapter architecture,
- drafting,
- developmental editing,
- line editing,
- continuity review,
- citation checking,
- fact verification,
- and manuscript assembly.

The published book will openly disclose the role of AI in its creation.

The project must not falsely imply that the Project Owner personally authored the manuscript in the conventional sense.

Disclosure should be clear, accurate, and confident. It should explain that the work was produced through a directed AI publishing process with human ownership, judgment, approval, and accountability.

The exact disclosure language will be developed later as a dedicated publishing artifact.

---

## 14. Project Owner Role

The Project Owner is not required to act as the book's traditional author.

The Project Owner serves as:

- publisher,
- project sponsor,
- editor-in-chief,
- product owner,
- final decision-maker,
- and accountable human authority.

The Project Owner is responsible for:

- defining the ambition of the book,
- approving the governing artifacts,
- resolving major scope and thesis questions,
- approving the narrative architecture,
- approving the final table of contents,
- reviewing defined editorial gates,
- deciding whether the work meets the publication standard,
- and authorizing final publication.

The Project Owner is not expected to:

- personally conduct all research,
- draft chapters,
- rewrite agent prose line by line,
- imitate a conventional author,
- or manually coordinate routine agent work.

The system should minimize unnecessary human intervention while preserving human authority over consequential decisions.

---

## 15. Agent Authority

Within the approved repository contracts and workflow, the Orchestrator and specialist agents may:

- create required project artifacts,
- assign and coordinate work,
- conduct research,
- propose thesis revisions,
- identify gaps,
- reject weak outputs,
- request agent revisions,
- compare competing interpretations,
- generate chapter briefs,
- draft chapters,
- perform editorial review,
- verify claims,
- maintain project state,
- and advance work between non-human gates.

Agents may not:

- silently redefine the book,
- bypass governing artifacts,
- lower evidence standards,
- invent unsupported material,
- conceal uncertainty,
- approve their own work at required human gates,
- delete conflicting evidence to simplify the narrative,
- represent provisional work as final,
- or declare the manuscript complete without Project Owner approval.

Agent outputs are work product, not authority.

Repository artifacts and approved decisions govern the project.

---

## 16. Model and Platform Independence

The project may use:

- Hermes for orchestration,
- OpenAI models,
- Anthropic models,
- local models,
- retrieval systems,
- code tools,
- and other approved services.

No critical project knowledge should exist only inside one vendor's platform, one conversation, one model context, or one agent's memory.

Models and agents are replaceable.

The durable project state must remain in the repository through:

- structured artifacts,
- Markdown documents,
- source records,
- decision logs,
- review records,
- status files,
- and version history.

The system should permit models to be upgraded, replaced, or reassigned without losing the book's institutional memory.

---

## 17. Repository Authority

The GitHub-backed local repository is the authoritative operational record of the project.

The repository must preserve:

- source provenance,
- research findings,
- claims,
- decisions,
- open questions,
- narrative architecture,
- chapter briefs,
- drafts,
- reviews,
- verification results,
- approvals,
- and final manuscript components.

Chat histories, model memories, and temporary agent context are not authoritative records.

Any consequential decision made outside the repository must be captured in an appropriate repository artifact before later work depends on it.

Structured artifacts should be used where validation, automation, or machine interpretation is important.

Markdown should be used where human review, explanation, or editorial judgment is primary.

---

## 18. Human Approval Gates

The project requires Project Owner approval at the following book-level gates:

### Gate 1: Thesis and Narrative Architecture

Approval is required for:

- the refined central thesis,
- the major explanatory framework,
- the proposed narrative structure,
- and the organizing logic of the book.

### Gate 2: Final Table of Contents

Approval is required before full chapter production begins.

The table of contents must show:

- chapter purpose,
- narrative progression,
- major subjects,
- evidence dependencies,
- and each chapter's contribution to the central question.

The project also requires Project Owner approval at the following chapter-level gates:

### Gate 3: Chapter Brief

Each chapter brief must be approved before drafting.

### Gate 4: First Draft

Each first draft must be approved, rejected, or redirected before final revision.

### Gate 5: Final Chapter

Each final chapter must be approved before being locked into the manuscript.

The Orchestrator may manage everything else automatically unless another governing artifact establishes an additional gate.

---

## 19. Quality Standard

A chapter is not successful merely because it is grammatically correct or factually dense.

Each chapter must:

- serve a clear narrative purpose,
- advance the book's central inquiry,
- contain traceable evidence,
- distinguish fact from interpretation,
- avoid redundancy with other chapters,
- maintain continuity,
- meet the approved voice and style standard,
- provide meaningful human stakes,
- and justify its place in the book.

The manuscript must demonstrate:

- one coherent voice,
- disciplined structure,
- reliable sourcing,
- intellectual honesty,
- narrative momentum,
- consistent terminology,
- controlled repetition,
- and a conclusion supported by the body of evidence.

Generic AI prose, inflated language, repeated framing, unsupported certainty, empty transitions, and synthetic-sounding moral lessons are unacceptable.

---

## 20. Commercial Standard

The book should be capable of reaching readers beyond Cedar Rapids.

Local specificity is an advantage, but the book must reveal broader truths about:

- how entrepreneurial ecosystems form,
- how communities produce builders,
- how institutions compound over time,
- how business cultures reproduce themselves,
- and why some cities repeatedly generate consequential enterprises.

The manuscript must remain rooted in Cedar Rapids rather than turning into a generic business book.

Commercial accessibility must not come at the expense of historical rigor.

---

## 21. Ethical Standard

The project must treat individuals, families, companies, institutions, and communities fairly.

Agents must avoid:

- unsupported accusations,
- careless reputational claims,
- present-day judgments detached from historical context,
- manipulation of evidence for narrative convenience,
- and concealment of material conflicts.

Positive civic mythology must be tested as rigorously as criticism.

Living individuals and sensitive claims require heightened verification and editorial review.

The book must be honest about who benefited from the city's systems, who did not, and which barriers or exclusions shaped the entrepreneurial record when the evidence supports those conclusions.

---

## 22. Decision Hierarchy

When project instructions conflict, the following order of authority applies:

1. Project Owner's explicit approved decision.
2. This `Book_Project_Charter.md`.
3. `The_First_Principles.md`.
4. Approved book-specific governing artifacts.
5. Approved repository schemas and workflow contracts.
6. Orchestrator instructions.
7. Specialist agent specifications.
8. Individual prompts and temporary task instructions.

Lower-level instructions may not override higher-level authority.

Conflicts must be surfaced and recorded rather than resolved silently.

## 24. Initial Production Sequence

Once the governing artifacts are approved, Hermes should direct the agents through the following sequence:

1. Establish the research universe.
2. Build the foundational chronology.
3. Identify founders, companies, institutions, and networks.
4. Gather and register sources.
5. Test the central premise.
6. Compare Cedar Rapids with relevant peer cities.
7. Produce a thesis assessment.
8. Propose the narrative architecture.
9. Produce a provisional table of contents.
10. Submit the thesis, architecture, and table of contents for human approval.
11. Create chapter briefs.
12. Begin controlled chapter production.
13. Run developmental review, verification, continuity review, and revision.
14. Assemble the manuscript.
15. Conduct full-manuscript editorial and factual review.
16. Prepare final publication files and AI disclosure.

The first chapter must not be drafted merely because it is first in sequence. It should be drafted only after its purpose within the full narrative is understood and its evidence base is sufficient.

---

## 25. Definition of Done

The project is complete only when:

- the manuscript fully addresses the central question,
- all chapters have passed required approval gates,
- major factual claims are traceable and verified,
- unresolved material uncertainties are disclosed or removed,
- the narrative reads as one coherent work,
- the manuscript has passed full-book developmental review,
- continuity and repetition have been checked,
- the final chapter successfully connects the city's history to its next builders,
- AI involvement is accurately disclosed,
- publication materials are complete,
- and the Project Owner approves the book for release.

The existence of a complete draft does not mean the project is complete.

The book is done when it is worthy of publication.

---

## 26. Governing Principle

When the needs of the system and the needs of the book conflict:

> The book wins.

Every artifact, agent, workflow, schema, and technical decision must earn its place by helping produce a more accurate, compelling, coherent, and publishable book.
