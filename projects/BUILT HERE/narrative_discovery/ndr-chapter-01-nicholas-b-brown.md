# Narrative Discovery Record — Chapter 1: Nicholas B. Brown

# Entrepreneur

**Name:** Nicholas B. Brown

**Chapter:** 1 — “Brown’s Proposition”

# The One Truth

The survey Brown was reportedly associated with commencing did not merely mark a settlement. It divided Rapids City into slightly more than sixty blocks and 805 planned lots before a proven lot market or dependable river-power system existed. Brown’s defining entrepreneurial act was helping turn unresolved possibility into a proposition concrete enough for someone else to enter.

# Why It Matters

“Surveying a town” sounds bureaucratic; 805 lots reveal the scale of the wager. Brown helped give an unproven venture commercial form before title, buyers, or the river could validate it. This changes him from a generic founder into a builder who created the next usable condition. For George Greene, the plat made uncertainty legible enough to act on. Brown’s courage was structuring uncertainty so another commitment could happen.

# Courage Revealed

> **Despite unresolved control, no proven lot market, and no dependable power system, Brown chose to help survey Rapids City because his actions support the belief that practical form could attract the commitments needed to create value.**

# Reader-Retell Sentence

> **Brown helped turn an uncertain settlement into 805 proposed lots before there was a proven market or dependable river power—he made the opportunity usable before he could make it safe.**

# Placement Requirements

**Required appearance:** Opening page, within the first five percent.

State the 805-lot scale and the missing commercial certainties together. Do not place treaty history, founder claims, or waterpower background ahead of Brown’s defining decision.

# Evidence Boundary

## Supported Facts

- A historic-context study describes slightly more than sixty blocks and 805 lots and names Brown among the platting company.
- A later transcription of Brown’s unrecovered account book reports an August 4, 1841 survey start and includes Brown in the survey group.
- Existing research establishes neither a proven market for all 805 lots nor dependable river power at the time.

## Required Qualifications

- Brown was a participant, not a documented sole founder, owner, surveyor, or designer.
- The lots were planned inventory; the record does not show Brown owned or sold them all.
- The survey date survives through a later transcription, not the original account book.
- Brown’s authority, ownership share, investment, motive, and risk assessment remain unknown.

# What Must Never Be Lost

- The physical scale: slightly more than sixty blocks and 805 planned lots.
- The commercial uncertainty: no proven lot market or dependable river power.
- The courageous decision: create usable form before success was visible.
- The inheritance: Brown made the proposition legible enough for George Greene’s next decision.

# What Must Never Be Added

- Brown as sole founder, owner, surveyor, or designer.
- Claims that Brown owned or sold all 805 lots.
- Invented motive, emotion, dialogue, opposition, or scenes.
- Claims that the plat guaranteed title, value, or dependable power.
- An empty-land origin that erases coercive settler expansion and continuing Meskwaki presence.
