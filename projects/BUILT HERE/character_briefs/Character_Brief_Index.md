# Character Brief Index — Built Here Version 1.5

## Scope and Evidence Scale

This index covers the canonical **22-chapter** Version 1.5 manuscript and its **19 unique entrepreneurs/builders**. Nicholas B. Brown is unified in one brief across Chapters **1 and 3**; Arthur Collins is unified in one brief across Chapters **15–17**. Every other builder has one chapter and one brief.

Evidence-strength labels assess the historical record available for answering the Character Brief questions, not the quality of the brief's prose:

- **Strong:** multiple, specific records support the builder's decisions, stakes, consequences, and character pattern.
- **Moderate:** the central actions are supported, but motives, authority, finances, or personal consequences remain incomplete.
- **Thin:** a defensible brief exists, but too few person-level or transaction-level records answer several required character questions directly.

## Ranked Five Weakest Character Briefs

This ranking measures only the thinness of the surviving historical record for the required character questions. It does not identify weak writing.

1. **#1 — John Pinkerton (Chapter 21).** The record identifies him as co-owner rather than sole founder and supports the stall-to-storefront sequence, but contains little John-specific biography, contemporaneous decision evidence, financial information, or division of authority among John, Teresa, and their partners. Missing are the tenant application and terms, sales or margin evidence behind expansion, lease and financing obligations, setbacks, and John's own account of why the larger commitment was justified. The brief must therefore derive character mainly from joint business actions and one “low risk” statement.
2. **#2 — James C. Young (Chapter 5).** An 883-lot addition and a later account of a brass-band and free-food promotion support the central strategy, but the record lacks Young's correspondence, financing, acquisition cost, mortgages, advertising campaign, sales ledger, buyer terms, sales result, losses, and demonstrated control of the streetcar connection. His desire, persistence, risk, and consequence are mostly structural inferences from the plat and promotion rather than person-level evidence.
3. **#3 — David W. King (Chapter 4).** The ferry franchise, flood interruption, and Kingston plats establish a consequential paired venture, but biography, operating records, tolls, ferry investment, land financing, lot sales, partnership terms, and the division of work between David and Mary King are absent. The record does not directly show King's response to the 1851 washout or how bridge competition and franchise expiration affected him.
4. **#4 — W. E. Penick (Chapter 10).** A contemporary report of repeated visits supports personal attention to the 1920 reconstruction, and later plant and patent records establish consequences. However, the purchase agreement, board authorization, construction budget, asset inventory, correspondence, design authority, first successful run, first shipment, and Penick's exact capital and management role are unavailable. Much of the operating story belongs to Penick & Ford, not demonstrably to W. E. Penick himself.
5. **#5 — Keith Barnes (Chapter 18).** The acquisition, assumed labor agreement, later layoffs, closure, bankruptcy, and claims provide a solid transaction-and-consequence outline. The weak point is person-level decision evidence: the record lacks a substantive Barnes interview, acquisition model, financing package, board materials, diligence findings, operating reports, negotiations with Wilson, lenders, customers, and the union, and a documented explanation of why he persisted or closed when he did.
