# CHARACTER BRIEF

## Entrepreneur

Gail Naughton

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> Gail Naughton was driven by the determination to keep a cultural mission alive even when the building that embodied it could no longer serve it.

## Core Identity

Naughton is an institutional entrepreneur, not the conventional owner of the National Czech & Slovak Museum & Library. Her defining work was to lead a mission-dependent organization through catastrophic physical loss by disassembling the problem into survivable parts: emergency removal, temporary public operations, offices, specialized conservation, fundraising, strategy, relocation, elevation, construction, and reopening.

Her character emerges through triage and orchestration. She did not equate stewardship with keeping every object or function in one place. She preserved continuity by allowing different parts of the institution to move and recover on different clocks.

## What They Wanted

Naughton wanted the museum's collections, staff work, programs, public identity, and mission to survive the 2008 flood and return in a permanent home less exposed to a recurrence. The concrete outcome was not simply repair: it was continued operation during displacement, conservation of recoverable materials, movement and elevation of the historic structure, expansion, and public reopening.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

The reviewed record does not establish a private psychological motive. Her documented role carried stewardship obligations toward irreplaceable collections, staff, donors, volunteers, Czech and Slovak communities, visitors, and a national cultural mission. Her choices—planning exhibits and store operations while the main building was an empty shell, sustaining temporary sites, aligning restricted funds, and moving the structure—show that she treated closure of the premises as unacceptable evidence for closure of the institution.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Naughton saw that the museum was not identical to its building. Collections, public programming, staff work, capital, and construction could be separated operationally without abandoning one coherent mission. She also saw preservation need not mean stasis: the faithful way to save the historic building was to move and elevate it.

## Defining Character Traits

- **Decisive under uncertainty:** Staff removed two truckloads before the flood; days after the crest, Naughton had to plan without knowing the full loss.
- **Adaptive:** She used a mall storefront and later separate offices while permanent construction remained unresolved.
- **Disciplined:** Different objects went through different conservation methods; different grants were matched to distinct work rather than treated as interchangeable money.
- **Persuasive and coalition-building:** Under her leadership, the museum assembled public and private support, a capital campaign exceeding a reported $25 million, and the labor of agencies, donors, staff, volunteers, conservators, movers, and builders.
- **Strategic:** The board adopted a new mission, vision, and strategic plan while recovery was still underway.
- **Resilient:** Her persistence is visible in four years of temporary operations, conservation, relocation, elevation, and return, not in an invented statement of emotion.

## Greatest Risk

Naughton risked the survival and credibility of an institution entrusted with irreplaceable objects. Failed continuity could mean staff and audience loss, donor fatigue, deteriorating collections, wasted restricted funds, and disappearance of a national cultural mission from public life. The building move and expanded facility added engineering, schedule, capital, and reputational risk. She did not personally own the institution, so its assets and liabilities should not be presented as her personal property.

## Greatest Obstacle

The greatest obstacle was simultaneous fragmentation. The flood damaged the building and multiple kinds of collections while displacing operations and affecting the surrounding district and city systems. No single repair, grant, site, or reopening date could solve the whole problem.

## Defining Decision

Naughton could have kept the organization closed until the permanent building was repaired, attempted to return everything to the same structure, or separate the mission from the premises and operate each recovery track independently. She chose the third path.

Within a month of the flood she was planning exhibits, store operations, and programs elsewhere. Temporary exhibits ran at Lindale Mall; offices later occupied the Kosek Building. The institution conserved collections through outside specialists, then chose to move and elevate the historic structure and connect it to an expanded facility.

The decision cost years of coordination, temporary operations, fundraising, and complex construction. It changed a flooded shell into an institution capable of operating through displacement and reopening in July 2012.

## Contradiction

Naughton was **a preservation leader who preserved by refusing to keep things in place**. Records traveled, books were frozen, programs and offices moved, and the historic building itself crossed 480 feet and rose eleven or twelve feet. The apparent contradiction is documented and central: continuity required movement.

## Human Stakes

Staff needed an operating institution and places to work. Donors and public agencies needed evidence that support would serve defined purposes. Volunteers and conservation partners carried thousands of hours of recovery labor. Czech and Slovak communities and visitors depended on access to objects and stories that could not simply be replaced from inventory. If the strategy failed, a damaged building could take the living institution down with it.

## Emotional Arc

> **Ambition:** Preserve the museum's mission, collections, and public life through a catastrophic flood → **Pressure:** Two truckloads are out, the rest of the loss is initially unknown, the cleaned building becomes an empty shell, and every recovery track demands different expertise and money → **Decision:** Separate mission from premises, operate temporarily, conserve by object type, and preserve the historic structure by moving and elevating it → **Consequence:** The organization remains visible, collections return unevenly, capital and construction converge, and a permanent public home reopens in July 2012 → **Legacy:** Naughton leaves Cedar Rapids a tested model of institutional survival: save what must continue first, then build a safer form around it.

## Memorable Details

- Two truckloads removed before inundation.
- On June 17, 2008, four days after the crest, Naughton still had not entered the building.
- The Cedar River gauge reached 31.12 feet.
- The building was cleaned, disinfected, and stripped to what she called an “empty shell.”
- A temporary Lindale Mall exhibit operated from October 2008 through December 2009.
- Offices operated in the Kosek Building from April 2010 through May 2012.
- Nearly 1,500 records returned after an estimated 750 hands-on conservation hours; another thirty boxes required at least four hundred hours.
- Roughly 7,500 books were frozen; the museum estimated 20 percent were beyond repair.
- A map arrived at conservation in thirty-two pieces.
- The historic building moved a stated 480 feet between June 8 and June 21, 2011, then was elevated.
- The reported capital campaign exceeded $25 million, assembled from distinct public and private sources.
- The museum reopened July 14–15, 2012, with 191 volunteers contributing nearly eight hundred hours during the weekend.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids gave Naughton a museum rooted in Czech Village, staff and volunteers, public attention, temporary commercial and office space, donors, cultural partners, city recovery planning, construction capacity, and a regional network of universities, conservators, agencies, and funders. The flood-damaged city also created competition for land, money, labor, and speed; local context was capability and constraint at once.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

Under Naughton's leadership, the museum remained a functioning employer, cultural institution, public destination, and steward of memory despite losing its permanent home. The moved and elevated building and expanded museum enlarged the city's repertoire of flood recovery and demonstrated that historic preservation could be adaptive rather than static.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

Naughton left usable recovery knowledge and a core principle: a damaged building should be judged by what mission can continue through and beyond it. John Pinkerton enters another flood-damaged industrial shell remade as NewBo City Market, where shared infrastructure lets him test a business without first carrying a whole storefront.

## Chapter North Star

> This chapter is the story of an institutional leader who saved a museum by proving that its mission could move before its building did.

## Closing Handoff

Naughton separates mission from a damaged building, keeps the institution alive in temporary spaces, and rebuilds around what can continue. NewBo City Market applies the complementary lesson to commerce: restore a flood-damaged factory as shared infrastructure so John Pinkerton can begin with a stall, learn, and decide whether the evidence justifies a storefront.
