# CHARACTER BRIEF

## Entrepreneur

Milo Barta

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> Milo Barta was driven by the need to turn individual worker vulnerability into a durable institution capable of contesting company power.

## Core Identity

Barta was a labor organizer and institution-builder, not a company owner or investor. At Wilson’s Cedar Rapids packinghouse, he and Lewis Clark formed Federal Labor Union 18530 on August 23, 1933. His entrepreneurial resource was not ownership capital; it was worker participation, collective commitment, and the organizational structure needed to convert those into leverage.

The record places Barta at the first hinge, not at every later achievement. Others affiliated the union, made it independent, entered the CIO, won recognition, negotiated agreements, struck, picketed, and used arbitration. His defining contribution was to help establish an organization designed to outgrow the grievance—and the founder—that began it.

## What They Wanted

Barta sought a durable vehicle through which packinghouse workers could act collectively over the terms of work. The concrete outcome was not a single protest but an organization capable of affiliation, recognition, bargaining, written agreements, grievance procedures, and eventually arbitration.

He wanted an individual employee facing a powerful employer to have rules, participation, and an institution behind him.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

**Interpretation, strongly supported by the founding act and workplace context:** Earlier walkouts and organizing efforts had challenged company authority without leaving the documented durable local standing Barta helped initiate. The packinghouse continued to concentrate management power over staffing, machinery, pace, benefits, discipline, and access to pay. An individual worker could object, leave, or endure, but could not create lasting counterpower alone.

No surviving personal statement establishes Barta’s private motive or emotional state. His documented choice supports a practical imperative: recurring vulnerability required an institution that could survive beyond one grievance, one protest, and one person.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Barta recognized that protest had to become structure. A charter or union number was only a beginning; lasting leverage required participation, affiliation choices, recognition, written obligations, grievance machinery, and procedures capable of changing outcomes.

He also helped act on the principle that workers could build an institution inside an enterprise already equipped with buildings, capital, managers, lawyers, and control of paychecks. This was institution-building without ownership. He should never be described as investing in or owning Wilson’s or the union in a proprietary sense.

## Defining Character Traits

- **Institutional:** He chose to form an organization intended to endure rather than rely on one protest.
- **Collective in method:** He founded with Lewis Clark; the union’s effective capital was participation, not Barta’s ownership.
- **Strategic:** The founding act created a base from which later affiliation, independence, CIO chartering, recognition, agreements, and arbitration could develop.
- **Risk-accepting, with limits:** Organizing placed livelihoods within a conflict over employer authority. The record does not establish a specific retaliation against Barta personally, so personal consequences must not be invented.
- **Founder willing to be surpassed:** His achievement is strongest because later organizers and members carried the institution beyond him.
- **Persistent as an institutional inference:** The choice to create durable structure addresses recurring workplace conflict, but the available account does not document Barta’s complete organizing career.

## Greatest Risk

The immediate practical risk was income. Workers had to participate in an organization opposed by an employer that controlled access to jobs and pay. Collective leverage ultimately depended on the ability to withhold labor, which also withheld the wages families needed.

For Barta personally, employment, standing among coworkers, and exposure to management opposition were plausible stakes inherent in organizing, but the available record does not document a specific firing, demotion, threat, or family consequence. These must remain possible risks, not stated outcomes.

## Greatest Obstacle

The central obstacle was asymmetry of organized power. Wilson possessed capital, facilities, managers, lawyers, control over staffing and discipline, and the ability to withhold work. Workers were divided by task and individually dependent on paychecks. A union number alone could not create participation, compel recognition, win an agreement, or enforce one.

## Defining Decision

Barta’s defining decision was to form Federal Labor Union 18530 with Lewis Clark on August 23, 1933.

- **Options:** Endure or leave individually; join an isolated protest; attempt another short-lived organizing drive; or establish a formal local institution that could seek broader support and durable standing.
- **Choice:** Barta and Clark created the federal labor union.
- **Why it mattered:** It began converting individual vulnerability into collective structure and created an entity later workers could affiliate, reshape, and use.
- **Cost:** Time, organizing effort, internal persuasion, conflict with management authority, and potential exposure of workers’ incomes. Barta’s personal financial cost is unknown.
- **What it changed:** The organization later affiliated with the Amalgamated, became independent, received a CIO charter, gained recognition, entered agreements, and developed procedures capable in at least one later case of returning a worker to a job.

## Contradiction

Barta helped build power by creating an organization that could not remain his. Founder-centered authority would have defeated the purpose of collective institution-building. His legacy grew as later members, organizers, federations, negotiators, and arbitrators took over work he did not personally complete.

A second tension is that the union’s leverage depended on workers risking what they were trying to protect. Withholding labor could pressure the company, but a missing paycheck could immediately harm a household. Collective power did not remove vulnerability; it reorganized where and how vulnerability could be contested.

## Human Stakes

Packinghouse workers faced dangerous machinery, heat, a production sequence divided into specialized tasks, changing staffing, mechanized pace, discipline, and employer-controlled benefits. Their households depended on wages that organization and strikes could put at immediate risk.

Management decisions affected who worked and under what terms. Later records show worker photographs and names circulated to company executives and attorneys during conflict, demonstrating how public collective action could become an employment record. The union did not abolish danger, layoffs, hierarchy, or discipline, but it built procedures that could sometimes stand between one worker and unilateral company action.

## Emotional Arc

> **Ambition:** Give packinghouse workers durable collective standing against decisions no individual can meaningfully contest alone. → **Pressure:** The employer controls work, pay, staffing, machinery, benefits, and discipline; earlier resistance has not created durable local power; participation can expose the incomes workers need. → **Decision:** Form Federal Labor Union 18530 with Lewis Clark on August 23, 1933, choosing an institution over a one-time protest. → **Consequence:** Others affiliate, reshape, expand, and use the organization through recognition, contracts, strikes, grievances, and arbitration; leverage grows but continues to impose private costs. → **Legacy:** Barta leaves a counterweight that no longer needs to remain his, proving that labor organizing can be entrepreneurial institution-building without ownership or investment.

## Memorable Details

- August 23, 1933, and the number Federal Labor Union 18530.
- Barta founding beside Lewis Clark, not alone.
- A slaughter process described in 1915 as moving through nearly fifty men in about twenty minutes.
- Earlier workplace deaths associated with a cauldron, a shaft, and a boiling vat; full circumstances and corrective actions are unavailable.
- A reported 1903 walkout after a staffing reduction and a 1921 organizing effort followed by employer lockout.
- A company dispensary reported to handle as many as one thousand minor injuries in a month; the claim comes from a later company history.
- Affiliation with the Amalgamated in November 1933, independence in 1935, CIO charter in 1939, recognition associated with 1941, and a master agreement in 1943.
- Workers’ automobiles photographed during a 1959 parade, with names and sometimes departments recorded on company copies.
- Conflicting descriptions of the 1959–1960 dispute as walkout and lockout, with different stages for agreement and arbitration.
- A later arbitration sequence in which company action was reversed and a worker returned; the employee’s name and full award are unavailable.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids concentrated a large packing operation, specialized workers, recurring industrial risk, and employer authority in one place. That concentration made organization necessary and possible: workers shared an enterprise, a workplace identity, and grievances that could be converted into collective participation. The city’s existing labor history also supplied examples of both resistance and the limits of resistance without durable structure.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

Barta and Clark helped create a lasting vehicle for organized worker response at one major plant. The institution changed the city’s industrial culture by making bargaining, recognition, written agreements, grievances, and arbitration part of the contest over workplace authority. Its scope must remain specific: it did not represent every Cedar Rapids worker or reorganize every workplace.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

Barta left institution, procedure, collective memory, organizing experience, and a lesson: vulnerable people can build durable counterpower without owning the enterprise they seek to influence. He also left unresolved problems—collective action still cost wages, procedures could fail, and union power did not eliminate physical danger, layoffs, hierarchy, or unequal coverage.

The next chapter’s builder, Grant Wood, inherits the value of organized cooperation in a different form. Barta helped make individual workers durable through a chain of people, rules, and procedures; Wood would need a chain of assistants, industrial space, specifications, specialist fabricators, and overseas work to make one design physically possible.

## Chapter North Star

> This chapter is the story of a worker who answered concentrated company power by helping build an institution designed to become stronger than any one founder.

## Opening Possibilities

1. **A decision:** Open with Barta and Lewis Clark forming Federal Labor Union 18530 on August 23, 1933, choosing a durable organization rather than a single protest.
2. **A moment of pressure:** Open with the asymmetry at the factory gate: management can change staffing, machinery, pace, benefits, and access to work, while one employee can only object, leave, or endure.
3. **A vivid physical or business problem:** Open with production divided among nearly fifty men in a twenty-minute slaughter sequence, a system that coordinates labor precisely while leaving each worker fixed at one vulnerable point; present this as reported 1915 context, not a scene witnessed by Barta.

## Closing Handoff

Barta's institution became durable because coordinated people, rules, and procedures could carry a purpose farther than one worker acting alone. Grant Wood faces the next version of that problem: his design cannot become a memorial window through authorship alone. It must survive a production chain of assistants, borrowed industrial space, specialist fabrication, and work across an ocean without losing the idea at its center.
