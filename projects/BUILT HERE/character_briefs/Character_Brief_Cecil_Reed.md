# CHARACTER BRIEF

## Entrepreneur

Cecil A. Reed

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> Cecil Reed was driven by a sustained refusal to treat racial exclusion as an unchangeable condition of either commerce or law.

## Core Identity

Reed was a working businessman, contractor, motel operator, civil-rights advocate, and legislator who applied different tools to the same recurring problem: unequal access. The Iowa legislative biography records early work as a janitor and shoe shiner, a floor-sanding contracting business lasting twenty-three years, marriage to Evelyn in 1936, and later public service. The City history records Cecil and Evelyn as the entrepreneurs who opened the Sepia Motel on fifteen acres in 1953 after the couple had been denied lodging during a road trip because of race.

His identity is not adequately captured by “motel owner” or “politician.” His documented movement from building one reliable place to pursuing fairer rules reveals a practical institution builder who enlarged the arena when a private solution reached its limit.

## What They Wanted

With Evelyn, Reed wanted travelers—especially Black travelers who could not rely on equal treatment—to have dependable lodging open to all. In public life, he pursued broader access to housing and employment so equal treatment would not depend solely on finding an exceptional proprietor.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

The City's historic marker ties Motel Sepia to the Reeds' own denial of lodging during a road trip. That documented experience gives the pursuit a direct personal stake without requiring an invented scene or emotion. Reed then spent decades in contracting, civil-rights advocacy, organizational service, and legislation. His repeated movement from business response to public rules supports the conclusion that he regarded exclusion as a practical problem requiring continued action, not a single symbolic protest.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Reed saw exclusion as both a market failure and a power problem. A motel could convert denied demand into dependable service, but one proprietor could guarantee access only on one property. Wider change required institutions able to alter housing, employment, and public rules. He saw that enterprise and legislation were not separate identities; they were tools operating at different scales.

## Defining Character Traits

- **Resourceful:** He moved from early service work into a floor-sanding contracting business and, with Evelyn, into lodging.
- **Practical:** Motel Sepia offered an operating answer—a room—rather than only condemning discrimination in principle.
- **Persistent:** His record spans decades of business, advocacy, board service, and legislative work.
- **Civic-minded:** He entered the Iowa House and worked on civil-rights and housing concerns.
- **Courageous:** He attached his name, enterprise, and political career to challenging entrenched racial exclusion.
- **Institutional:** He moved from control of one business to participation in organizations and government capable of affecting many transactions.

Each trait is supported by repeated actions. The record does not justify assumptions about his private temperament.

## Greatest Risk

Reed risked business capital and occupancy at Motel Sepia, professional standing as a contractor, and political and reputational exposure as a Black civil-rights advocate in markets and institutions structured by discrimination. Evelyn shared the business risk. Their household depended on enterprises operating under unequal conditions. Exact financing, room counts, rates, occupancy, and losses remain unavailable.

## Greatest Obstacle

The central obstacle was racial exclusion embedded in private decisions, property transactions, public accommodations, and institutional power. Reed could open one inclusive motel but could not, as proprietor, compel another owner to rent a room, sell land, provide a job, or admit a patron.

## Defining Decision

Reed's defining sequence contains one decision in two stages. After a documented lodging denial, Cecil and Evelyn could continue navigating an unreliable travel market or create a dependable alternative. They opened Motel Sepia in 1953 on fifteen acres along Bertram and Mount Vernon roads, then the Lincoln Highway, and opened it to all.

When private ownership revealed its limit, Reed could remain solely a proprietor and advocate or enter government. He served as a Republican in Iowa's Sixty-second General Assembly in 1967–1968 and was connected to fair-housing work. The move cost time and exposed his proposals and reputation to political opposition. It changed his field of action from one doorway to the rules governing many doors.

## Contradiction

Reed's supported tension is **entrepreneurial workaround versus structural reform**. Motel Sepia succeeded as a response precisely because the surrounding market failed Black travelers; the business's necessity demonstrated the inadequacy of solving discrimination one customer at a time. Reed built an alternative and then worked toward a world in which such a racial workaround would not be necessary.

## Human Stakes

Black travelers risked refusal, delay, danger, and humiliation when lodging could not be assumed. Evelyn shared ownership and operating responsibility. Reed's contracting and motel enterprises affected household livelihood. Families seeking housing and workers seeking opportunity depended on rules broader than one business. Failure would leave travelers and residents dependent on the same discriminatory gatekeepers; success could make access more dependable, first at one property and then in law.

## Emotional Arc

> **Ambition:** Create dependable access where racial exclusion made ordinary transactions uncertain → **Pressure:** Confront a lodging and property market in which one proprietor's choice could close a door and one inclusive motel could reach only its own guests → **Decision:** Build Motel Sepia with Evelyn, then enter advocacy and state government to address the rules beyond the property → **Consequence:** Travelers gain an inclusive lodging option while Reed's public work expands the contest into housing and employment policy → **Legacy:** His business and legislative records survive together, showing a builder who first opened another door and then challenged the lock.

## Memorable Details

- Born October 23, 1913, in Collinsville, Illinois; later moved with his family to Cedar Rapids.
- Worked as a janitor and shoe shiner before becoming a contractor.
- Married Evelyn Ruth Reed in 1936.
- Operated a floor-sanding contracting business in Cedar Rapids for twenty-three years.
- Cecil and Evelyn were denied lodging during a road trip because of race, according to the City marker.
- Motel Sepia opened in 1953 on fifteen acres along Bertram Road and Mount Vernon Road, then the Lincoln Highway.
- The motel was open to all.
- African American travel guides in Reed's papers place dependable lodging within a national information network.
- Reed served in the Iowa House in 1967 and 1968.
- His papers place motel records beside photographs, speeches, civil-rights work, and public service.
- The property was sold to Cedar Rapids in 1964.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids gave the Reeds a home, a contracting market, land along a major road, Black churches and civic organizations, the NAACP and other advocates, customers moving through the region, and eventually routes into formal public service. The city's exclusions also defined the problem; Cedar Rapids was not merely supportive context but one of the places where access had to be built and contested.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

The Reeds gave the city an inclusive lodging business and a visible demonstration that exclusion was neither natural nor commercially unavoidable. Cecil gave Cedar Rapids contracting work, advocacy, board service, legislative representation, and a preserved archive connecting business decisions to civil rights.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

Reed left an institutional lesson: when a building serves a mission larger than its walls, access must be protected by people, records, organizations, and public authority. Gail Naughton inherits that problem when floodwater makes the National Czech & Slovak Museum's physical doorway unusable and she must preserve access to the institution by separating mission from place.

## Chapter North Star

> This chapter is the story of a businessman who answered exclusion by opening a door he could control, then entered government to challenge who controlled all the others.

## Opening Possibilities

1. **A decision:** Open with Cecil and Evelyn choosing to create Motel Sepia after their documented denial of lodging, carefully avoiding any invented road-trip scene or dialogue.
2. **A moment of pressure:** Open with Reed confronting the limit of his own property: a guest can enter Motel Sepia, but a family seeking a home still faces neighbors, institutions, votes, and rules outside his control.
3. **A vivid physical or business problem:** Open with a Black traveler consulting a documented travel guide not for amenities but to determine whether arrival at a listed motel will end in a room rather than refusal; keep the traveler generic and do not invent an individual scene.

## Closing Handoff

Reed demonstrated that access can begin with one reliable building but cannot depend on a building alone. When the Cedar River enters the National Czech & Slovak Museum in 2008, Gail Naughton must preserve access by carrying the institution's mission, collections, staff work, and public programs beyond walls that can no longer function.
