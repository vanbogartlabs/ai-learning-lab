# CHARACTER BRIEF

## Entrepreneur

Howard Hall

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> Howard Hall was driven by the ambition to reorganize the customer’s whole job, turning a portable aggregate plant into the foundation of a broader road-machinery capability.

## Core Identity

Hall was a system builder and market framer. With John Jay, he controlled the Cedar Rapids concern that became Iowa Steel & Iron Works, giving the partnership a base in casting and fabrication. In 1923 they incorporated Iowa Manufacturing Company from or around the former Bertschey Engineering Company and recruited an engineer and team to pursue portable crushing equipment.

Hall should not be written as a lone inventor. The engineer’s surname appears as Frazee in one account and Franzee in another; the division of invention and exact year remain unsettled. Hall’s distinct role is clearer at the business level: he organized a company around integrating crushing, conveying, and screening so heavy processing could follow road and quarry work. Later, he also helped frame Cedar Rapids itself as the “road machinery capital of the world.”

## What They Wanted

Hall wanted road builders and quarry operators to process aggregate closer to changing work sites rather than remain bound to cumbersome stationary plants. The concrete product goal was a portable outfit combining crushing, screening, and conveying.

The larger commercial goal was to build a company capability around the customer’s complete workflow—manufacture, transport, setup, operation, maintenance, and service—so one initial product concept could support later asphalt and paving markets.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

**Interpretation, strongly supported by decisions:** Hall had access to foundry capability but did not treat existing fabrication as sufficient. He helped establish a separate machinery company, recruited technical expertise, and accepted responsibility for integrating several failure-prone functions. This pattern indicates that the unresolved customer problem—not merely selling more iron—kept drawing investment and organizational attention.

No private statement establishes obsession or personal need. The supported reason he persisted is commercial and practical: roads and quarries moved, fixed plants imposed cost and delay, and solving more of that problem could create a defensible market.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Hall saw the customer’s entire moving job as the product. The opportunity was not simply a better crusher; it was coordinated mobility. If crushing, screening, and conveying could travel and work together, geography would shift from obstacle to market.

He also saw that a first product could create a reusable capability. Hot-mix asphalt plants and later paving equipment extended the same logic toward more stages of road construction. Finally, his “road machinery capital” phrase recognized that Cedar Rapids’s cluster of related manufacturers could itself become market positioning, though the world-capital ranking is not supported by a common comparative measure.

## Defining Character Traits

- **System-minded:** He treated crushing, screening, conveying, transport, setup, and service as one customer problem.
- **Commercially inventive:** His supported innovation was the business organization around portability, not an unsupported lone-inventor claim.
- **Collaborative by documented action:** He worked with John Jay, recruited an engineer and team, and relied on foundry and production work performed by many specialists.
- **Ambitious:** He moved beyond a foundry catalog to establish a machinery company and pursue an integrated product.
- **Persuasive and category-making:** The phrase “road machinery capital of the world” positioned Cedar Rapids as an equipment center. Its comparative supremacy remains unproven.
- **Forward-positioned:** The capability established before the interstate era later fit expanding highway and paving markets.

## Greatest Risk

Hall risked capital and reputation on an integrated machine whose components had to survive transport and function together in uncontrolled field conditions. Weight, dimensions, erection, power, maintenance, and repair all had to work at sites chosen for rock or road construction rather than factory convenience.

Integration magnified failure: if one component stopped, several stages could stop together. The company also faced uncertain customer savings, unpreserved early performance measures, competing equipment makers, and the need to support machines after shipment.

## Greatest Obstacle

The central obstacle was making heavy industrial processing genuinely mobile. A “portable” label was worthless unless the crusher, screen, conveyor, power arrangements, and structure could be transported, erected, run, maintained, and repaired as a system under field conditions.

## Defining Decision

Hall’s defining decision was to establish Iowa Manufacturing around an integrated portable aggregate system rather than merely add another stationary component to Iowa Steel’s output.

- **Options:** Continue selling foundry and fabrication products; manufacture separate stationary crushing components; or form a machinery company around a coordinated portable outfit.
- **Choice:** Hall and Jay incorporated Iowa Manufacturing in 1923, brought in technical help, and pursued the “One Piece Outfit” or early portable crusher concept.
- **Why it mattered:** It made the customer’s entire task—not the weight of iron sold—the unit of value.
- **Cost:** A new organization, engineering and production effort, capital, field-performance exposure, and responsibility for multiple linked functions. Exact financial cost is unavailable.
- **What it changed:** Portability became the base capability from which the company expanded toward asphalt and paving equipment and participated in Cedar Rapids’s road-machinery cluster.

## Contradiction

Hall is remembered through a singular product and a strong personal name, but the product was irreducibly collective. Engineers, draftspeople, patternmakers, foundry workers, machinists, welders, assemblers, inspectors, painters, buyers, carriers, salespeople, and service technicians all made portability real.

Another supported tension is that Hall used a superlative civic slogan to market a real industrial concentration whose precise comparative rank cannot be proved. The positioning was entrepreneurial; it was not audited evidence of world supremacy.

## Human Stakes

Customers depended on the outfit working far from the factory. A breakdown could halt several linked stages and idle crews at a road or quarry site. Iowa Manufacturing employees depended on the company translating a promising concept into repeatable orders, manufacturable designs, and serviceable machines.

Cedar Rapids workers across foundries and machinery plants gained employment and transferable technical skill. Public road programs expanded opportunity but did not guarantee orders; every machine still had to be specified, financed, produced, inspected, delivered, and supported.

## Emotional Arc

> **Ambition:** Use Cedar Rapids foundry skill to solve the full aggregate-processing problem for work that keeps changing location. → **Pressure:** Heavy components must travel, survive, be erected, and function together, while early performance and customer savings remain uncertain. → **Decision:** Form Iowa Manufacturing around an integrated portable outfit and recruit technical collaborators rather than remain a component supplier. → **Consequence:** Portability becomes a compounding capability, expanding toward asphalt and paving equipment and making Hall answerable for more of the customer’s workflow. → **Legacy:** Hall leaves both a durable industrial platform and a market identity for Cedar Rapids as a concentrated road-machinery center, with the “world capital” superlative explicitly unproven.

## Memorable Details

- Iowa Steel & Iron Works producing beams, boilers, pulleys, and architectural ironwork.
- Iowa Manufacturing’s 1923 incorporation from or around Bertschey Engineering Company.
- The engineer remembered as Guy Frazee or Guy Franzee; spelling and division of invention remain unsettled.
- The name “One Piece Outfit.”
- Crusher, screen, and conveyor required to travel and function together.
- Work sites chosen by the location of rock and roads, not factory convenience.
- Hot-mix asphalt plants dated by a successor history to 1929.
- The Army-Navy E award associated in later accounts with wartime production in 1944.
- Hall’s phrase “road machinery capital of the world,” preserved in a later government-sponsored history but not supported by a common ranking table.
- A later report of 108 foreign engineers visiting in 1947; their itinerary is not preserved.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids gave Hall foundry and fabrication capability, engineers and skilled production workers, rail and carrier access, and a dense network of adjacent machinery companies solving road, quarry, earthmoving, lifting, spreading, and transport problems. That industrial concentration widened the available technical vocabulary and made a specialized machinery company plausible.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

Hall helped create Iowa Manufacturing’s portable aggregate-machinery platform and contributed to a broader equipment economy. His category-making phrase gave the city a market identity that could speak to recruits, customers, and visitors. He did not create the cluster alone, but he joined existing capabilities to a business model that increased Cedar Rapids’s visibility in construction technology.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

Hall left a customer-centered method: define the whole job, integrate around it, and let the first solution create a capability that can compound as markets change. He also left engineering skill, manufacturing infrastructure, service knowledge, and a visible machinery cluster.

The next builder can use that lesson beyond heavy equipment. Paul Lattner would also discover that selling a machine was only the beginning; a customer needed an entire support ecology around automobile ownership.

## Chapter North Star

> This chapter is the story of a foundry owner who stopped selling pieces of machinery and built a company around the moving problem his customers actually had.

## Opening Possibilities

1. **A decision:** Open with Hall and Jay choosing in 1923 to organize Iowa Manufacturing around portable aggregate equipment rather than leave the idea inside a foundry catalog.
2. **A moment of pressure:** Open at the commercial threshold before a “portable” outfit has earned the word—every linked component must survive transport, setup, operation, and repair before a customer’s job can continue.
3. **A vivid physical or business problem:** Open with a road or quarry project moving beyond the practical reach of a stationary crushing plant, requiring rock to be crushed, screened, and conveyed where the work is; do not invent a specific customer or scene.

## Closing Handoff

Hall proved that a machine becomes more valuable when a builder organizes around the customer’s whole job. Paul Lattner would face the same truth at street scale. An automobile in a showroom could attract attention, but without fuel or charging, tires, parts, repairs, storage, and resale, the sale would collapse under the cost of ownership. Hall’s integrated plant hands the story to a dealer building the ecology around another unfamiliar machine.
