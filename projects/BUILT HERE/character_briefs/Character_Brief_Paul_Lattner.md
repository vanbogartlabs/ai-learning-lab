# CHARACTER BRIEF

## Entrepreneur

Paul Lattner

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> Paul Lattner was driven by the ambition to turn public curiosity about automobiles into a durable local market capable of supporting the machine after the sale.

## Core Identity

Lattner was an early market builder rather than merely a vehicle seller. With his brother Joe, he commissioned purpose-built premises for Cedar Rapids Auto Company in 1909, before the surviving record can establish demand sufficient to justify the fixed space. Paul later promoted the economics of repair, occupied a building in Automobile Row, and offered the Milburn Light Electric while propulsion and support systems were still unsettled.

His supported identity rests on willingness to confront the automobile’s unfinished commercial form. No balance sheet establishes his wealth or even the full outcome of his firms. His significance lies in putting space, salesmanship, service logic, and multiple technologies behind an unfamiliar durable product.

## What They Wanted

Lattner wanted curiosity to become ownership and ownership to generate a continuing, supportable commercial relationship. The concrete goal was not just to deliver new vehicles. It was to sustain a business around display, explanation, repair economics, parts and battery needs, service, and eventually the automobile’s return to the market.

He pursued a local market sturdy enough that the machine could remain usable after novelty wore off.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

**Interpretation, supported by repeated commitments:** Each unresolved ownership problem created both an objection to the automobile and a reason for Lattner’s business to exist. He committed a building, participated in public promotion, argued about continuing repair cost, moved into Automobile Row, and offered an electric model while the market was still choosing technologies.

No personal statement establishes obsession or motive. The documented persistence supports a practical explanation: the first sale exposed recurring needs, and meeting those needs could turn a one-time novelty into continuing commerce.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Lattner saw that a technology becomes a market only when businesses build the system around it. Automobiles required explanation before purchase and fuel or electricity, tires, batteries, parts, repairs, storage, roads, and resale after purchase.

He also acted within a commercial geography that made neighboring specialists valuable. Automobile Row’s showrooms, vehicular bays, warehouses, stations, lots, and visible street frontage formed a shared ecology no one proprietor controlled. The insight is inferred from his documented choices; no surviving quotation shows him stating it in those terms.

## Defining Character Traits

- **Early-committing:** Paul and Joe commissioned a dedicated building in 1909 before surviving sales history could justify it.
- **Market-making:** Paul participated in the work of explaining and promoting an unfamiliar product rather than waiting for normalized demand.
- **Customer-economics focused:** His 1915 claim emphasized savings in repair expense, revealing attention to continuing ownership cost. It was sales language, not verified performance.
- **Technologically open:** His company advertised the Milburn Light Electric in 1919 while gasoline had not become the only imaginable propulsion system.
- **Adaptive in location and format:** He moved from the Fourth Avenue premises into Automobile Row, where service businesses and street access supported the trade.
- **Risk-tolerant, not proven reckless:** Fixed premises and uncertain technology support measured commercial risk, not claims about personality outside business.

## Greatest Risk

Lattner risked fixed property and business reputation before automobile demand, propulsion, service networks, and operating economics were settled. Every breakdown or unavailable part could undermine confidence in both the vehicle and the dealer. A purpose-built building could outlast a franchise or fail to attract enough sales and service work.

Geography added risk. Public highway routing could deliver traffic to Automobile Row or move advantage away without moving the building. The record does not preserve project cost, personal guarantees, or a balance sheet, so personal financial stakes cannot be quantified.

## Greatest Obstacle

The greatest obstacle was the incomplete system around automobile ownership. A vehicle could be physically present while the market lacked reliable fuel or charging, mechanics, batteries, tires, replacement parts, storage, repair confidence, road access, financing, or resale channels. Lattner had to sell trust in that surrounding system as well as trust in the machine.

## Defining Decision

Lattner’s defining decision was the 1909 commitment, with Joe, to commission a purpose-built Cedar Rapids Auto Company building at 212–216 Fourth Avenue.

- **Options:** Sell from temporary or adapted premises; wait for demand and service infrastructure to mature; or place fixed space behind the emerging trade.
- **Choice:** They commissioned the dedicated building.
- **Why it mattered:** The premises gave physical form to a market that was not yet complete and made the brothers responsible for more than occasional vehicle sales.
- **Cost:** Building, occupancy, inventory, staffing, and reputational exposure; the project’s price and final cost are not preserved.
- **What it changed:** It established an early commercial base from which Paul could promote automobiles, address ownership economics, and participate in the district and service ecology that followed.

## Contradiction

Lattner sold freedom in the showroom while the business depended on recurring trouble behind it. Broad windows and public shows presented novelty and mobility; service bays, parts, batteries, repairs, and eventual resale acknowledged cost, breakdown, and depreciation.

He also acted early without needing a defensible claim to being first. Later accounts call the Lattners the founders of Cedar Rapids’s first dealership in 1902, but contemporary evidence leaves that distinction contested. His stronger legacy is that he stayed early enough to face what did not yet work.

## Human Stakes

Paul and Joe bore the direct commercial exposure of fixed premises and uncertain demand. Customers risked substantial money on machines whose reliability and support could not be taken for granted. Mechanics, attendants, parts handlers, clerks, bookkeepers, cleaners, drivers, and lot workers depended on the market producing recurring work.

A failed support system could leave an owner with an unusable vehicle and leave employees without a durable trade. A functioning system could create mobility, jobs, technical skill, and many new small-business opportunities, while also beginning the physical remaking of city blocks around cars.

## Emotional Arc

> **Ambition:** Convert the unfamiliar automobile from spectacle into a usable, supportable product for Cedar Rapids customers. → **Pressure:** Demand is unproven, rival first claims abound, every breakdown threatens trust, and gasoline and electric systems remain unsettled. → **Decision:** Put fixed premises behind the trade in 1909 and continue building confidence through promotion, ownership-cost arguments, and participation in Automobile Row. → **Consequence:** The automobile becomes the center of a growing ecology of showrooms, service bays, batteries, tires, parts, stations, roads, and used inventory that no one dealer controls. → **Legacy:** Lattner helps establish that every machine sold creates future work—and that the enduring market is the support system after the sale, not the novelty in the window.

## Memorable Details

- The commissioned 1909 building at 212–216 Fourth Avenue.
- Eight automobile-related concerns in the 1909 city directory, six described as dealers.
- The contested claim that the Lattners founded the first dealership in 1902.
- A March 1910 automobile show used to display and compare unfamiliar machines.
- Paul’s 1915 phrase about “the savings in repair expense,” preserved as a sales claim rather than verified result.
- P. M. Lattner Sales Company at 616 Second Avenue SE from 1917 through 1919.
- A 1919 advertisement for the Milburn Light Electric.
- Showroom glass in front and service bays behind.
- Broad windows, open spans, vehicular bays, parts warehouses, alleys, lots, and parking.
- The Lincoln Highway passing near or through Automobile Row on First Avenue and later Second Avenue.
- Addresses surviving successive automobile businesses after individual proprietors departed.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids gave Lattner a growing customer base, rail delivery, streets and bridges, visible commercial frontage, an emerging Lincoln Highway connection, and a developing cluster of dealers, garages, battery firms, tire shops, parts sellers, and service stations. The concentration let no single dealer solve every problem, but it made the whole ownership proposition more credible.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

Lattner helped turn automobile commerce from novelty into a local operating ecosystem. His fixed premises, public promotion, repair-cost selling, electric-car offer, and place in Automobile Row contributed to new work, specialized businesses, and reusable commercial architecture. The trade also helped remake city blocks around showrooms, service areas, warehouses, stations, lots, and parking.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

He left a local market, physical spaces, technical trades, and a durable lesson: a product becomes viable when builders support its entire life after purchase. He also left a question of power. The system around machinery organized tasks and employment, but workers did not necessarily control the terms. Milo Barta would treat that imbalance as an institution-building problem.

## Chapter North Star

> This chapter is the story of an early dealer who risked fixed space and reputation to build everything an unfamiliar machine needed after the customer said yes.

## Closing Handoff

Lattner helped build the commercial system around the machine. Milo Barta would confront the authority inside industrial systems themselves. Factories had organized machinery, pace, staffing, benefits, discipline, and pay with precision; individual workers remained vulnerable. Lattner’s ecology of recurring work hands the story to a labor organizer trying to give workers an institution as durable as the enterprises employing them.
