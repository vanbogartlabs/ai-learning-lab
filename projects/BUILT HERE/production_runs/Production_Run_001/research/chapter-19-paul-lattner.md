# Research Record — Chapter 19: Paul Lattner

## Scope and Evidence Posture

This record supports approved Production Run 001 Chapter 19, **Automobile Row**. Chapter 19 is an explicit chronology braid: it rewinds from Ronk's 1926 relocation to Paul Lattner's 1909 automobile-market commitment and follows selected consequences into the 1920s. Repository materials are evidence summaries; the underlying building commission, directories, advertisements, permits, and financial records were not inspected.

## Entrepreneur, Agency, and Chapter Function

- **Protagonist:** Paul M. Lattner, with his brother Joe's joint role preserved.
- **Person-level agency:** Paul and Joe commissioned purpose-built premises for Cedar Rapids Auto Company in 1909; Paul later promoted repair economics, occupied Automobile Row premises, and advertised the Milburn Light Electric.
- **Chapter function:** Show an early dealer committing fixed space and operating support to an unfamiliar durable product before the ownership ecosystem matured.
- **Center of gravity:** The 1909 premises decision, not a disputed claim to Cedar Rapids's first automobile or dealership.

## Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **Paul Lattner, acting with Joe, chose in 1909 to commission a dedicated Cedar Rapids Auto Company building at 212–216 Fourth Avenue before surviving evidence could establish demand sufficient to support the fixed premises and service obligations.**

### Personally accepted responsibility and risk

The brothers placed a business they operated behind dedicated premises and thereby accepted building/occupancy, inventory, staffing, service, and reputation exposure. Every unsupported vehicle could return as a dealer problem. Paul later put his own sales reputation behind repair-cost claims and an electric model while technology and support remained unsettled. Project cost, title, financing, lease terms, and guarantees are unknown.

### Safer alternative

They could have sold from temporary or adapted premises, represented vehicles opportunistically, or waited for demand, roads, fuel/charging, parts, mechanics, finance, and resale channels to mature. The record does not preserve their deliberation.

## Supported Interpretation

Lattner's entrepreneurial insight can be stated as an interpretation from repeated actions: an automobile became a market only when local businesses supported its life after sale. His documented actions span dedicated premises, public promotion, operating-cost argument, district participation, and an electric-car offer. No source records him stating the full “ecosystem” thesis.

Automobile Row was a distributed commercial system no one dealer controlled. Lattner contributed to it but did not create every garage, tire shop, battery concern, station, route, financing source, or used-car lot.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids gave Lattner

The city supplied a growing customer base, rail delivery, streets and bridges, visible commercial frontage, Lincoln Highway proximity, and neighboring dealers and specialists. Public routing could help or bypass a fixed address; city infrastructure enlarged possibility without guaranteeing sales.

### What Lattner gave Cedar Rapids

He placed fixed premises, promotion, service logic, and technological openness behind the young trade. His enterprise contributed to reusable automobile architecture and specialized work for mechanics, attendants, parts handlers, clerks, bookkeepers, cleaners, and drivers. Broader district change cannot be credited to him alone.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Early versus first:** Lattner's strong story does not depend on winning a contested first-dealership claim.
2. **Showroom promise versus service trouble:** Sales depended on recurring repair, parts, battery, tire, and resale problems.
3. **Gasoline outcome versus electric possibility:** The 1919 Milburn offer preserves technological uncertainty.
4. **Private premises versus public route:** Highway routing could shift commercial advantage without moving the building.
5. **Named brothers versus Paul's chapter:** Joe's role in the 1909 commission cannot be erased even though Paul carries the chapter.
6. **Address continuity versus firm continuity:** Buildings outlived individual dealers and franchises.

## Human Stakes and Consequence

The Lattners exposed their business to fixed costs and uncertain demand. Customers risked substantial money on machines whose support could fail. Workers' employment depended on enough vehicles producing recurring sales and service work. A functioning ecology widened personal mobility and created specialized enterprise while beginning to reshape city blocks around automobiles.

## Chapter Necessity

Chapter 19 is necessary because it changes the scale of the argument from manufacturing machinery to **building the market around a machine after sale**. It also supplies the labor transition: a growing service and industrial economy organizes recurring work but does not determine who has authority over its terms.

## Inheritance and Transition to Chapter 20

Lattner leaves premises, market routines, technical trades, and a durable support ecology. Milo Barta does not inherit a personal organization from Lattner. He encounters an industrial city in which firms have organized capital, machinery, service, staffing, and discipline. Chapter 20 asks whether workers can build an institution of comparable durability to contest employer authority.
