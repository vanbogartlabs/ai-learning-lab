## Chapter Function and Qualified Production Center

- **Frozen function:** Show entrepreneurial institution-building without ownership by tracing how workers created durable countervailing capacity inside industrial employment.
- **Protagonist:** Milo Barta, with Lewis Clark's co-founding role mandatory.
- **Classification:** Institutional entrepreneur, in a bounded and nonproprietary sense.
- **Strongest supportable action:** Barta and Clark participated in the named and dated co-founding act that formed Federal Labor Union 18530 at Wilson's Cedar Rapids packinghouse on August 23, 1933.
- **Occupation support:** University of Iowa archival catalog metadata identifies Barta as a packinghouse worker and catalogs his 1977 interview, audio, and transcript.
- **Qualification:** The public catalog does not expose the interview content. Barta's employment status on the founding date, knowingly accepted livelihood risk, employer response, and personal consequence remain unknown.

## Agency Classification and Strongest Supported Action

Barta's co-founding act supports a real institutional decision: he helped create a durable organization with Clark rather than remaining only an unnamed participant in a general labor movement. His packinghouse-worker classification materially connects him to the labor world the institution addressed.

That evidence does not complete a personal-risk biography. It does not show his exact employer or job on August 23, 1933; whether he expected retaliation; whether Wilson's management identified him; or whether he experienced firing, demotion, threats, surveillance, blacklisting, wage loss, lost standing, or household consequences.

Barta can therefore be centered as a co-founder whose documented action built institutional capacity. He cannot be narrated as a worker proven to have knowingly wagered his own Wilson livelihood unless later evidence establishes that claim.

## Defining Institutional Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **Milo Barta, acting with Lewis Clark, co-founded Federal Labor Union 18530 at Wilson's Cedar Rapids packinghouse on August 23, 1933, helping create a durable vehicle for collective worker authority.**

### Agency and responsibility qualification

The co-founding act is attributable and consequential at institution level. It supports Barta's participation in creating the organization, not sole authorship of its rules, affiliations, later leadership, bargaining, strikes, grievances, or arbitration. Clark and later members retain their own agency.

### Personal-risk qualification

Barta's classification as a packinghouse worker makes employment and livelihood exposure structurally plausible. Packinghouse workers depended on work and pay within an employer-controlled setting, and becoming a named founder could create visibility. But the inspected evidence does not show what Barta himself knowingly accepted, feared, lost, or endured. Structural exposure must remain structural; realized harm and subjective acceptance remain unknown.

### Structural safer alternatives

A worker might avoid a named founding role, support organization privately, rely on individual complaint, wait for others, leave for other work, or participate in a temporary protest. No source in the bounded packet records Barta weighing those options. They may be described only as analytical alternatives to public co-founding, not as his private deliberation.

## Supported Interpretation and Responsible Narrative Spine

The chapter can responsibly narrate the creation of countervailing authority inside an industrial city. Barta and Clark's co-founding act is the hinge: named workers helped convert dispersed vulnerability into a durable institution. Barta's packinghouse-worker status keeps the act connected to worker experience rather than treating him as an outside owner or investor.

The story should then widen from the founders to the institution's later life. Affiliation changes, recognition, agreements, grievances, strikes, and arbitration demonstrate that the organization could outgrow any one founder. They must remain collective and later achievements unless a specific record attributes a later act to Barta.

Risk should be narrated in two layers. At the structural layer, packinghouse workers depended on employer-controlled jobs and pay, and organization could expose livelihood and standing. At the person-specific layer, the packet does not establish what Barta knowingly risked or what happened to him. The chapter's strongest consequence is institutional capacity, not a documented personal sacrifice or triumph.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids offered Barta and Clark

Cedar Rapids concentrated packinghouse employment, specialized labor, worker networks, and employer authority in one place. That concentration made collective organization both possible and structurally consequential. It does not prove city sponsorship or Barta's personal employment terms.

### What Barta, Clark, and later workers returned

Barta and Clark helped create a local vehicle through which workers could build collective authority. Later members and organizers developed bargaining, agreements, grievances, strikes, and arbitration. The exchange belongs to a distributed institution, not to Barta alone.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Institutional entrepreneur versus owner:** Barta helped build an organization without proprietary capital or ownership of Wilson's.
2. **Barta versus Clark:** The founding act is shared.
3. **Founders versus later members:** Institutional durability depends on later collective agency.
4. **Packinghouse-worker status versus date-specific employment:** The catalog identifies occupation, not the exact 1933 employer relationship.
5. **Structural exposure versus personal risk:** Livelihood dependence is plausible; Barta-specific knowing acceptance and consequence remain unknown.
6. **Institutional capacity versus guaranteed protection:** Collective procedures could alter authority without ensuring victory or safety.

## Human Stakes and Consequence

Workers depended on paychecks while employer-controlled access to work made organization consequential. A durable union could create rules, representation, collective leverage, and procedures between an individual worker and unilateral company action. Management faced coordinated worker authority and possible interruption. The packet supports those institutional stakes but not a specific loss, retaliation, or household consequence for Barta.

## Chapter Necessity

Chapter 20 expands the book's definition of building. It shows that entrepreneurial capacity can be institutional and collective, created by people who do not own the industrial enterprise whose authority they contest. Without this chapter, industrial growth would be narrated primarily through owners and managers, omitting the durable authority workers built within it.
