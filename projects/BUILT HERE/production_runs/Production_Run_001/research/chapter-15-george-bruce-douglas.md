## Qualified Interpretations

- Industrial scale concentrated output and exposure. This does not establish negligence, foreknowledge, or a specific preventable cause.
- The blast crossed the factory boundary through deaths, injuries, household disruption, property damage, public relief, and economic uncertainty.
- Sale preserved the possibility of industrial continuity while ending Douglas family control. It did not restore lives, homes, wages, or trust.
- Douglas's persistence changed form from ownership and expansion to relinquishment. This is an interpretation from outcome, not a claim about his private motive.
- The city's desire to retain industry existed beside, not in place of, obligations to affected people.

## Courage Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> After the 1919 catastrophe, George Bruce Douglas participated in the Douglas family's decision to sell the ruined works to Penick & Ford, allowing industrial use to continue without continued Douglas control.

- **Uncertainty:** Cause, casualty counts, losses, claims, asset condition, buyer capability, future employment, transaction value, and public expectations.
- **Supported risk and cost:** Loss of company identity and control; uncertain value of damaged assets; reputational and civic exposure; unresolved obligations to workers and neighbors.
- **Analytical safer alternative:** Retain the site without immediate commitment, abandon industrial use, or attempt Douglas-controlled rebuilding. The detailed option set and deliberations are not preserved, so these are analytical comparisons.
- **Why distinct:** Stuart accepted reduced control to enlarge a rebuilt enterprise; Douglas relinquished control after catastrophic destruction and human loss. Penick then accepted the separate reconstruction wager.
