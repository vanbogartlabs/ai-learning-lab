# Research Record — Chapter 18: George T. Ronk

## Scope and Evidence Posture

This record supports approved Production Run 001 Chapter 18, **Speeder, Link-Belt, and the Building Boom**. The repository lacks a standalone Ronk Character Brief and underlying corporate, property, financing, payroll, relocation, and customer records. Later Link-Belt, labor, and closure history may show the consequence of Ronk's decision but must not be attributed to his continuing authority.

## Entrepreneur, Agency, and Chapter Function

- **Protagonist:** George T. Ronk.
- **Person-level agency:** Ronk led the young Speeder Machinery concern that had begun in Leon and moved to Fairfield; in 1926 he chose another relocation, this time to Cedar Rapids, where the enterprise entered temporary quarters and then a permanent plant.
- **Chapter function:** Show an entrepreneur risking continuity to move and enlarge an operating machinery company before workforce transfer, restart, building cost, and customer demand were assured.
- **Center of gravity:** Ronk's 1926 relocation. Link-Belt combination, postwar expansion, organized labor, outside control, and closure are consequences and limits, not later Ronk decisions unless direct evidence proves otherwise.

## Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **Ronk chose in 1926 to move Speeder from a small Fairfield operation to Cedar Rapids and enlarge it into a plant-scale machinery business before equipment, workers, production continuity, and sufficient orders were secure.**

### Personally accepted responsibility and risk

As the leader moving an operating young company for the second time, Ronk accepted responsibility for continuity: machinery had to move or be replaced, workers had to follow or be recruited, production had to restart, and customers had to continue buying while the company changed its physical base. Reported plant spending and transfer counts indicate company exposure, but the record does not establish Ronk's equity, personal guarantee, cash contribution, salary, or loss.

### Safer alternative

The safer structural alternative was to remain in Fairfield, expand incrementally, lease additional space, or delay relocation until orders and a Cedar Rapids workforce justified a larger plant. No surviving source records Ronk's own option analysis.

## Supported Interpretation

Ronk's relocation converted a shop-sized enterprise into a Cedar Rapids industrial platform worth later combination with a broader product line. The move was entrepreneurial because it placed continuity at risk before scale was proved. It was not courageous merely because the company later grew.

The later arc clarifies the double edge of Ronk's decision: capacity can capture demand, but fixed plant and outside control deepen vulnerability when demand changes. Ronk cannot be credited with David Lehti's postwar capacity choice, the Link-Belt transaction's exact legal structure, FMC policy, or the 1985 closure.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids gave Ronk

The city offered rail connections, power, metalworking labor, builders, a machinery cluster, room, and civic recruitment. Temporary use of a streetcar barn reduced the gap between arrival and permanent capacity. These conditions created an opening; none guaranteed orders or retained workers.

### What Ronk gave Cedar Rapids

Ronk brought an operating machinery concern, products, some workers, and a commitment to restart and enlarge. The resulting enterprise added employment, technical skill, and a complementary excavating-equipment line to the city's machinery economy. Later scale was collective and corporate, not Ronk's sole creation.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Projection versus result:** The announced 150 families is not the reported actual transfer of about 100 workers.
2. **Temporary start versus permanent bet:** The streetcar barn reduced immediate commitment while the permanent plant increased it.
3. **Ronk's company versus later management:** Postwar expansions belong to later executives and organizations.
4. **Combination terminology:** Purchase, merger, combination, and subsidiary formation are not interchangeable without transaction records.
5. **Growth versus control:** Cedar Rapids gained scale as authority later moved outside the city.
6. **Capacity versus exposure:** The plant that captured orders became fixed cost when the market contracted.

## Human Stakes and Consequence

Workers faced whether to move households, leave Speeder, or trust a new location. Ronk and the company risked production interruption and customer confidence. Cedar Rapids assumed consequences associated with a larger industrial employer, while later employees bore contraction and closure after decision authority had moved beyond the founder's local enterprise.

## Chapter Necessity

Chapter 18 is necessary because it tests a different decision from Hall's integration: **moving a living company to obtain scale**. It also introduces the cost of capacity and the transition from locally controlled growth to later external control. Removing it would collapse relocation, workforce continuity, plant formation, combination, and long-run capacity exposure into generic machinery history.

## Inheritance and Chronology Reset to Chapter 19

Ronk's company helped leave roads-and-construction machinery, service trades, skilled labor, and an enlarged mobility economy. Chapter 19 must explicitly rewind from the 1926 relocation and later building boom to Paul Lattner's 1909 automobile-market commitment. The handoff is thematic and systemic—machines and roads create mobility demand—not personal contact or linear chronology.
