# Research Record — Chapter 31: The Market in the Factory

## Scope, Authority, and Evidence Posture

This record tests frozen Production Run 001 Chapter 31 by identifying the documented NewBo founding leader associated with converting a flood-damaged industrial site into a public market and entrepreneurial platform. Ann Poe was tested rather than assumed. John Pinkerton's later stall is excluded as a substitute for platform creation. Repository drafts are candidate evidence summaries; targeted contemporary reporting and official NewBo history were inspected. This record maps primarily to RQ-006, RQ-007, RQ-018, RQ-026, RQ-027, RQ-034, RQ-042, RQ-044, and RQ-045.

## Identified Founding Leader, Agency, Chronology, and Chapter Function

- **Documented founding leader:** Sarah Ordover, president of Cedar Rapids City Market in contemporaneous June 2010 reporting.
- **Why Ordover rather than Ann Poe:** The 2010 account places Ordover at the head of the founding nonprofit while its board presented the final site and business plans, formally sought control of the Quality Chef site, sought preservation of flood-affected industrial buildings, assembled a proposed funding structure, and sequenced site control, capital campaign, and construction. The first inspected Ann Poe account is from October 2012, when she was executive director during finishing work and merchant move-in. A 2019 interview says she held that job for close to a year. Poe clearly shaped opening operations and articulated the incubator purpose, but the inspected record does not make her the founding platform chooser.
- **Chronology control:** Post-2008 flood organizing; 2010 site and business plan; acquisition/funding/construction work; October 2012 opening period. Later merchant outcomes may illustrate use but cannot replace the founding decision.
- **Chapter function:** Show a civic entrepreneur accepting the larger platform risk so smaller operators could enter through staged commitments.

## Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **As president of Cedar Rapids City Market, Sarah Ordover publicly led the nonprofit's 2010 commitment to secure the flood-affected Quality Chef site, keep industrial buildings from demolition, and develop a public market combining food access, shared commercial space, community uses, and lower-threshold entry for vendors.**

### Supported responsibility and risk

The contemporary account identifies Ordover as president, attributes the funding explanation and site-control sequence to her, quotes her answering council concern that the enterprise could fail, and records her insistence that the site agreement had to precede fundraising and construction. This establishes executive/public responsibility, uncertainty, fundraising exposure, and reputational accountability. It does not establish that she alone voted, owned the site, guaranteed debt, signed every agreement, or made the later design pivot.

### Structurally safer alternative

The nonprofit could have abandoned the site, accepted demolition, leased ordinary space, limited the plan to temporary outdoor markets, or waited until private development carried the place risk. Those alternatives would reduce capital and redevelopment complexity while surrendering the shared platform and adaptive-reuse opportunity. No source records Ordover enumerating all of them; the council's lease-versus-transfer concern is the one documented alternative in the inspected account.

## Supported Interpretation

Ordover is the strongest documented founding protagonist because she appears before construction and opening as president of the nonprofit seeking site control, preservation, funding, and construction. Her role makes the platform wager legible: the organization would carry land, building, fundraising, programming, and public-space risk so vendors did not each need a stand-alone property before testing demand.

The accomplishment was collaborative. The board made organizational decisions; the City controlled the site; Project for Public Spaces developed plans and market analysis; public and private funders supplied capital; volunteers and contractors executed; Ann Poe led opening-period operations and merchant-facing purpose. Ordover may be centered only as the documented founding president who accepted public execution responsibility, not as sole inventor, financier, designer, or builder.

The physical conversion also requires qualification. The 2010 account describes a mixed plan with a new market hall and reuse of two existing structures. Later official history says the flooded Quality Chef plant was renovated into Rotary Hall. The record inspected here connects the same site and organization across time but does not identify who authorized the final design change or which 2010 building became Rotary Hall.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids gave Ordover and the founding organization

The city supplied a flood-recovery opening, a valuable industrial site, possible in-kind property support, public negotiation, damaged structures capable of reuse, local vendors, civic volunteers, and access to public and private funding networks. The city also imposed conditions: demolition pressure, uncertain site control, council concern about enterprise failure, and a district still recovering from catastrophic loss.

### What Ordover and the organization gave Cedar Rapids

They advanced a specific public-market proposition, defended keeping industrial structures from demolition, organized plans and funding around the site, and created a platform intended to support fresh food, public gathering, community uses, and business entry. Poe and later leaders translated that platform into opening operations. No single leader can claim the whole conversion.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Sarah Ordover versus Ann Poe:** Ordover is documented at the founding site-and-capital decision; Poe is documented as opening-period executive director and incubator advocate.
2. **President versus board:** Ordover had leadership responsibility, but the board and nonprofit made collective decisions.
3. **Initial plan versus completed form:** The 2010 plan included new construction and multiple reused buildings; later history describes renovation of the Quality Chef plant into Rotary Hall.
4. **Public support versus nonprofit risk:** City property and sought public funds reduced some exposure while creating accountability and dependence.
5. **Incubator promise versus merchant outcome:** Shared space can reduce place risk but cannot guarantee sales, survival, graduation, or profit.
6. **Platform creation versus platform use:** Pinkerton and other stallholders demonstrate use only; they did not make the founding site decision.

## Human Stakes and Narrative-Useful Detail

A failed platform could leave the nonprofit without a viable site, funders without a functioning public asset, and entrepreneurs without the promised lower-threshold entry. Preserving buildings exposed organizers to unknown structural and adaptation costs; demolition would erase potential space. The 2010 council request, $900,000 parcel estimate, explicit concern about enterprise failure, two threatened buildings, and Ordover's statement that nothing could proceed without site control provide a documented decision sequence. Use them in attributed summary, not as a reconstructed scene.

## Chapter Necessity

Chapter 31 is necessary because it explains who accepted the larger risk behind the “small first bet.” Chapter 30 shows an institution moving mission through damaged space; Chapter 31 shows a founding nonprofit turning flood recovery into shared commercial infrastructure. Without Ordover and the founding decision, a merchant stall appears spontaneously and Pinkerton receives credit for a platform he merely used.

## Inherited Condition and Transition to Chapter 32

Chapter 31 receives a city-level recovery capability from Chapter 30: damaged structures can be adapted around a future mission, and complex capital can be organized through institutions. It does not receive a personal handoff from Gail Naughton.

The outgoing inheritance is staged-risk infrastructure: shared place, gathered traffic, selection, utilities, programming, and smaller spaces allow entrepreneurs to test products without first carrying a whole storefront. Chapter 32 may inherit a city practiced in adaptive use and changing productive forms. It must not imply that ADM's corporate operating decision arose from NewBo, Ordover, Poe, or a stallholder.

## Research Verdict

**PASS**

Targeted contemporary research identifies Sarah Ordover—not Ann Poe and not John Pinkerton—as the strongest documented NewBo founding leader.
