## Entrepreneur and Agency Test

A December 1907 engineering article is summarized in the prior draft as identifying W. J. Greene as manager of the lighting department of the Cedar Rapids & Iowa City Railway & Light Company. The summary connects him to an improved boiler operating in the company's plant and an order for another boiler.

That supports operational association and perhaps technical or departmental management. It does not establish that Greene:

- proposed or approved the boiler purchase;
- controlled capital allocation;
- signed the purchase order or financing;
- served as a director or executive with investment authority;
- authorized construction of the interurban corridor;
- bore personal or executive responsibility for passenger or freight results;
- decided how generation capacity was divided between lighting and railway operations; or
- accepted a documented consequence if the investment failed.

**Agency finding:** W. J. Greene is presently an associated manager, not a verified investment decision-maker. The person or board that authorized and bore responsibility for the railway/light commitment remains unidentified.

## Chronology

- A preservation-history summary places organization of the Cedar Rapids and Marion Street Railway Company in 1879.
- Steam service reportedly began in 1880, another downtown line followed by 1882, and electrification occurred in 1891.
- Competing interurban proposals reportedly appeared by 1902.
- A government-study summary says Iowa Railway & Light Company built the Cedar Rapids–Iowa City corridor in 1903–1904 and began electric passenger and freight service on August 13, 1904.
- Professional accounts summarized from 1907 describe freight operations, a rate dispute, shop training for motormen, and Greene's lighting-department association with boiler equipment.
- The chapter's person-level decision must therefore precede or coincide with a documented 1907 capital or operating commitment; later ridership, bus substitution, corporate separation, and the 1953 passenger-service ending are consequences of the system, not Greene's decision.

## Qualified Interpretations

- Generation capacity was a strategic constraint for a company selling lighting and railway movement, but the record does not establish Greene's control over the combined system.
- An additional boiler may indicate a commitment to capacity or reliability. It cannot be called Greene's bet without an authorization chain.
- Scheduled movement created potential reach; timetables, fares, transfers, reliability, and service hours determined whether that reach was usable for particular people.
- Passenger and freight operations could share infrastructure while having different economics. No surviving record in the repository shows Greene choosing between those markets.
- Later continuation of freight after passenger service ended demonstrates divergent market outcomes, not vindication of a 1907 Greene decision.

## Conflicts and Evidence Boundaries

1. **Managerial association versus investment authority:** The architecture correctly treats Greene's title as insufficient.
2. **Department versus corporation:** Lighting-department management does not automatically confer railway, board, or capital authority.
3. **Equipment chronology versus authorship:** A boiler was operating and another was ordered; sequence does not identify the authorizer.
4. **Corporate-name variation:** Iowa Railway & Light Company and Cedar Rapids & Iowa City Railway & Light Company appear in summaries. Legal continuity, ownership, and authority must be verified rather than normalized silently.
5. **Infrastructure versus usable access:** Track and schedules do not prove affordability, reliability, shift compatibility, or equal access.
6. **Later outcome versus original responsibility:** Wartime patronage, 1950 separation, 1953 passenger closure, and freight survival cannot be assigned to Greene.

Downstream work must not say Greene built the interurban, ordered the boiler, financed the plant, guaranteed the schedule, or chose the passenger/freight strategy.

## Courage Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

- **Required risk:** Documented capital exposure, debt, franchise obligation, service commitment, operating responsibility, or accountability to owners, regulators, customers, and workers.
- **Analytical safer alternative:** Delay capacity, separate lighting and railway investment, limit service, lease power, or defer corridor expansion. These are analytical comparisons unless records show they were considered.
- **Present prohibition:** Do not convert Greene's management title into courage or responsibility.
