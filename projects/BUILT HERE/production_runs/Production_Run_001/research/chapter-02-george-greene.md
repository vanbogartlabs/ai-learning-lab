## Defining courageous decision

Greene is reported to have accepted or purchased an interest in the Rapids City lands in 1841 while the interest's deed, parcel, seller, price, legal character, participant structure, value, and economic engine cannot be reconstructed from repository evidence. His courage is the bounded act of joining uncertainty, not proof that he cured title, financed the whole town, or knew it would succeed.

## Future attempted

By joining, Greene participated in the attempted future represented by the plat: a commercially viable town whose lots and prospective river power could attract people, capital, and enterprise. The record does not recover Greene's private formulation of that future. Later transport activity may illuminate a broader character pattern, but it is not necessary to prove this chapter's 1841 decision and should not displace it.

## Uncertainty and safer alternative

The acquisition's essential terms are missing; ownership and participant traditions conflict; the 805 planned lots had no demonstrated buyer base; and the river had not become dependable power. Greene's safer alternative was to wait for a recoverable title chain, clear transaction terms, proven demand, and operating waterpower—or to avoid taking an interest in the venture.

## Supported personal and venture risk

- **Supported personal exposure:** The reported acquisition placed Greene inside the legal and economic uncertainty of an unproven land venture. Later sources identify him as a lawyer and territorial councilor, creating a narrative tension between legal training and entry into ambiguity, but not proof of professional damage.
- **Supported venture exposure:** The interest depended on collective success: a large planned inventory still required buyers, participants, infrastructure, and productive use. Joining added commitment without resolving those dependencies.
- **Boundary:** Greene's exact capital contribution, purchase price, ownership fraction, individual work, expected loss, reputation at stake, and return are unknown. The reported $3,000 figure and three-fourths interest cannot be assigned cleanly to him or to a settled purchaser group.

## Supported opposition and constraints

No named skeptic or organized opposition to Greene is documented. Structural constraints supply the supported pressure: unclear title and transfer terms; overlapping but non-equivalent purchaser, company, and survey lists; unproven demand for planned lots; an unsettled founder story; collective dependence among participants; and a Cedar River that did not yet provide dependable mechanical power. The incomplete modern record must not be converted into a claim that Greene himself lacked all information now missing.

## Minimum essential facts

1. A later account places Greene in an 1841 acquisition of an interest in “the lands,” but the deed or contract is not recovered in the repository summaries.
2. The exact parcel, date, seller, price, legal character, ownership fraction, and Greene's formal capacity remain unresolved.
3. Rapids City was surveyed/platted in 1841 and is described in a professional historic-context study, as summarized in the repository, as slightly more than sixty square blocks with **805 planned lots** on the east bank and streets aligned to the Cedar.
4. Greene appears in summaries of both the platting company and survey work, but those categories do not establish identical roles.
5. Later accounts identify Greene as a lawyer and territorial councilor; no repository evidence shows that he cured the venture's title or designed its legal structure.
6. His reported participation broadened the venture beyond Brown alone without proving Greene's sole authority or causation.
7. Surveyed inventory and additional participation did not make the river productive; that unresolved business problem is the Chapter 3 handoff.

## Evidence boundary

### Supported within the repository summaries

- Greene was associated with the 1841 Rapids City venture and repeatedly appears among its participants.
- Rapids City had a surveyed/platted commercial form of slightly more than sixty blocks and 805 planned lots.
- Greene is later identified as a lawyer and territorial councilor.
- The venture's participant roles and transaction chain remain materially unresolved.
- The river-centered economic engine was not yet dependable.

### Qualified

- Greene's “purchase” or acceptance of an interest is reported by a later account rather than demonstrated here by an inspected deed.
- The claim that his joining converted Brown's plan into shared commitment is a bounded interpretation of participation, not evidence that Brown had acted alone or that Greene's entry alone caused collective action.
- The lawyer/ambiguity contrast is narratively useful, but the record does not establish what legal information Greene possessed, how he evaluated it, or whether contemporaries viewed the choice as reputationally risky.
- Any larger pattern linking this choice to Greene's later waterpower, steamboat, or railroad activity is interpretive and belongs outside Chapter 2's minimum proof.

### Unknown / prohibited inference

- Exact object and terms of purchase; precise seller and purchaser group; fraction and price attributable to Greene; title quality; Greene's formal office in the venture; individual contribution to the survey; private motive, confidence, fear, dialogue, calculations, or knowledge.
- Greene cannot be called sole founder, sole purchaser, legal architect, surveyor, financier, or the person who made the plat or title valid.

## Contradictions and tensions to preserve

- One later account reports a three-fourths interest sold for $3,000 to a group including Greene; repository summaries note varying purchaser lists and a different reported disposition of a remaining quarter. No executed transfer resolves the conflict.
- Greene appears in both a platting-company list and a survey-work list, but the lists differ. Membership, ownership, and work must not be collapsed into one role.
- A lawyer entered an interest whose legal character is now opaque; this is a supported tension, not proof that he acted recklessly or knowingly ignored defects.
- Participation increased the venture's capacity and exposure at once: more people could act, but none of the essential land, demand, or power uncertainties disappeared.

## Indigenous and ethical context

Greene entered the same settler land proposition described in Chapter 1. Repository summaries establish that this opportunity arose within coercive United States expansion after the Black Hawk War and did not follow from empty land or Indigenous disappearance; continuing Meskwaki presence in Iowa must remain visible. Chapter 2 should carry that ethical fact forward economically rather than repeat a treaty prelude. Do not assert uncertain local treaty geography or attribute to Greene any undocumented knowledge, attitude, participation, or response.

- The strongest opening fact is the asymmetry between action and surviving documentation: Greene reportedly chose an interest before the repository can identify exactly what he bought.
- The missing deed, parcel, seller, price, and legal character should be qualified once, not repeated as archival atmosphere.
- “805 planned lots” distinguishes defined inventory from demonstrated demand: lots were not buyers, and streets were not traffic.
- Greene's identity as lawyer and territorial councilor sharpens the decision without authorizing claims of legal mastery or private reasoning.
- The participant lists can show an overlapping network rather than a solitary founder, provided roles remain separate.
- A final contrast is concrete and accurate: survey lines could define lots but could not build or stabilize waterpower.
