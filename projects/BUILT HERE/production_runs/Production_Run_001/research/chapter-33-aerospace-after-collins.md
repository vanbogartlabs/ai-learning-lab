## Entrepreneur Candidate, Agency, and Chapter Necessity

- **Primary candidate:** Nathan Boelkins, president of Collins Aerospace's Avionics business unit.
- **Documented scope:** Collins's official profile says he leads more than 15,000 people designing and maintaining flight-deck and connected-aviation solutions for commercial and military customers. It says his prior Business & Regional Avionics role focused on customer satisfaction, portfolio growth, strategic planning, and advanced product development.
- **Candidate co-actor/replacement lead:** Jenny Miller, vice president and general manager of Military Avionics and Helicopters, is the named executive quoted in the March 2025 Black Hawk/Mosarc contract announcement.
- **Agency limit:** Boelkins's profile describes broad responsibilities but no discrete bid, allocation, retention, or mandate. The Black Hawk release documents an $80 million award, Cedar Rapids/Huntsville work, and Miller's product explanation, but does not say Miller or Boelkins chose to bid, committed capital, assigned Cedar Rapids work, retained a team, or approved Mosarc as the program solution.
- **Chapter necessity:** The slot is necessary to show that Cedar Rapids aerospace capability survived the Collins founder and later corporate combinations only by winning renewed work. A heritage name or headquarters history cannot perform the decision.

## Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

### Candidate decision

No person-level defining decision is verified.

The strongest event is Collins Aerospace's receipt of an $80 million U.S. Army contract in March 2025 to upgrade Black Hawk avionics using the Mosarc modular open-systems product family, with work in Cedar Rapids and Huntsville. Receipt of an award proves customer selection and company performance opportunity. It does not identify who authorized the bid, selected Mosarc, allocated internal investment, assigned work between sites, or accepted responsibility for talent retention.

### Supported exposure and responsibility

Boelkins's presidency establishes executive responsibility for a large avionics portfolio. Miller's vice-president/general-manager role and attributable product explanation establish proximity to the Black Hawk program. Both carry professional accountability in a regulated, customer-dependent aerospace business.

No inspected record establishes personally accepted capital, bid, schedule, staffing, or product exposure by either person. Corporate office, a large team, or public quotation is structural exposure, not proof of a courageous decision.

### Structurally safer alternatives

A corporate avionics business could refrain from bidding, use a closed/proprietary architecture, allocate work to another site, buy capability, reduce local staffing, or sustain only legacy products. These are analytical alternatives, not documented options considered by Boelkins or Miller. No source establishes the approval path or losing consequence.

## Supported Interpretation

Cedar Rapids remains important because practiced technical capability continues to receive difficult work inside a global company. The 2025 Black Hawk award proves that current work was assigned partly to Cedar Rapids. It does not prove that Boelkins personally “kept Cedar Rapids consequential.” That phrase describes an objective and desired outcome.

Boelkins is a stronger candidate than a generic corporate spokesperson because his official profile gives him operating responsibility for Avionics. Miller is a stronger program-level lead because the contract announcement identifies her relevant general-management domain. Neither passes without an attributable commitment. A company can win a contract through a long chain of capture managers, engineers, pricing authorities, executives, and customer decisions; grammar must not collapse that chain into one hero.
