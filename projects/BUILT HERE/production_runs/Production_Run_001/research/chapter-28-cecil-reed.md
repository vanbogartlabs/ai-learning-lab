## Supported Interpretation

The strongest evidence-safe interpretation is **entrepreneurial workaround versus structural reform**. The Reeds used property they controlled to make one ordinary transaction—lodging—more dependable for travelers exposed to racial refusal. Cecil's later public work addressed the limit of that solution: one inclusive proprietor could not control other lodging, housing, employment, or public rules.

This interpretation must remain bounded. Motel Sepia did not end discrimination, and the record does not establish profitability, customer volume, individual traveler outcomes, or the private thoughts of either Reed. Cecil's legislative work should not absorb Evelyn's ownership and operating role. Conversely, preserving Evelyn does not require making her the documented actor in Cecil's later public career.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids gave the Reeds

Cedar Rapids supplied a home, a contracting market, a road-connected parcel, customers moving through the region, Black churches and civic organizations, civil-rights networks, and a route into state office. The city's racial exclusions were also part of the business problem. Place was both capability and constraint.

### What the Reeds gave Cedar Rapids

Together they supplied an inclusive lodging option in a market where equal access could not be assumed. Cecil also supplied contracting work, advocacy, organizational service, and legislative representation. His preserved papers make business and civil-rights action legible together rather than as unrelated careers.
