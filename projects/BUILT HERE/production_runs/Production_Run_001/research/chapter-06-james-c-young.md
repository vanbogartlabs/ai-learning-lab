**Why this chapter is necessary:** Chapter 5 leaves rail-enabled circulation. ## Supported Facts

1. Repository summaries identify Young as a land developer and promoter associated with southwest Cedar Rapids additions. **Confidence: Moderate**, pending underlying records.
2. Young's Third Addition is reported as an 883-lot addition laid out in 1883. **Confidence: Moderate/qualified**; the original plat is not registered.
3. A later real-estate account reportedly recalls an 1883 Young promotion using brass-band music and free food. **Confidence: Low to Moderate**; no contemporary advertisement or report is registered.
4. The repository has no dependable sales result for the promotion. **Confidence: High as a present evidence boundary**.
5. Cedar Rapids reportedly had 2,110 platted lots east of the river and 1,980 west of it by 1880, followed by more than 3,000 additional lots from 1882 through 1884. **Confidence: Moderate/qualified**.
6. Streetcar service reportedly reached the southwest area in 1884, after the 1883 platting and promotion. **Confidence: Moderate/qualified**.
7. Young's control, financing, negotiation, or ownership of that streetcar extension is not established. **Confidence: High as a present boundary**.
8. Population reportedly increased from 10,104 in 1880 to 18,020 in 1890, with boundary changes contributing. The increase cannot be credited to Young. **Confidence: Moderate/qualified**.

## Qualified Interpretation

The scale and public promotion support the interpretation that Young asked buyers to treat an unfinished district as credible before all surrounding systems existed. A lot was concrete and legally describable, while much of its usefulness depended on transportation, passable streets, drainage, utilities, construction, institutions, neighbors, and continued demand outside Young's sole control.

Promotion made attention and expectation part of the business problem. It did not prove deception, confidence, success, or control of later neighborhood development.

## Person-Level Agency

Young's supported agency is the choice to place and publicly promote a reported district-scale inventory before access, services, occupancy, and demand were proved. His public promotion is attributable at summary level. His command over the systems needed to complete the district is not.

## Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

### Defining decision

> Young chose to promote a reported 883-lot southwest addition in 1883 before its access, services, occupancy, buyer response, and surrounding commitments were proven.

### Supported risk

- A large inventory had to compete amid extensive contemporary platting.
- The proposition depended on coordinated action beyond Young's sole control.
- Buyers could commit before daily access and services existed in useful combination.
- Young likely faced structural inventory exposure, but no personal amount, debt, or loss is established.
- Promotion could transfer some uncertainty to buyers; transaction terms are absent, so the allocation cannot be judged.

### Safer alternative

A structurally safer alternative was to delay broad promotion until more access, services, or occupancy existed, or to market parcels incrementally rather than expose a district-scale proposition. The record does not show Young consciously considered these alternatives.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

**What Cedar Rapids gave Young:** Rail-enabled regional circulation, a growing two-bank city, extensive platting, expanding local access, population and institutional growth, and a plausible argument that present distance might become future usability.

**What Young gave Cedar Rapids:** A public market proposition that made outer land visible and divisible for buyer decision. He did not supply every system needed to fulfill it and cannot be credited with the neighborhood's later life.

## Human Stakes

Young exposed a large proposition to public judgment, although his personal financial burden is unknown. Prospective buyers and households could commit money to parcels before transportation, streets, drainage, utilities, institutions, houses, and neighbors existed in usable combination. Other builders and workers carried the labor of completion. No named buyer experience is available; do not invent one.
