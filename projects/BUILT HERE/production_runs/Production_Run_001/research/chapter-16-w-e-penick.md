## Entrepreneur and Agency

Penick & Ford acquired the Douglas works and rebuilt on the site in 1920. A July 1920 newspaper report, as summarized in the repository, says W. E. Penick had visited Cedar Rapids several times, remained in close touch with construction, and inspected the new plant.

This supports personal attention and executive follow-through after acquisition. It does not prove that Penick alone selected the site, authorized the purchase, supplied capital, designed the works, or controlled every construction decision. The chapter's defensible person-level action is his repeated engagement while the company's reconstruction wager remained unproven.

## Chronology

- Late 1919 or February 1920: Repository summaries place different steps in the Douglas-to-Penick & Ford transaction.
- 1920: Penick & Ford rebuilt on the former Douglas site.
- July 1920: A contemporary newspaper reportedly described Penick's repeated visits, close contact, and inspection of the new plant.
- February 1925 through May 1927: Penick & Ford reportedly used methods to produce cerelose that later became the subject of federal patent litigation.
- Later ruling: A federal court reportedly found infringement, ordered an injunction, and required an accounting.
- 1935: Company president F. T. Bedford reportedly announced a $200,000 grain-storage project; this is later company consequence, not Penick's decision.

The first successful run and first shipment are absent. Later patent, storage, ownership, and plant-history facts may demonstrate consequence and continuing risk but must not displace the 1920 decision.

## Conflicts and Evidence Boundaries

2. **Company versus person:** Penick & Ford committed capital; W. E. Penick is documented through attention. Do not assign the whole company decision or cost to him without authority records.
3. **Inherited assets:** Rail, foundations, utilities, machinery, warehouses, or structures may have mattered, but no asset list establishes what transferred or was reused.
4. **Safety:** The new plant had to operate on catastrophe-marked ground, but no design comparison supports saying Penick rebuilt safer or ignored safety.
5. **Proof of operation:** Later litigation implies operation; the first run, shipment, sustained output, customers, and profit remain unknown.
6. **Capacity versus performance:** Reported capacity is not output or commercial success.
7. **Later executives and owners:** Bedford's storage decision and later transfers cannot be folded into Penick's agency.

Prohibited claims include Penick's private beliefs or emotions, exact investment, personal guarantees, sole purchase authority, design authorship, first-run scene, safety improvements, profitability, or personal responsibility for later patent practices.

## Courage Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> After Penick & Ford acquired the ruined Douglas site, W. E. Penick repeatedly attended to and inspected the 1920 reconstruction while the new plant had not yet proved it could operate.

- **Uncertainty:** Condition of inherited assets, demolition and construction cost, machinery performance, workforce, product quality, customer demand, first successful operation, reputation of the site, and future legal or process risk.
- **Supported risk:** Company capital in a nonfunctioning, damaged site; management attention; construction and machinery exposure; uncertain market acceptance. Personal dollar exposure is not established.
- **Analytical safer alternative:** Build elsewhere, liquidate or repurpose the property, delay reconstruction, or disengage after acquisition. The record does not show Penick expressly considering these alternatives.
- **Why distinct:** Douglas's decision transferred continuity without control. Penick's decision was successor execution: maintain attention until a new company system could test the inherited site's productive value. Chapter 17's portable-machinery decision concerns a different customer problem and must not begin here.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

**What Cedar Rapids gave Penick & Ford:** Regional corn, an established industrial parcel, transportation, possible inherited connections, experienced labor, processing knowledge, and public demand for industrial return.

**What Penick gave Cedar Rapids:** Documented executive attention during reconstruction; the company returned the site to productive use and supported employment and specialized processing. Credit must remain distributed among engineers, managers, construction workers, operators, chemists, maintenance crews, loaders, packers, rail workers, and office staff.

## Human Stakes

A successful successor plant could restore employment and demand for farmers, carriers, suppliers, and services. Failure could consume company capital and leave the site nonproductive. Families harmed in 1919 remained part of the moral landscape; renewed production did not settle their losses. No representative worker, family, or customer may be invented.
