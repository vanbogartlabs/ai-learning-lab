# Research Record — Chapter 30: Saving Memory, Rebuilding Place

## Scope, Authority, and Evidence Posture

This record supports frozen Production Run 001 Chapter 30 and tests Gail Naughton's post-flood institutional and rebuilding decision independently of Chapter 29's unresolved emergency-decision gate. Architecture controls the slot and transition, not the facts. Repository briefs and drafts are candidate evidence summaries. This record maps primarily to RQ-006, RQ-016, RQ-018, RQ-029, RQ-034, RQ-042, and RQ-045.

## Entrepreneur, Agency, Chronology, and Chapter Function

- **Protagonist:** Gail Naughton as an institutional entrepreneur and NCSML president and CEO, not as owner of the museum's assets.
- **Chronology control:** Post-inundation damage assessment through temporary operations, specialized conservation, capital assembly, strategic planning, movement and elevation of the historic building, expansion, and July 2012 reopening.
- **Distinct agency:** The official NCSML retrospective explicitly places the fundraising, saving and moving of the original building, expansion, and reopening under Naughton's leadership. Her accepted responsibility is executive, strategic, fundraising, and reputational; it is not sole board authority, personal ownership, or personal liability.
- **Chapter function:** Show rebuilding as institutional design. Mission, collections, staff work, programs, capital, and construction had to proceed on different tracks before they could be recombined in a safer permanent form.

## Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **After flood damage made the former operating arrangement unusable, Naughton led the choice to keep NCSML functioning through displaced programs and offices while assembling the distinct conservation, capital, strategy, relocation, elevation, expansion, and construction work required for a permanent return.**

This decision is materially different from Chapter 29's candidate emergency choice. Chapter 29 asks whether Naughton personally committed the mission under unknown loss. Chapter 30 begins after damage and displacement are known enough to become a portfolio of projects and asks whether she will rebuild the institution rather than merely wait, repair in place, or close.

### Supported responsibility and risk

The official museum account credits Naughton's leadership over a recovery that raised more than $28 million, saved and moved the original building, added 30,000 square feet, and reopened in July 2012. Her role carried professional, fiduciary-adjacent, execution, fundraising, schedule, engineering, donor-trust, and reputational exposure. Exact bylaws, delegated authority, votes, contract signatures, indemnity, and personal financial exposure were not recovered; the museum's money and liabilities were not hers personally.

### Structurally safer alternative

The institution could have remained closed pending a single permanent answer, repaired the building in place without expansion, abandoned the historic structure, or reduced the mission to what existing temporary capacity could support. Those options could lower coordination or capital risk while increasing exposure to recurrence, invisibility, collection loss, staff attrition, and mission contraction. No inspected source records Naughton enumerating them; they are analytical alternatives.

## Supported Interpretation

Naughton's strongest evidence-safe entrepreneurial act was not a single physical rescue. It was converting one damaged institution into coordinated workstreams that could survive on different schedules and then converge. Preservation required movement: collections traveled, programs and offices relocated, capital was matched to particular purposes, and the historic building itself moved.

The interpretation must preserve distributed agency. The board, staff, volunteers, conservators, structural movers, architects, builders, donors, public agencies, and temporary-space providers were not extensions of Naughton. The official phrase “under her leadership” supports executive responsibility but not sole authorship of each decision, contract, gift, treatment, or construction act.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids gave Naughton and NCSML

Cedar Rapids supplied an institution rooted in Czech Village, staff, volunteers, donors, public attention, temporary space, city recovery planning, construction capacity, and regional links to universities, conservators, agencies, and funders. The damaged city also created competition for land, money, labor, and speed.

### What Naughton and NCSML gave Cedar Rapids

Under her leadership, the museum remained publicly legible during displacement and returned in a moved, elevated, and expanded form. The project enlarged the city's recovery repertoire: preservation did not have to mean restoring every function in the same place on the same schedule. The evidence does not prove that NCSML alone revived Czech Village or caused nearby commercial investment.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Individual leadership versus distributed execution:** Naughton led; institutions and specialists made the work possible.
2. **Preservation versus movement:** The historic building survived by changing location and elevation.
3. **Mission continuity versus permanent construction:** Public programs and staff work could continue before the building returned.
4. **One campaign versus restricted uses:** Capital totals cannot be treated as one interchangeable pool.
5. **Eleven versus twelve feet:** Repository accounts differ on elevation; the inspected official retrospective uses a foundation three feet above the flood. Do not silently harmonize different measurements.
6. **Reopening versus completion:** A reopening date establishes access, not completion of every conservation track.
7. **Chapter 29 versus Chapter 30:** This chapter may receive unknown-loss context but must not claim the unverified truckload decision as Naughton's distinct act.

## Unknowns and Explicit Prohibitions

- Do not assign the museum's ownership, assets, liabilities, campaign funds, or contracts to Naughton personally.
- Do not claim she personally conserved objects, selected every grant, engineered the move, or controlled every board decision.
- Do not invent board deliberations, private motive, emotion, dialogue, donor conversations, or construction scenes.
- Do not blend grant amounts or campaign totals without identifying scopes and restrictions.
- Do not choose eleven or twelve feet as elevation without resolving what each source measured.
- Do not equate opening-weekend attendance with long-term institutional or district success.
- Do not claim the rebuilding caused NewBo City Market; the transition is a city-level inherited capability, not personal causation.

## Human Stakes and Narrative-Useful Detail

Staff needed work and an operating institution. Donors and agencies needed accountable use of restricted support. Czech and Slovak communities and visitors depended on continued access to nonreplaceable cultural holdings. Volunteers and specialists carried labor that executive summaries can obscure. Frozen books, hundreds of hours spent on sound recordings, a map in thirty-two pieces, displaced exhibitions, and a whole building moved are useful details after exact-source verification. They cannot support invented scenes or claims that Naughton personally performed the physical work.

## Chapter Necessity

Chapter 30 is necessary because it advances the flood story from uncertainty into institution-building. It shows that rebuilding is not synonymous with repairing walls: an organization must preserve mission, divide work, align authority and restricted capital, and choose a permanent form. This is a materially distinct entrepreneurial decision from Chapter 29's intended but currently unverified emergency commitment.

## Inherited Condition and Transition to Chapter 31

Chapter 30 receives an institutional condition, not a certified personal handoff: collections, staff responsibility, public mission, and recovery work continue after a building fails. Because Chapter 29 is blocked, Chapter 30 must not pretend an earlier Naughton decision has passed; it can begin from documented post-flood displacement and Naughton's later attributed leadership.

The outgoing inheritance is a Cedar Rapids capability: damaged buildings can be evaluated by what future mission they can support, and public/private coalitions can preserve useful space through adaptation. Chapter 31 may receive that recovery repertoire and the availability of a flood-damaged industrial site. It may not claim Gail Naughton selected the NewBo site, recruited its organizers, financed it, or personally handed a project to its founding leader.
