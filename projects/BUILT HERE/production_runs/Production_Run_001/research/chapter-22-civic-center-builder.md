# Research Record — Chapter 22: The Civic Center on an Island

## Scope and Gate Resolution

This record supports approved Production Run 001 Chapter 22 and tests whether Grant Wood held entrepreneurial production responsibility for the Veterans Memorial Window. Targeted research located an official City of Cedar Rapids Veterans Memorial Commission history. It states that Wood had never taken on a contract of this proportion, that he and Arnold Pyle devoted months to preparation, that the Commission remained committed through delays, that Emil Frei was awarded the fabrication bid, that Wood requested travel to Germany, and that he supervised stages of production in Munich.

That evidence establishes more than artistic participation. It establishes a commissioned creative practitioner accepting responsibility for translating a design through full-scale study, assistant labor, borrowed industrial space, a commercial bidder, overseas fabrication, delay, supervision, and return. No replacement protagonist is required at Research stage. The Veterans Memorial Commission and Emil Frei remain essential institutional/commercial actors and cannot be reduced to Wood's helpers.

## Entrepreneur, Agency, and Chapter Function

- **Protagonist:** Grant Wood as an independent creative builder and commissioned production steward.
- **Person-level agency:** Wood accepted a stained-glass contract of unprecedented scale for him; worked for months with Arnold Pyle on a full-size study; used Quaker Oats space; requested travel to the Munich fabrication branch; and supervised production stages.
- **Commercial organizer:** The Veterans Memorial Commission, not Wood, awarded the fabrication bid to Emil Frei. The Commission therefore controlled an essential contracting decision; exact financing and contract allocation remain unknown.
- **Chapter function:** Show how local authorship becomes a durable public object only when one accountable creator translates it into an executable chain spanning public client, assistant, industrial space, commercial procurement, specialist fabrication, supervision, transport, and installation.

## Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **Wood chose to remain responsible for executing the memorial design at full scale through a distributed production chain—rather than limit the work to his existing stained-glass capacity or surrender responsibility after design—culminating in his requested travel to Munich to supervise fabrication.**

### Personally accepted responsibility and risk

The official City account calls the work a contract of a proportion Wood had never undertaken and describes limited prior stained-glass experience, months of preparation, delay, travel request, and supervision. Those facts establish time, professional standing, unfamiliar-medium, execution, and reputational exposure. A prominent public memorial could fail technically, depart from the design, or remain delayed while Wood's authorship remained visible.

The exact fee, liability, travel funding, payment schedule, insurance, indemnity, and personal cash exposure are unknown. “Risk” must therefore remain professional and executional unless contracts establish financial liability.

### Safer alternative

Wood could have proposed a smaller or familiar-medium work, stopped at design submission, accepted fabrication without overseas supervision, or declined a contract beyond his prior scale. No source records him enumerating those alternatives.

## Supported Interpretation

Wood qualifies as an entrepreneurial creative builder because he accepted a paid/public commission and remained responsible for making an idea executable beyond his own hands. His contribution was not merely artistic authorship; it included specification at full scale, coordination with Pyle, use of borrowed capacity, engagement with the public/commercial chain, and supervision of specialist fabrication.

The window was nevertheless a distributed accomplishment. The Commission organized procurement and retained commitment; Emil Frei won the bid and supplied specialist capability; Munich workers fabricated; Quaker Oats supplied space; Pyle assisted. Wood should be centered because the evidence shows his accepted production responsibility, not because others were incidental.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids gave Wood

Cedar Rapids supplied a consequential public client, a memorial purpose, Arnold Pyle's assistance, industrial-scale Quaker Oats space, and institutional/commercial links capable of reaching a specialist St. Louis firm and Munich branch. The city lacked every required craft locally but could assemble a chain that made the project possible.

### What Wood gave Cedar Rapids

Wood translated local memorial ambition into a durable design and stayed engaged through the chain needed to fabricate it. The window gave the building's commemorative purpose a lasting visual form. He did not build May's Island, finance the whole building, award the bid, or unify city, county, federal, convention, and memorial functions.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Independent author versus dependent builder:** Wood's authorship required assistants, corporate space, public procurement, and specialist fabrication.
2. **Wood's responsibility versus Commission authority:** The Commission awarded the fabrication bid and remained committed through delay.
3. **Local design versus overseas execution:** Cedar Rapids originated and studied the work but did not fabricate it alone.
4. **Public memorial versus mixed-use building:** Municipal and convention functions did not erase the memorial purpose.
5. **Retrospective official account versus transaction files:** Civic history supports the production chain but not exact financial terms.
6. **Building chronology and attribution conflicts:** Architectural credit and some island/courthouse dates vary in repository summaries; those disputes are context and must not displace the window decision.

- A claim that Wood contracted or financed the entire civic center, awarded Frei's bid, or fabricated the glass himself.

## Human Stakes and Consequence

Wood's professional standing was attached to a prominent public memorial in an unfamiliar medium. Pyle and specialist fabricators carried execution responsibilities. The Commission and public depended on the delayed chain delivering a work faithful enough to fulfill the memorial purpose. Veterans and families were the commemorative constituency, but the record does not support invented individual reactions.

## Research Verdict

**PASS**

The official City account establishes Wood's entrepreneurial production responsibility: an unprecedented-scale contract in a relatively unfamiliar medium, months of full-scale preparatory work, personal request to travel, and supervision of Munich production through delay. The Veterans Memorial Commission is the documented contracting organizer that awarded Emil Frei the fabrication bid; Wood need not be falsely credited with financing or procurement authority. Financial terms and transaction records remain publication-level gaps, not protagonist-fatal gaps.
