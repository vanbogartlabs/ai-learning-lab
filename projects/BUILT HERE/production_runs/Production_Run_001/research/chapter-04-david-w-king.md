## Entrepreneur and Chapter Function

**Entrepreneur:** David W. King.

**Chapter function:** Center King’s entrepreneurial use of a divided river market, not a general history of Cedar Rapids bridges. Brown’s Chapter 3 leaves productive activity and practical need on both banks; King receives a market for passage. King’s supported story joins a time-limited, exclusive ferry privilege to the platting and expansion of Kingston with Mary King. Connection makes west-bank land more usable, while a west-bank destination creates more reasons to cross.

The chapter’s history of later free, floating, toll, and publicly financed bridges belongs only where it demonstrates the consequence and limit of King’s original crossing proposition. King must not be credited with building, financing, owning, or directing bridges that the repository assigns to other commissioners, contractors, companies, voters, county supervisors, or municipal actors.

The inheritance to James C. Young is outward market, not bridge chronology: once connection makes the two-bank city’s usable ground larger, the commercial problem moves from crossing the central barrier to persuading buyers that a more distant edge can become valuable.

## Defining Courageous Decision

> **King chose to make the crossing more than a time-limited ferry service by pairing the protected passage opportunity with David and Mary King’s platting and expansion of Kingston, committing land and service to one another before settlement demand, continuous passage, franchise duration, or replacement by a bridge could be guaranteed.**

This formulation is the strongest repository-grounded version of King’s decision. The batch architecture calls for King to “commit to a bridge enterprise,” but the repository’s historical synthesis does not establish that King built or financed a bridge. It establishes a ferry franchise that expressly preserved future bridge construction, the Kingston plats credited to David and Mary King, an 1851 flood interruption, and later bridge efforts by other actors.

Downstream artifacts should therefore use **crossing enterprise**, **ferry-and-land proposition**, or similarly bounded language unless direct evidence of King’s bridge participation is recovered. A chapter title may use “bridge” metaphorically only if the prose immediately prevents misattribution.

## Future Attempted

The future visible in King’s actions was a viable west-bank settlement connected to the east-bank town by a revenue-producing crossing: lots and streets creating a destination, and passage making that destination usable.

The record does not establish King’s private vision, words, motives, or division of authority with Mary King. The interpretation rests on the structural conjunction of the ferry privilege and the Kingston plats. It should not become a claim that King predicted annexation, public bridge finance, or Cedar Rapids’ later urban form.

## Uncertainty and Safer Alternative

### Uncertainty King’s enterprises faced

- Whether traffic and toll revenue would justify ferry equipment, landing, labor, and operation.
- Whether river conditions would permit continuous or safe passage.
- Whether west-bank buyers and settlers would give Kingston’s lots practical value.
- Whether the ten-year privilege would last long enough to support the combined proposition.
- Whether a future bridge, expressly protected in the franchise law, would displace the ferry.
- Whether capital and coordination beyond one franchise would be required as the two-bank market grew.
- How David and Mary King divided land ownership, platting decisions, authority, work, and risk.

### Safer alternative

The record does not preserve King’s deliberation. A defensible structural alternative was to operate only the temporary ferry privilege, hold land without organizing a settlement, wait for public infrastructure, or avoid tying west-bank land value to a river crossing that law and nature could interrupt.

Do not claim King consciously compared these options unless a source is later recovered.

## Supported Risk and Opposition

### Supported risk

- **Time-limited legal position:** The franchise lasted ten years and was exclusive only within one mile upstream and downstream.
- **Built-in obsolescence:** The same law protected future bridge construction; exclusivity did not guarantee a permanent crossing monopoly.
- **Physical interruption:** A repository summary reports that an 1851 flood washed out the ferry operation. Duration, equipment loss, cost, and King’s personal loss are unknown.
- **Coupled land-and-access exposure:** If passage failed, west-bank lots became less usable; if settlement demand failed, the crossing had fewer reasons to attract traffic. This is a business inference from the paired ventures.
- **Shared exposure:** Mary King, other property holders, prospective settlers, travelers, merchants, and workers depended in different ways on access. Their amounts and individual experiences are not preserved.

### Supported opposition

No named critic or organized opposition to King is documented in the repository. Structural opposition is supportable:

- flood, ice, open water, and interrupted passage;
- expiration of the franchise;
- future bridge competition explicitly allowed by law;
- uncertain west-bank demand;
- and the scaling requirement for capital, engineering, and public coordination.

Later political contests over bridge location and finance should not be projected backward as opposition King personally faced.

## Essential Facts

1. The Iowa General Assembly granted King, his heirs, and assigns a ten-year ferry franchise across the Cedar opposite Cedar Rapids, exclusive for one mile upstream and downstream.
2. The summarized law required a suitable landing and protected construction of a future bridge from interference.
3. Professional historic-context surveys, as summarized in the repository, credit both David and Mary King with platting Kingston and expanding it the following year.
4. Kingston’s first plat date conflicts: one later account gives 1850, while preservation studies give 1852. Later lot totals and descriptions of municipal status also conflict.
5. A repository summary reports that an 1851 flood washed out the ferry operation. The duration, equipment damage, full loss, and service response are unknown.
6. The surviving repository synthesis supports the conjunction of passage and destination: the ferry served movement to the west bank, while Kingston supplied lots, streets, and recurring reasons to cross. The causal strength and King’s private strategy are interpretive.
7. Later bridge efforts involved other actors and multiple arrangements—free, floating, temporary, tolled, and publicly financed—and repeated physical loss. These details show the scale of the crossing problem but do not establish King as bridge builder.
8. A. Ruger’s 1868 bird’s-eye view was titled “Cedar Rapids and Kingston,” placing both settlements in one represented landscape. A map title does not by itself prove economic integration, traffic, ownership, or causation.
9. Kingston joined Cedar Rapids through a reported sequence involving petitioning, an election, and an April 22, 1870 ordinance; the exact legal sequence remains uncertain.
10. After an ice-driven flood interrupted communication with the west bank in 1871, city voters approved $12,000 in bridge bonds by a reported vote of 483 to 83. Later contract and total-cost figures belong to the public infrastructure consequence, not King’s personal enterprise.
11. The repository explicitly states that King did not build the later bridges or accomplish annexation himself; his bounded contribution was helping create a two-bank market whose need for connection outgrew the original private privilege.

## Contradictions and Tensions

1. **Architecture/evidence conflict:** The batch architecture centers a “bridge enterprise,” while the repository synthesis establishes a ferry franchise and Kingston platting and expressly denies King credit for later bridge construction. Historical truth requires reframing the decision as a crossing enterprise unless new evidence appears.
2. **Plat-date conflict:** Kingston’s first plat is dated 1850 in one later account and 1852 in preservation studies. Do not silently choose.
3. **Sole-protagonist pressure versus shared action:** Chapter 4 belongs to David W. King, but professional surveys credit both David and Mary King with platting. Center David’s crossing decision without erasing Mary or inventing their division of labor.
4. **Exclusivity versus public need:** King’s private advantage depended on exclusivity, while the settlement’s growth made connection important enough to demand alternatives beyond that privilege.
5. **Law versus nature:** The legislature could restrict nearby ferry competition but could not keep flood or ice from ending service.
6. **Connection versus obsolescence:** The franchise offered a market while expressly preserving the infrastructure that could replace it.
7. **Map versus proof:** The 1868 title “Cedar Rapids and Kingston” is visually useful but cannot by itself establish traffic, integration, ownership, or causal influence.
