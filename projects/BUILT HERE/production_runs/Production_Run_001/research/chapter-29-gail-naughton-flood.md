# Research Record — Chapter 29: Thirty-One Feet

## Scope, Authority, and Evidence Posture

This record tests frozen Production Run 001 Chapter 29 only: whether Gail Naughton made a distinct, attributable decision to preserve the National Czech & Slovak Museum & Library's mission while loss and access were still unknown in June 2008. Chapter 30's later institution-and-building strategy may not be borrowed to make this opening emergency pass. Repository briefs and drafts are evidence summaries, not independent corroboration. This record maps primarily to RQ-006, RQ-016, RQ-018, RQ-027, RQ-034, RQ-042, and RQ-045.

## Candidate Entrepreneur, Agency, Chronology, and Chapter Function

- **Candidate protagonist:** Gail Naughton, NCSML president and CEO since 2002.
- **Chronology control:** June 2008 warning, evacuation, inundation, inaccessible building, initial damage assessment, and the earliest continuity choice. Permanent fundraising, strategic planning, building movement, expansion, and 2012 reopening belong to Chapter 30.
- **Intended chapter function:** Show leadership acting before loss can be fully counted: preserve operating mission under unknown physical damage rather than wait for certainty.
- **Agency problem:** The repository summaries state that staff removed two truckloads before inundation and that Naughton had not entered the building four days after the crest. They do not attribute the evacuation order, selection of objects, timing, authority, or accepted responsibility for those two truckloads to Naughton. The earliest clearly consequential continuity action—planning exhibits, store operations, and programs away from the building—also governs Chapter 30's institution-before-place strategy.

## Chronology Established and Not Established

1. The Cedar River crested at the Cedar Rapids gauge on June 13, 2008; the repository draft reports 31.12 feet from USGS material.
2. Staff removed two truckloads before inundation.
3. On June 17, Naughton reportedly had not entered the building and did not know the full loss.
4. Within roughly a month, the building had been cleaned, disinfected, and stripped to an “empty shell,” while Naughton was discussing exhibits, store operations, and programs elsewhere.
5. Temporary exhibition and office arrangements, conservation tracks, capital assembly, building relocation/elevation, expansion, and reopening followed; those later acts are reserved for Chapter 30.

The sequence is supportable as organizational chronology. The current packet does not allocate the first evacuation decision to Naughton personally or prove a separate executive authorization between uncertainty on June 17 and the later continuity strategy.

## Candidate Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **Candidate decision requiring proof: while access and total loss were still unknown, Naughton personally authorized the museum to preserve and continue its mission rather than suspend institutional action until a full inventory and permanent-building plan existed.**

### Supported exposure

As president and CEO, Naughton held a visible stewardship role over staff, collections, public credibility, donors, and mission. The official museum retirement account confirms that the later flood recovery occurred under her leadership. That establishes structural and professional exposure, but not the exact emergency decision, delegation, or authority demanded by this chapter.

### Structurally safer alternative

The institution could have limited activity to safety, insurance, and damage assessment until access returned and the loss was known. That course would reduce premature commitments but risk staff, audience, donor, and mission continuity. No inspected source records Naughton considering or rejecting this alternative; it is analytical only.

## Supported Interpretation

The record safely shows an institution acting under unknown loss and a chief executive whose later leadership was consequential. It does not yet show that the two truckloads were “Naughton's first decision,” nor does it isolate a second early decision from the later strategy of operating while rebuilding. Title plus temporal presence cannot substitute for attributed authority.

The chapter's intended distinction is analytically coherent: Chapter 29 would be **mission under unknown loss**, while Chapter 30 would be **institutional reassembly and permanent rebuilding after damage became legible**. The current evidence packet does not yet make the first half person-specific enough to support two Gail Naughton chapters.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Chief executive role versus attributed act:** Office makes authority plausible; it does not prove who ordered evacuation or selected materials.
2. **Two truckloads versus mission decision:** Physical removal is an opening action, not automatically a distinct strategic choice.
3. **Unknown loss versus later hindsight:** The 2012 reopening cannot be projected backward as certainty or intent in June 2008.
4. **Chapter 29 versus Chapter 30:** Off-site operations currently function as the hinge in both chapters; reuse would duplicate the defining decision.
5. **Individual leadership versus institutional work:** Staff, board, volunteers, conservators, donors, agencies, and contractors must remain visible.
