# Research Record — Chapter 21: Women in the Business of Building

## Scope and Replacement Decision

This record supports approved Production Run 001 Chapter 21 and resolves the architecture's prohibition on reusing Anna Jacobs. The repository did not contain another woman with sufficiently discrete ownership, capital, market-entry, or operating evidence. Targeted web research identified **Mary Snyder**, a Cedar Rapids-raised woman who, after losing her Marion Ford-garage job during the Depression, converted her own automobile into a taxi in 1932, secured a chauffeur's license, formed Mari-An Cab Company with Anne Jordan, acquired an office building, and continued operating after Jordan retired.

Mary Snyder replaces Anna Jacobs as the Chapter 21 protagonist for upstream development. She is better documented for an attributable market-entry decision, personally committed asset, operating continuity, partnership, customer exposure, and outcome. The geographic limitation is material: her company was based in adjacent Marion, not Cedar Rapids. Her Cedar Rapids ties and trips permit a Cedar Rapids-connected regional exchange, but downstream work must not call Mari-An a Cedar Rapids company.

## Entrepreneur, Agency, and Chapter Function

- **Protagonist:** Mary Snyder.
- **Essential collaborator:** Anne Jordan; the partnership and joint two-vehicle company must remain visible.
- **Person-level agency:** After losing her job and failing to find another, Snyder applied for a chauffeur's license and turned her own car into a taxi in 1932. She then partnered with Jordan to form the independent Mari-An Cab Company.
- **Chapter function:** Show a woman converting a personally owned asset into market entry under constrained employment conditions, then coordinating vehicles, partner labor, a depot market, property, safety practices, and long-distance service.
- **Chronology control:** Chapter 21 rewinds from Barta's August 1933 founding to Snyder's 1932 entry, then advances selectively through the company's operation. Chapter 22 rewinds again to the 1928 window project and must say so explicitly.

## Defining Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

> **After losing her Ford-garage job during the Depression and failing to find another, Mary Snyder chose in 1932 to put her own automobile into commercial service, obtain a chauffeur's license, and enter the taxi market rather than preserve the car for private use or continue waiting for wage employment.**

### Personally accepted responsibility and risk

The asset was Snyder's own car. Commercial use exposed it to mileage, wear, maintenance, accident, theft, uncertain fares, and personal safety risk. She also accepted operating responsibility by driving passengers herself. Later partnership expanded capacity but did not erase her exposure. A 1961 armed theft occurred long after market entry and may show the occupation's realized danger; it must not be projected backward as what she specifically anticipated in 1932.

### Safer alternative

The safer alternatives were to preserve the car as a household asset, continue seeking wage work, drive only informally, or delay entry until the Depression market improved. The Gazette account reports that she chose self-employment after being unable to find another job; it does not preserve a fuller deliberation.

## Supported Interpretation

Snyder's 1932 act is a genuine entrepreneurial market-entry decision: she converted an asset she controlled into a revenue-producing service and assumed direct responsibility for delivery. The later partnership, office building, depot routine, and continued operation after Jordan retired show that this was not a one-time informal fare.

Her case deepens women's authority more effectively than a second Anna Jacobs chapter because it preserves an attributable choice, an asset, a license, a partner, a company, operations, and a later exit. It does not make Snyder representative of all women entrepreneurs or all women workers.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What the Cedar Rapids area gave Snyder

Snyder's Cedar Rapids upbringing is documented, and the adjacent Cedar Rapids–Marion market supplied passengers and destinations. One reported nighttime passenger requested a ride into Cedar Rapids. The local transport ecology also included Marion's Milwaukee depot and jurisdictional rules governing where Cedar Rapids cab companies could operate in Marion. Those boundaries created market territory but do not make Mari-An a Cedar Rapids firm.

### What Snyder gave the Cedar Rapids area

Snyder and Jordan supplied woman-operated passenger service across municipal boundaries and to regional destinations. The article reports that women passengers welcomed woman drivers and that male passengers expressed surprise. Their company expanded practical mobility and demonstrated female operating authority in a service market involving licensing, vehicles, schedules, safety, customers, property, and long-distance travel.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Cedar Rapids connection versus Marion base:** Snyder belongs only as a Cedar Rapids-connected regional entrepreneur; company location must be stated accurately.
2. **Snyder protagonist versus Jordan partnership:** Anne Jordan cannot disappear from the company-building story.
3. **Own car versus joint company:** Snyder's 1932 entry is personal; later two-vehicle and office commitments are joint unless records show otherwise.
4. **Retrospective article versus contemporary records:** The 2020 Gazette account appears to draw on earlier reporting but is not a substitute for licenses, deeds, ledgers, or the 1937 article.
5. **Customer preference versus representativeness:** Reported approval from women passengers does not prove the views of all passengers.
6. **Courage versus hindsight:** Later longevity and sale do not prove success was visible in 1932.

## Human Stakes and Consequence

Snyder's car was both working capital and workplace; damage or breakdown could interrupt income. Driving alone with strangers created personal safety exposure. Customers depended on a driver and functioning vehicle, while the partners depended on enough fares to maintain two cars and eventually property. Jordan's retirement tested whether the enterprise could continue under Snyder's direct operation.

## Chapter Necessity

Chapter 21 is necessary because it adds a documented woman making a market-entry decision with an asset she controlled, rather than treating employment, association, or a surviving name as entrepreneurship. It shifts the movement from workers building collective authority to a woman authoring her own operating entry, while preserving collaboration and municipal market boundaries.

## Inheritance and Transition to Chapter 22

Snyder leaves a concrete operating lesson: a service becomes durable when a builder coordinates an asset, license, partner, routes, schedules, safety, customer trust, and property. Chapter 22 rewinds to 1928 and enlarges coordinated production from two vehicles and a service company to Wood's chain of assistant, borrowed industrial space, public commission, specialist bidder, overseas fabrication, supervision, and installation. This is an inherited capability in the book's argument, not a personal connection.
