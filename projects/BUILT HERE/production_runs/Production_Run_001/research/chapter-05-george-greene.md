## Qualified Interpretation

Greene's sequence of association with land, waterpower, steamboat investment, and railroad promotion supports a bounded interpretation that he kept pursuing the commercial problem of connection while changing technologies. It does not establish a private philosophy, a linear master plan, or a solitary shift from river to rail.

The strongest person-level interpretation is that Greene accepted participation in a collective rail proposition before the system's route, financing, construction, traffic, and usefulness were secure. Rail exchanged dependence on variable water for dependence on organized capital, rights-of-way, track, equipment, schedules, interchange, maintenance, and corporate operators.

## Conflicts and Tensions

1. **Chapter 2 versus Chapter 5:** Chapter 2 owns Greene's reported 1841 land entry. Chapter 5 owns later rail promotion. Reusing title ambiguity or the early purchase as the defining decision would duplicate the protagonist arc.
2. **Individual versus collective agency:** The repository calls Greene a railroad promoter but does not establish that he alone selected the route, supplied the decisive capital, granted Fourth Street, negotiated rights-of-way, or controlled construction.
3. **Dependability versus dependence:** Rail reduced seasonal transport limits while creating reliance on corporate infrastructure and decisions beyond local shippers' control.
4. **Arrival versus operating system:** A line reaching Cedar Rapids did not itself create dependable circulation; facilities, schedules, labor, rolling stock, maintenance, rates, and interchange still had to work.
5. **Sequence versus causation:** Later industrial concentration near rail demonstrates use of inherited circulation, not proof that Greene caused each location or enterprise.

The current repository supports Greene as a **participant-promoter**, not sole railroad builder. ## Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

### Defining decision

> After river transport exposed seasonal and operating limits, Greene chose to participate in promoting fixed rail connection before the route's capital, construction, traffic, and dependable usefulness were assured.

### Supported risk

- Capital and reputation were exposed to an infrastructure proposition requiring collective commitments and long lead times.
- Fixed track required rights-of-way, bridges, depots, equipment, maintenance, schedules, and operating agreements before nominal access became useful.
- Local firms could become dependent on rates, service, and corporate decisions outside their control.
- Greene's exact personal financial exposure is unknown and must not be quantified.

### Safer alternative

A structurally safer alternative was to continue using river transport and existing local routes, defer commitment until another promoter or city absorbed more of the uncertainty, or wait until a completed line demonstrated traffic. The record does not show Greene consciously enumerated these options.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

**What Cedar Rapids gave Greene:** A growing town with productive aspirations, river-based transport experience, merchants and mills needing wider markets, local collaborators, and a connection problem valuable enough to organize around.

**What Greene gave Cedar Rapids:** Bounded, collective promotion of a fixed external connection and continued willingness to participate before the network was secure. Do not upgrade this to sole authorship.

## Human Stakes

Merchants, manufacturers, farmers, shippers, railroad workers, and households could gain wider and more regular access, while bearing new dependence on rates, schedules, maintenance, and corporate control. Properties and firms near rail facilities gained advantages not evenly distributed across the city. The repository does not identify an individual shipper, worker, displaced owner, or household experience attributable to Greene's campaign; no representative scene may be invented.
