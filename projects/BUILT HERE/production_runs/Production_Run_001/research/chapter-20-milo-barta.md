# Research Record — Chapter 20: Milo Barta

## Scope and Evidence Posture

This record supports approved Production Run 001 Chapter 20, **Labor, Risk, and the Factory Bargain**. It addresses the architecture gate directly: whether Milo Barta can be treated as an **institutional entrepreneur** and whether his decision involved personally accepted risk. Repository summaries establish the founding act and workplace context but do not document a firing, demotion, threat, lost wage, family consequence, or financial investment specific to Barta. The distinction between accepted exposure and proven realized harm is controlling.

## Entrepreneur Classification, Agency, and Chapter Function

- **Protagonist:** Milo Barta, acting with Lewis Clark.
- **Classification:** **Institutional entrepreneur.** Barta did not own Wilson's or invest proprietary capital. He helped create a new durable organization by assembling worker participation, rules, affiliation pathways, and collective leverage where no local institution with that documented continuity existed.
- **Person-level agency:** Barta and Clark formed Federal Labor Union 18530 at Wilson's Cedar Rapids packinghouse on August 23, 1933.
- **Chapter function:** Show entrepreneurial institution-building without ownership: converting dispersed worker vulnerability into an organization capable of surviving its founders and contesting company authority.
- **Attribution limit:** Barta belongs at the founding hinge; later affiliations, recognition, agreements, strikes, grievances, and arbitration were collective achievements by later members and organizers.

## Defining Decision, Personally Accepted Risk, and Safer Alternative

The decision itself is established:

> **Barta chose with Lewis Clark to become an identifiable founder of Federal Labor Union 18530 on August 23, 1933, creating a durable organization to contest employer authority rather than relying on individual objection or a single protest.**

### Personal-risk gate

The workplace structure makes employment and income exposure plausible: Wilson controlled staffing, discipline, access to work, and pay, and Barta became a named founder of an organization contesting that authority. However, the inspected evidence does not show Barta's job or employment status on the founding date, a statement by him accepting livelihood risk, a contemporaneous employer response naming him, or a specific firing, demotion, threat, surveillance action, blacklisting, wage loss, or family consequence.

Accordingly, **personally accepted risk is not verified to the architecture's required standard**. It may be researched as a strong hypothesis, but it may not be converted into a PASS by treating structural plausibility as person-level proof. The record establishes institutional entrepreneurship and a courageous-decision candidate; it does not yet complete the personal-risk gate.

### Safer alternative

Barta could have endured conditions individually, left for other work, participated only in a temporary protest, supported organization without becoming a named founder, or waited for another organizer. No source records his deliberation; these are structural alternatives.

## Supported Interpretation

Barta qualifies as an institutional entrepreneur because he helped create a durable countervailing organization from resources that had to be assembled: participation, legitimacy, structure, affiliation, and willingness to act collectively. His “capital” is metaphorical only if clearly labeled; do not imply he owned worker participation or invested equity.

The union's strongest entrepreneurial feature was that it could outgrow its founders. Later members changed affiliation, won recognition, negotiated, struck, grieved, and used arbitration. Barta's agency is not diminished by refusing him credit for work others performed.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

### What Cedar Rapids gave Barta

Cedar Rapids concentrated a large packing operation, specialized labor, recurring workplace risk, immigrant and working-class networks, and employer authority in one place. Concentration made collective organization both necessary and possible. This does not imply city government sponsored the union.

### What Barta gave Cedar Rapids

Barta and Clark helped create a durable local vehicle for worker response. Bargaining, agreements, grievances, and arbitration became part of the city's industrial authority structure at one major plant. The local cannot stand for every Cedar Rapids worker, workplace, woman worker, or union.

## Conflicts and Tensions to Preserve

1. **Institutional entrepreneur versus business owner:** Barta built an organization without proprietary ownership.
2. **Personal exposure versus documented retaliation:** His founding role exposed livelihood and standing; no specific adverse action against him is established.
3. **Founder versus collective achievement:** Clark and later workers cannot be absorbed into a founder biography.
4. **Protection through risk:** Collective leverage depended on workers risking wages they sought to protect.
5. **Benefits versus authority:** Employer-provided welfare measures could be valuable without giving workers control over their terms.
6. **Durability versus incomplete coverage:** The institution could alter some outcomes without abolishing danger, layoffs, hierarchy, or unequal representation.

## Unknowns and Prohibited Inferences

- Barta's private motive, feelings, dialogue, ideology, family situation, finances, or full organizing career.
- Whether he was fired, demoted, threatened, monitored, blacklisted, denied promotion, or lost pay.
- His exact job, department, wage, seniority, dues, hours, citizenship status, or household obligations.
- A claim that he alone founded, led, or achieved every later stage of the union.
- A claim that Local 3 represented all Cedar Rapids labor or eliminated industrial danger.
- Exact local facts for nationally described strikes unless separately documented.

## Human Stakes and Consequence

Workers and households depended on paychecks while organization could put access to work and wages at risk. Management faced coordinated opposition and potentially interrupted production. The institution created procedures capable of placing rules, representation, and sometimes arbitration between one worker and unilateral company action. That consequence is meaningful without claiming inevitable victory.

## Chapter Necessity

Chapter 20 is necessary because it introduces **countervailing institutional entrepreneurship** into a sequence otherwise dominated by owners, manufacturers, and dealers. It changes the book's definition of building: durable capacity can be created by people who do not own the enterprise whose power they contest. Without the chapter, industrial growth would be narrated without the organized authority workers built inside it.

## Inheritance and Transition to Chapter 21

Barta leaves a method of coordinated operating authority: people pool participation, create rules, and build an organization that can survive any one founder. Chapter 21 should carry that capacity into a woman entrepreneur's documented market-entry and operating decision. No personal handoff or direct relationship should be invented.
