# Research Record — Chapter 11: Workers Build Neighborhoods

## Scope and Evidence Status

This Research-stage artifact is limited to Production Run 001, Chapter 11. The frozen architecture requires a documented person with agency over housing production: a developer, builder, lender, landlord, or household entrepreneur who converted land or capital into housing and accepted responsibility for the result.

Targeted external discovery resolved the person-level gate with **John T. Liddle**, replacing Anna Vild as protagonist while preserving the frozen housing slot. An inspected 1911 Linn County history states that Liddle invested in real estate, erected and sold many small homes for wage earners, and allowed purchasers who missed scheduled payments additional time rather than enforcing forfeiture. This is a Tier 3 local history rather than a deed, contract, ledger, or loan file, so exact properties, prices, buyers, terms, and dates remain publication-level research gaps. The source nevertheless documents production agency, sale/credit responsibility, accepted payment-delay exposure, a safer enforceable alternative, and consequences at person level.

## Chapter Function

- **Approved function:** Connect industrial wages, credit, land restrictions, construction, tenure, and neighborhood security to the scheduled movement examined in Chapter 12.
- **Book-arc function:** Test whether industrial opportunity could become durable household control rather than merely wages or nearby housing.
- **Required center:** One named person who made and bore responsibility for a housing-production decision.
- **What does not satisfy the function:** A neighborhood survey, an employer's payroll, a surviving cottage, physical proximity to a plant, or a deed restriction without proof of who financed, built, rented, sold, or operated the housing.

## Entrepreneur and Agency Test

### Replacement resolution: John T. Liddle

Luther A. Brewer and Barthinius L. Wick's 1911 *History of Linn County Iowa*, volume 2, describes John T. Liddle's Cedar Rapids business career. After retiring from mercantile work, he devoted attention to real-estate investment, “erected and sold many small homes for wage earners,” and granted time when purchasers could not make payments as scheduled. The account says many laboring men thereby retained homes that they otherwise would have forfeited.

**Agency finding:** Liddle directly passes the frozen housing-production gate. He supplied or controlled investment capital, caused small homes to be erected, sold them to wage earners, carried payment exposure, and chose forbearance rather than immediate forfeiture. The source does not identify the parcels, builders, purchasers, prices, instruments, or duration of extensions; those facts may not be invented.

### Anna Vild

The repository identifies Anna Vild with Lot 18, Block 2, Riverdale Addition and reports a restriction requiring a dwelling costing at least $1,600. The original instrument is not registered or inspected. The record does not establish her purchase price, financing, mortgage, building contract, final construction cost, occupancy, rental activity, sale, default, or outcome.

**Agency finding:** Vild is a named participant in a property transaction, but the present evidence does not show that she converted the lot into housing, financed or commissioned construction, operated a rental, accepted a documented household-enterprise obligation, or bore a traceable consequence. The restriction imposed an obligation; it does not prove she performed it. Anna Vild alone is therefore insufficient.

**Protagonist finding:** John T. Liddle replaces Anna Vild as the chapter protagonist. Vild remains useful restriction context but cannot carry the action chain.

## Chronology

- John T. Liddle came to Cedar Rapids in the early 1870s and remained active in mercantile and manufacturing enterprises until retiring from active commercial work in 1892, according to the inspected 1911 history.
- After that retirement he pursued real-estate investment and erected and sold small homes for wage earners. The source does not date individual projects; the activity is bounded between his 1892 retirement and his death on July 3, 1909.

- Sinclair & Company acquired its sixteen-acre packinghouse site in February 1872, according to the repository summary.
- The packinghouse payroll and workforce grew during the 1870s; these facts establish housing demand context, not a particular person's housing enterprise.
- A west-side bridge was completed in 1875, with public and private contributions reported; no household use or housing-production decision follows automatically.
- Preservation-study summaries describe west-side household composition in 1880, 1895, 1900, and 1920.
- Anna Vild's Riverdale Addition transaction belongs to the addition era, but the exact instrument date remains unresolved in the repository.
- Cottage surveys cited in the prior draft date from 1994, 2000, and 2006 and describe or interpret earlier buildings; later survey observations cannot be projected backward without underlying records.

The chapter may use Liddle's bounded 1892–1909 housing activity without assigning an exact year to any individual house or payment extension.

## Qualified Interpretations

- Liddle joined housing production to an installment-credit relationship: his responsibility continued beyond construction and sale because he could press for payment or carry delay.
- Forbearance exposed Liddle to delayed or uncertain receipts. The evidence supports payment risk, not a quantified loss, default rate, mortgage structure, or charitable motive.
- The enforceable safer alternative is unusually concrete: the source says purchasers otherwise would have forfeited their homes if pressed for payment. It does not establish whether Liddle considered every extension in the same way.
- The reported construction minimum made Vild's lot more demanding than land without such a restriction, but the record does not show whether she accepted, fulfilled, transferred, or failed that obligation.
- Industrial wages may have supported rent or ownership, but aggregate payroll cannot establish any household's income, creditworthiness, mortgage, or tenure.
- Restrictions may reflect a developer's attempt to control minimum improvement value. This remains an interpretation until the grantor, instrument language, and enforcement history are recovered.
- Worker neighborhoods emerged through distributed action among landowners, developers, builders, lenders, landlords, public bodies, employers, and households. That structural truth cannot substitute for one protagonist's documented decision.
- Ownership could expose a household to capital and income risk; renting could expose a household to another party's terms. The repository does not establish which path Vild took.

## Conflicts and Evidence Boundaries

1. **Documented enterprise versus transaction detail:** The 1911 history establishes Liddle's production, sale, and forbearance pattern, but not the identity or terms of any individual transaction.
2. **Named transaction versus demonstrated enterprise:** Vild's name and lot are concrete; her housing-production action and outcome are not.
3. **Survey attribution versus original records:** A later survey calls cottages Sinclair rental housing, but transaction-level evidence is absent.
4. **Physical survival versus household continuity:** A surviving house proves a structure endured, not that one family owned, occupied, or benefited from it continuously.
5. **Proximity versus participation:** Nearby factories, churches, schools, and community institutions do not prove employment, membership, use, or equal access.
6. **Benevolent language versus motive:** The local history praises Liddle. The chapter may use the reported conduct and consequence, but it must not convert promotional character judgments into private motive.

Downstream work must not claim Vild built a house, obtained a mortgage, operated a rental, achieved ownership, or suffered loss. It must not identify a specific Liddle house or buyer, quantify his investment or loss, call his terms affordable, or claim altruistic motive without direct evidence. It must not call the cottages subsidized, paternalistic, coercive, charitable, or affordable without direct evidence.

## Courage Decision, Risk, and Safer Alternative

The defensible person-level decision is bounded:

> **After leaving active mercantile work, John T. Liddle chose to invest in, erect, and sell small Cedar Rapids homes for wage earners and, when purchasers missed scheduled payments, to carry the delay rather than press an available forfeiture.**

- **Accepted risk:** Real-estate and construction capital plus delayed or uncertain receipts from purchasers. No amount, duration, default rate, or realized loss is known.
- **Safer alternative:** Press payment and enforce forfeiture. Unlike a hypothetical alternative, this one is explicit in the source's statement that purchasers would otherwise have forfeited their homes if pressed.
- **Consequence:** The source attributes home retention by many laboring men to Liddle's forbearance. Individual buyers and durability of their tenure remain unknown.
- **Present prohibition:** Do not manufacture courage from a restriction imposed on Vild or from praise of Liddle's character.

## Cedar Rapids Exchange

**What Cedar Rapids offered:** Industrial employment, wage earners seeking homes, platted land, bridges, transportation, builders, lenders, institutions, and a growing housing need. These created a market but did not guarantee payment or household security.

**What the documented entrepreneur gave Cedar Rapids:** Liddle converted real-estate investment into small homes for wage earners and carried some purchasers through missed payment dates rather than enforcing forfeiture. The evidence supports a housing-and-credit pathway, not a map of its scale or an affordability claim.

## Inheritance and Transition

The approved transition remains usable only in conditional form. Verified housing agency would leave households and workers with a more durable relationship to place, while exposing how security depended on wages, credit, restrictions, construction, and tenure. Chapter 12 would then test whether scheduled movement enlarged the usable reach of those neighborhoods.

Do not imply that Anna Vild personally handed anything to W. J. Greene or that housing caused transit investment. The inherited condition is a city with growing residential and industrial separation that made dependable movement more consequential.
