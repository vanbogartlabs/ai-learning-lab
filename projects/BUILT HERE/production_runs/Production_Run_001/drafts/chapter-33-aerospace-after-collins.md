# Chapter 33: Aerospace After Collins

Cedar Rapids retains adaptable industrial capability but not demonstrated final local control over its use. In 2025 and 2026, Collins Aerospace/RTX received an $80 million Black Hawk avionics award and a $438 million FAA radar award connected to Cedar Rapids; Nathan Boelkins and Jenny Miller supplied leadership voices, but the record identifies no personal bid, product, capital, site, or staffing authorization. <!-- EIQ-33-C01 -->

The later award came into public view in January 2026. RTX announced a $438 million FAA Radar System Replacement contract from Cedar Rapids, and Nathan “Nate” Boelkins appeared as president of Collins Aerospace's Avionics business. His presence made current portfolio leadership visible. The announcement did not say that he authorized the proposal, selected the radar solution, committed capital, assigned local work, or accepted a specific downside. <!-- EIQ-33-C02 -->

Ten months earlier, in March 2025, Collins Aerospace had announced an $80 million U.S. Army award for the H-60M MOSA Avionics Architecture Solution program. The solution used the Mosarc modular open-systems product family, and announced work would occur in Cedar Rapids and Huntsville. The release did not divide tasks, dollars, staffing, or duration between those locations. <!-- EIQ-33-C03 -->

Jenny Miller, vice president and general manager of Military Avionics and Helicopters, publicly explained Mosarc's intended capability in connection with that award. Her words placed her close to a specific program proposition. They did not establish that she selected Mosarc, authorized the bid, funded the product, assigned Cedar Rapids work, or controlled the customer decision. Explanation is evidence of program proximity, not proof of product authority. <!-- EIQ-33-C04 -->

The two awards establish scale and renewed customer selection. They do not establish delivery, fielding, local job creation or retention, capital investment, supplier spending, schedule performance, or a person-linked result. Award values belong to the corporate contracts; they cannot become Cedar Rapids totals. Opportunity is consequential without being confused with completed execution. <!-- EIQ-33-C05 -->

That boundary is central because aerospace publicity shows the endpoints of a process more clearly than its interior. A customer selects a proposition. A president or program leader may speak publicly. Between those visible points sit capture management, engineering, pricing, proposal review, product governance, executive approval, customer evaluation, site allocation, fabrication, integration, installation, acceptance, and sustainment. The current record does not identify who held each decision right. <!-- EIQ-33-C06 -->

The responsible acting subject is therefore institutional. Collins Aerospace/RTX pursued and accepted obligations associated with current Black Hawk avionics and FAA radar work. Boelkins supplied broad Avionics stewardship in public; Miller supplied a program-adjacent technical voice. Neither person can be credited with winning the awards or with the undisclosed chain that produced them. <!-- EIQ-33-C07 -->

Boelkins's official profile describes him as leading an Avionics business of more than 15,000 people. That is broad portfolio scope, not a Cedar Rapids headcount. The profile also associates his prior work with portfolio growth, strategic planning, and advanced product development. Those responsibilities make him relevant to stewardship, but they do not map automatically onto either named award or prove one attributable commitment. <!-- EIQ-33-C08 -->

His exposure is correspondingly bounded. Leading a regulated, customer-dependent technical portfolio carries public and professional responsibility for difficult execution. Miller's role creates similar program-adjacent accountability. The evidence does not establish either executive's personal financial exposure or a personally accepted bid, schedule, product, staffing, site, or performance wager. Institutional obligations cannot be converted into private courage by title. <!-- EIQ-33-C09 -->

At corporate scale, the analytical alternatives were real even though no deliberation is preserved. Collins/RTX could have declined to bid, offered a different architecture, deferred investment, allocated work elsewhere, bought capability, or relied on legacy products. These possibilities explain the renewal test. They do not support a scene in which Boelkins or Miller weighs and rejects them. <!-- EIQ-33-C10 -->

The Collins name can obscure that test. Cedar Rapids inherited deep technical associations from Arthur Collins and decades of later corporate development, but a heritage name does not issue a new customer award. Existing skill becomes current only when engineers, program teams, suppliers, corporate reviewers, and customers connect it to a proposition selected now. The 2025 and 2026 awards show renewed opportunity, not self-renewing prestige. <!-- EIQ-33-C11 -->

Cedar Rapids supplied accumulated engineering, program-management, manufacturing, supplier, test, maintenance, and customer-support capability. Official announcement language publicly connected the city to current work. That connection is meaningful but incomplete: it does not disclose the local work package, workforce effect, dollar share, capital commitment, or duration. Local relevance must remain distinct from a local denominator the sources do not provide. <!-- EIQ-33-C12 -->

The larger enterprise supplied access to national customers, product platforms, certification and program systems, capital, and a global workforce. Those systems can turn local skill into part of a major proposition. They can also allocate tasks and authority beyond local view. Cedar Rapids benefits from membership in the larger system while depending on decisions the public award record does not locate. <!-- EIQ-33-C13 -->

Mosarc makes the contradiction unusually sharp. Its proposition is modular and open in a technical sense, designed to accommodate third-party capability. Yet an open architecture does not create open governance evidence. Product modularity says nothing by itself about who approved the bid, selected the architecture, priced the work, assigned sites, or controlled staffing. Technical openness and organizational opacity can coexist. <!-- EIQ-33-C14 -->

The human stakes extend far beyond the visible executives. Engineers and technicians must design and integrate systems; program staff coordinate requirements and schedules; suppliers contribute components and services; customer crews and airspace users depend on performance and safety; taxpayers have a stake in cost and execution. None of those interests can be reduced to an award ceremony or assigned a specific outcome before delivery evidence exists. <!-- EIQ-33-C15 -->

This distributed chain is not a reason to drain the chapter of people. It is a reason to place people accurately. Boelkins represents responsibility at portfolio scale. Miller explains a named military-avionics proposition. Other actors, many unnamed in the public packet, carry capture, technical, commercial, site, and execution work. Stewardship means keeping the chain accountable without claiming one leader authored it all. <!-- EIQ-33-C16 -->

Nor should the chapter become corporate advertising. An award announces customer selection and contract opportunity. It does not prove that a system has been delivered, accepted, fielded, or sustained. It does not guarantee the announced work will remain in the same locations for the same duration, and it does not establish local employment effects. The future obligations are part of the award's meaning precisely because performance remains ahead. <!-- EIQ-33-C17 -->

The city's technical inheritance is therefore conditional. Buildings, tools, expertise, suppliers, and institutional memory may persist, but they become economically and publicly useful through assignment. Current customers must select current propositions, and the corporation must route work through actual teams and sites. A surviving capability without renewed work can become a name; renewed work without execution can remain a promise. <!-- EIQ-33-C18 -->

Boelkins and Miller make that conditionality legible at two levels. One speaks from broad Avionics leadership in connection with the FAA award; the other speaks near a specific Black Hawk/Mosarc program. Their different positions should not be flattened into co-authorship of one choice. They show layers of public stewardship inside an institution whose exact internal authorization remains undisclosed. <!-- EIQ-33-C19 -->

The chapter's courage claim must remain institutional and qualified. Collins Aerospace/RTX accepted the obligations attached to pursuing and performing awarded work in demanding technical environments. Boelkins and Miller accepted bounded public accountability in roles connected to those obligations. The evidence does not establish a personal courageous decision for either one, and responsible narrative does not need to invent it. <!-- EIQ-33-C20 -->

From the ADM chapter, the control problem has changed industries but not disappeared. Corn-processing assets and aerospace skill can both remain locally useful while assignment authority sits within a global owner and customer system. There is no Cuddy–Boelkins handoff and no evidence of learning between the enterprises. The continuity is analytical: local capability depends on choices made through larger structures. <!-- EIQ-33-C21 -->

The chronology must now be handled carefully. This chapter ends in the contemporary frame of 2025 and 2026. The next chapter rewinds to the pandemic and derecho of 2020, and from global aerospace scale to one new local pub. That reset is not a personal passage from Boelkins or Miller to Amy Winker. It compares how retained capability receives a usable assignment under shock at radically different scales. <!-- EIQ-33-C22 -->

The awards leave Cedar Rapids with verified present opportunity and unresolved future performance. The city remains publicly connected to difficult technical work, but neither the Collins name nor a visible executive can guarantee the next customer choice. Capability stays consequential only through repeated selection, corporate assignment, distributed execution, and accountable renewal. <!-- EIQ-33-C23 -->

Customer selection is only one threshold. After award, technical teams still must convert a proposition into systems that meet requirements through integration, testing, installation, acceptance, and sustainment. The evidence packet does not trace those later stages for either program. That absence keeps the awards from becoming premature victory scenes and locates the corporation's responsibility in work still to be performed, not merely in publicity already achieved. <!-- EIQ-33-C24 -->

The location language is similarly prospective. Cedar Rapids and Huntsville were announced as places of Black Hawk work, but the release did not divide the work between them. The FAA release's Cedar Rapids dateline connected the city publicly to Avionics leadership and radar opportunity without supplying a local allocation. Responsible place writing can preserve that association while refusing to turn a dateline into a workforce, investment, or task count. <!-- EIQ-33-C25 -->

Distributed authority also protects a more accurate understanding of innovation. A modular architecture may involve years of product development, customer learning, engineering judgment, commercial review, and program coordination before an award appears. The current sources do not identify those contributions individually. Crediting the visible president or quoted program leader with the whole result would erase the very institutional capability the chapter is trying to explain. <!-- EIQ-33-C26 -->

Stewardship, then, is less a claim of personal authorship than a duty to keep specialized capacity governable and available for current work. Boelkins's portfolio role makes that duty visible at one level; Miller's product explanation makes it visible closer to a program. The chapter can interpret both as public responsibility while keeping the undisclosed choices—bid, investment, architecture, site, and staffing—outside their attributed acts. <!-- EIQ-33-C27 -->

The two award values widen the stakes without resolving them. Large contracts can support opportunity and impose demanding obligations, but their face values do not reveal profit, local share, expenditure timing, or realized performance. Their safest narrative function is comparative scale: current customers were selecting consequential propositions associated publicly with Cedar Rapids, and the institution now had to make those selections real through distributed execution. <!-- EIQ-33-C28 -->

This is how technical inheritance differs from an artifact kept behind glass. Skill remains alive only in use, and use requires repeated commitments from customers, companies, teams, and sites. No single award secures permanence. The chapter's present-tense evidence shows renewal underway while preserving the uncertainty that gives renewal meaning: future work must still be won, assigned, executed, and trusted again. <!-- EIQ-33-C29 -->

Renewal also distributes credit across time. Current teams work with accumulated knowledge, existing products, corporate systems, and customer relationships they did not create alone. Their responsibility is not diminished by inheritance; it is defined by what they make usable now. The award record supports that institutional continuity while leaving individual authorship unresolved. <!-- EIQ-33-C30 -->

Cedar Rapids retains technical skill whose usefulness depends on renewed customer selection and corporate assignment.
