# Chapter 24 — The Arsenal of Skills

In 1942, Collins Radio enlarged its plant at 855 Thirty-Fifth Street and occupied leased locations around Cedar Rapids. Wartime capacity came not from one spectacular machine or one heroic shift, but from scattering work across more space, people, and systems. At the same time, procurement authorities could shape requirements, materials, prices, labor matters, inspection, and even the conditions under which work ended. The young company gained reach by entering a structure of dependence.

That was Arthur Collins’s second consequential company decision. During the Depression years, he had turned specialized radio practice into an organization with a name, records, customers, roles, and legal obligations. By the beginning of the 1940s, that organization had crossed into dedicated plant capacity. It could receive demands larger than one person’s bench. It had not yet proved that it could reproduce technical quality through many hands and in many places.

The pressure of wartime work made that distinction decisive. An order could create demand without creating the capacity to fulfill it. More work required room, but room alone was not production. Every added section needed people, materials, schedules, supervision, records, inspection, and movement. Every leased location increased what the company could attempt while multiplying the points at which coordination could fail.

Collins chose enlargement. The company added to its Thirty-Fifth Street plant and used other locations around the city rather than keeping all work concentrated at its existing base. The exact authorizations, lease signatures, costs, financing, and board actions are not established in the approved record. What is established is the physical direction of the commitment and Collins’s founder-level accountability for it: the company would perform communications work at organizational scale.

There were structurally safer routes. Collins Radio could have accepted less work, subcontracted more of it, or expanded less. Concentration at the existing plant would have reduced the number of sites to coordinate. Those alternatives are analytical, not a recovered list from Collins. They would also have limited the company’s ability to execute larger assignments. The choice was not between danger and safety so much as between bounded exposure and a larger capability burden.

That burden began with fixed space. Plant additions can remain after an order ends. Leased capacity can offer flexibility, but rent, movement, duplication, and supervision continue as operating obligations. Wartime demand could make those commitments rational in 1942 without guaranteeing their usefulness under a different demand regime. Collins accepted a company whose physical footprint could outlast the customer pressure that had called it forth.

The archive’s 1941–1942 building-addition files and its 1942 plant survey establish that original records exist for this phase. Their titles do not disclose cost, design, authorization, occupancy, or the use of each room. They do reveal the category of problem. A company that once had to make skill durable now had to make space legible: what work belonged where, what moved between locations, and what capacity could be trusted.

A plant survey is therefore more than a building detail. It marks the point at which technical ambition becomes an allocation problem. Floor area, equipment, people, and workflow have to be arranged so that one stage of work does not defeat another. When operations spill into leased sites, the problem becomes distributed. A part, specification, record, or decision may need to cross the city before the finished equipment can cross an inspector’s threshold.

The approved evidence does not identify every leased address, landlord, department, or transport method. Inventing them would produce a false tour of wartime Cedar Rapids. The supported picture is both plainer and more consequential: local space allowed Collins Radio to expand before a single purpose-built campus could contain the work, and that flexibility came with coordination costs whose exact mechanics still require archival inspection.

People made the enlarged footprint function. Engineering, production, inspection, purchasing, clerical work, maintenance, training, and management were structurally necessary to an organization operating across more capacity. The record does not assign each role to named individuals or establish the experience of any particular worker. It does establish that scale redistributed execution. Arthur Collins could accept responsibility for enlargement; he could not personally perform all the work that made enlargement useful.

That distinction protects his agency rather than diminishing it. Collins is the protagonist because he committed the young organization to a form of performance that depended on other people’s judgment. Founder responsibility became less like doing every task and more like making an institution answer for the relationship among tasks. The larger the company grew, the more consequential its routines became—and the less honest it would be to describe every accomplishment as Collins’s alone.

The archive inventory makes those routines visible through record classes. Organization charts point toward formal allocation of authority. Employee and salary materials point toward recruitment, classification, compensation, and administration. A women’s-earnings file indicates that the archive contains records in that category, but it does not establish which jobs women held, whether pay was equal, who advanced, or who remained after the war. First-aid reports likewise identify a record class, not an injury rate or an individual story.

Those limits matter because wartime expansion is often narrated as a crowd scene: workers flood into a plant, production rises, and urgency supposedly resolves every difference. The surviving categories suggest the opposite. Growth created questions that required administration. Who was hired? How was work classified? What rules governed pay? How was safety handled? Which authority could settle a labor dispute? The available folder titles cannot answer those questions in detail, but they prevent scale from being mistaken for simplicity.

National War Labor Board matters place the company’s employment practices inside a broader wartime system. The approved record does not establish the outcome of a particular Collins matter, so no dispute can be reconstructed. The significance is structural. Hiring and compensation were not entirely private arrangements between founder and worker. Public rules and institutions could enter the relationship, adding another authority to an already distributed organization.

The same was true of prices. Navy price-adjustment records and Treasury rulings identify administrative dimensions of public work. They do not prove a particular price, refund, tax result, or conflict. They show that producing acceptable equipment did not end the company’s obligations. Cost, price, accounting, and settlement could remain subject to review after engineering and fabrication had done their part.

This was customer-shaped scale. Wartime procurement could govern specifications, scarce materials, inspection, facilities, completion, cancellation, and settlement through distinct agencies and contracts. “The government” was not one customer making one decision. Different authorities could control different parts of the relationship. A requirement might define what had to be built; material allocation could constrain how it was built; inspection could determine whether it was accepted; price review could reshape what the work yielded.

For Collins Radio, the business problem was to translate those externally set conditions into repeatable internal action. Specifications had to reach the people who needed them. Materials had to arrive, be accounted for, and become usable components. Work had to be checked before an outside inspector judged it. Records had to connect performance to payment and settlement. The company was privately organized, but much of the operating environment was not privately chosen.

That dependence should not be confused with passivity. Collins’s decision mattered because the organization had to respond. Public demand did not automatically create a capable supplier. Procurement rules did not arrange the plant, train workers, coordinate locations, preserve quality, or maintain records. They set conditions within which Collins Radio’s employees and managers had to build an operating answer.

Mobilization schedules make that answer tangible at the record-class level. A schedule assumes that time, materials, people, and sequence can be coordinated. Spare-parts files extend responsibility beyond an initial object: equipment placed in service can create a continuing need for support. Neither folder title proves a particular schedule was met or a particular part delivered. Together, they show how wartime capacity widened the definition of completion.

The customer needed functioning communications equipment, not merely technical promise. That requirement exposed everyone in the system. Workers and managers had to produce to specifications. Suppliers had to provide usable inputs. Landlords had to make space available. Inspectors had to judge compliance. Public officials administered contracts and rules. Collins Radio had to coordinate these relationships without possessing complete authority over any of them.

The human stakes were distributed as well. Employees and households depended on recruitment, classification, training, pay, hours, and continued orders. Suppliers depended on purchases and payment. Customers depended on equipment meeting requirements. Collins faced organizational, contractual, technical, and reputational exposure as the founder whose name remained attached to performance. Exact wages, household circumstances, injuries, promotions, contract values, and personal cash exposure remain unknown.

Resisting unsupported specificity does not make those stakes abstract. A company dispersed into added and leased capacity had more promises to keep. A defect could affect acceptance. A delayed material could idle work. An ended order could strand space and people. No invented factory scene is needed to understand the pressure. The operating system itself placed livelihoods, public requirements, and the young company’s standing in relation to one another.

Cedar Rapids made the commitment possible in practical ways. The city supplied the recently built plant, leasable industrial and commercial space, utilities, transport, labor, suppliers, and institutions accustomed to distant markets. Its existing industrial vocabulary—moving materials, organizing shifts, maintaining equipment, keeping records—offered capabilities a radio company could draw upon. The evidence does not identify precisely who supplied each resource or on what terms.

The city did not remove the risk. Multiple local sites solved an immediate space problem while creating a movement and management problem. Labor availability did not guarantee training or quality. Utilities and transport did not guarantee materials. A city could provide conditions for expansion without deciding whether the enlarged company would perform, whether orders would continue, or whether the capacity would remain useful afterward.

Collins Radio, in turn, gave Cedar Rapids more than wartime employment in the generic sense. It expanded demand for technical, production, inspection, clerical, purchasing, maintenance, training, and managerial capability. It developed routines for coordinating specifications, quality, labor administration, records, support, and locations. The exact distribution of opportunity remains unresolved, and the institution’s contribution must remain collective.

The defining decision can now be stated without founder worship. Arthur Collins chose to enlarge fixed and leased capacity in 1942 so that a young company could perform wartime communications work at organizational scale. He accepted that the work would become more dependent on employees, managers, landlords, suppliers, inspectors, public agencies, and procurement rules. His courage lay not in knowing demand would last, but in becoming accountable for a system whose success he could not produce alone.

The choice changed Collins as an entrepreneur. During formation, he had made technical practice answerable through a company. In wartime expansion, he made the company answerable through distributed systems. The first decision created an entity beyond his own bench. The second required him to trust and organize capability beyond one facility and beyond his direct action.

That progression also changed what founder control could mean. In a small practice, Collins’s own technical judgment could reach much of the work directly. In a dispersed organization, authority had to travel through roles, charts, schedules, specifications, inspections, and records. A founder could set direction and remain accountable while still depending on decisions made elsewhere. Control became less a matter of touching the work and more a matter of maintaining connections among people whose competence mattered independently.

The distinction is important to the book’s larger argument about building. Enlargement is often presented as the reward for an earlier risk: first prove the company, then enjoy growth. Collins’s 1942 choice shows enlargement as a new wager. The routines that made the young business durable were necessary but insufficient. They now had to carry quality across added distance, greater specialization, and rules administered by institutions with their own purposes.

The wager also spread responsibility without distributing it equally. Procurement authorities could influence materials, price, inspection, and termination. Managers could allocate work. Inspectors could reject it. Workers could contribute knowledge that the organization needed. Collins remained answerable at the founder level, but he did not possess each actor’s authority or skill. The company’s strength depended on keeping those differences coordinated rather than pretending they disappeared inside one name.

This is why the least glamorous archive categories carry so much narrative weight. An organization chart, salary file, price-adjustment matter, spare-parts record, or mobilization schedule marks a place where technical purpose met another obligation. None supplies a complete story by title alone. Taken as categories, they reveal the organization Collins had chosen to become: one in which engineering could succeed only if administration, labor, supply, inspection, accounting, and support also held.

It also changed the nature of uncertainty. Before expansion, the question was whether the young organization could meet larger demands. After expansion, the question was what would happen to the facilities, workforce, administrative systems, and support routines if the demand regime changed. Capability and dependence had grown together. Success at wartime work could make the postwar problem larger, not smaller.

That is why this chapter cannot end with a production victory. The approved record does not supply a single total, contract, or triumphant completion that could settle the decision. More important, fulfillment of wartime demand would not answer whether distributed capacity had a durable civilian or peacetime purpose. The customer that accelerated organizational development retained the power to reduce, cancel, terminate, or settle its work.

The arsenal Cedar Rapids gained was therefore an arsenal of skills and systems, not merely products. Space had been allocated. People had been organized. Quality had to travel across roles and locations. Administrative records connected labor, prices, materials, inspection, schedules, and support. Those capabilities could be reused, but their reuse was not guaranteed by the conditions that created them.

Arthur Collins had chosen the fixed and leased capacity that made this transformation possible. Yet the resulting institution belonged in operation to a much wider group. Its value now resided in coordinated knowledge distributed across facilities, workforce, records, managers, and customer relationships. Its vulnerability resided in exactly the same distribution: remove orders, materials, working capital, or governing permission, and no founder’s technical authority could substitute for the missing system.

The company entered the war as a durable young organization and emerged from the 1942 commitment with a harder inheritance. It had learned to reproduce technical work through many people and places under rules set partly outside itself. The next question was not whether Collins could choose scale again. It was whether the institution created by customer-shaped scale could survive beyond that customer’s demand.

Distributed technical capacity—facilities, workforce, administration, quality routines, and customer coordination—that must survive the wartime demand regime.
