# Chapter 33: Aerospace After Collins

Cedar Rapids retains adaptable industrial capability but not demonstrated final local control over its use. In January 2026, RTX announced from Cedar Rapids a $438 million FAA Radar System Replacement award, with Nathan “Nate” Boelkins speaking as president of Collins Aerospace's Avionics business. In 2025 and 2026, Collins Aerospace/RTX received an $80 million Black Hawk avionics award and a $438 million FAA radar award connected to Cedar Rapids; Nathan Boelkins and Jenny Miller supplied leadership voices, but the record identifies no personal bid, product, capital, site, or staffing authorization. <!-- EIQ-33-C01 -->

The FAA announcement made current customer selection and Boelkins's portfolio leadership visible. It did not say that he authorized the proposal, selected the radar solution, committed capital, assigned local work, or accepted a specific downside. <!-- EIQ-33-C02 --> Ten months earlier, Collins Aerospace had announced the $80 million U.S. Army award for the H-60M MOSA Avionics Architecture Solution, using the Mosarc modular open-systems family. Work was announced for Cedar Rapids and Huntsville without any division of tasks, dollars, staffing, or duration. <!-- EIQ-33-C03 -->

Jenny Miller, vice president and general manager of Military Avionics and Helicopters, publicly explained Mosarc's intended capability. Her program proximity did not establish that she selected Mosarc, authorized the bid, funded the product, assigned Cedar Rapids work, or controlled the customer decision. <!-- EIQ-33-C04 --> Together the awards establish scale, renewed selection, and future obligation—not delivery, fielding, local jobs or retention, capital investment, supplier spending, schedule performance, or a person-linked result. Their face values belong to corporate contracts, not Cedar Rapids totals. <!-- EIQ-33-C05 -->

Aerospace publicity reveals endpoints more clearly than the work between them. Customer selection and a leader's public statement sit around capture management, engineering, pricing, proposal review, product governance, executive approval, customer evaluation, site allocation, fabrication, integration, installation, acceptance, and sustainment. The specific decision rights remain undisclosed. <!-- EIQ-33-C06 --> Collins Aerospace/RTX is therefore the acting subject, institutionally pursuing and accepting the Black Hawk and FAA obligations. Boelkins supplies portfolio-level public stewardship; Miller supplies program-adjacent technical explanation. Neither can be credited with winning the awards or authoring the hidden chain. <!-- EIQ-33-C07 -->

Boelkins's profile describes leadership of an Avionics business of more than 15,000 people and prior responsibilities in portfolio growth, strategic planning, and advanced product development. That is broad business-unit scope, not a Cedar Rapids headcount or proof of a discrete commitment to either award. <!-- EIQ-33-C08 --> His role, and Miller's, carries public and professional accountability for difficult execution, but no personal financial exposure or personally accepted bid, schedule, product, staffing, site, or performance wager is established. Institutional obligation cannot become private courage by title. <!-- EIQ-33-C09 -->

Collins/RTX could analytically have declined to bid, offered another architecture, deferred investment, allocated work elsewhere, bought capability, or relied on legacy products. No recovered deliberation assigns those alternatives to Boelkins or Miller. <!-- EIQ-33-C10 --> Nor could the Collins name renew capability by itself. Existing skill became current only when engineers, program teams, suppliers, corporate reviewers, and customers connected it to a proposition selected now. The awards show renewed opportunity, not self-renewing prestige. <!-- EIQ-33-C11 -->

Cedar Rapids supplied accumulated engineering, program-management, manufacturing, supplier, test, maintenance, and customer-support capability. The announcements connected the city to current work without disclosing local packages, workforce effects, dollar share, capital commitment, or duration. <!-- EIQ-33-C12 --> The larger enterprise supplied national customers, product platforms, certification and program systems, capital, and a global workforce. Those systems can turn local skill into a major proposition while allocating tasks and authority beyond local view. <!-- EIQ-33-C13 -->

Mosarc sharpens the distinction: a modular, technically open architecture intended to accommodate third-party capability does not create open governance evidence. Product modularity says nothing by itself about who approved the bid, selected the architecture, priced the work, assigned sites, or controlled staffing. <!-- EIQ-33-C14 --> The obligations reach engineers and technicians, program staff, suppliers, customer crews, airspace users, and taxpayers, whose interests in safety, cost, schedule, and performance cannot be reduced to award publicity or assigned an outcome before delivery. <!-- EIQ-33-C15 -->

Distributed work does not drain the story of people; it places them accurately. Boelkins represents portfolio responsibility, Miller explains a named proposition, and unnamed actors carry capture, technical, commercial, site, and execution work. Stewardship means keeping that chain accountable without claiming one leader authored it all. <!-- EIQ-33-C16 --> An award is customer selection and contract opportunity, not proof that a system has been delivered, accepted, fielded, or sustained, that work will remain in the same places for the same duration, or that local employment has changed. Performance remains ahead. <!-- EIQ-33-C17 -->

The city's inheritance is conditional. Buildings, tools, expertise, suppliers, and institutional memory become useful when customers select current propositions and the corporation routes work through actual teams and sites. Capability without work can become a name; selection without execution can remain a promise. <!-- EIQ-33-C18 --> Boelkins and Miller make that condition legible at different levels—broad Avionics leadership and a specific Black Hawk/Mosarc explanation—without becoming co-authors of one choice. <!-- EIQ-33-C19 --> Collins Aerospace/RTX accepted the institutional obligations of awarded work; its visible leaders accepted bounded public accountability. No personal courageous decision is established for either. <!-- EIQ-33-C20 -->

The control problem resembles ADM's only analytically. Corn-processing assets and aerospace skill can remain locally useful while assignment authority lies within global owners and customer systems. There is no Cuddy–Boelkins handoff or evidence of learning between enterprises. <!-- EIQ-33-C21 -->

The chronology must now be handled carefully. This chapter ends in the contemporary frame of 2025 and 2026. The next chapter rewinds to the pandemic and derecho of 2020, and from global aerospace scale to one new local pub. That reset is not a personal passage from Boelkins or Miller to Amy Winker. It compares how retained capability receives a usable assignment under shock at radically different scales. <!-- EIQ-33-C22 -->

The awards leave Cedar Rapids with verified present opportunity and unresolved performance. Neither the Collins name nor a visible executive guarantees another customer choice; capability stays consequential through repeated selection, corporate assignment, distributed execution, and accountable renewal. <!-- EIQ-33-C23 --> Customer selection is only the first threshold. Technical teams still owe integration, testing, installation, acceptance, and sustainment, stages the available material does not trace for either program. Responsibility lies in work still unperformed, not publicity already achieved. <!-- EIQ-33-C24 -->

Location language remains prospective. Cedar Rapids and Huntsville were named for Black Hawk work without a division between them; the FAA release's Cedar Rapids dateline connected the city to radar opportunity without a local allocation. A dateline cannot become a task, workforce, or investment count. <!-- EIQ-33-C25 --> Nor should visible executives receive credit for years of product development, customer learning, engineering judgment, commercial review, and program coordination whose contributors are not individually identified. That would erase the institutional capability being renewed. <!-- EIQ-33-C26 -->

Stewardship is the duty to keep specialized capacity governable and available for current work, not a claim of personal authorship. Boelkins makes that duty visible at portfolio level and Miller nearer a program; bid, investment, architecture, site, and staffing choices remain outside their attributed acts. <!-- EIQ-33-C27 --> The award values widen the stakes while revealing neither profit, local share, expenditure timing, nor realized performance. Their defensible function is comparative scale: customers selected consequential propositions, and distributed teams must now make them real. <!-- EIQ-33-C28 -->

Skill remains alive only in use. No award secures permanence; future work must still be won, assigned, executed, accepted, and trusted again. <!-- EIQ-33-C29 --> Current teams also inherit knowledge, products, systems, and customer relationships they did not create alone. Their responsibility is defined by what they make usable now, while individual authorship remains unresolved. <!-- EIQ-33-C30 -->

Cedar Rapids retains technical skill whose usefulness depends on renewed customer selection and corporate assignment.
