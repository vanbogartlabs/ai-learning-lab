# Chapter 30: Saving Memory, Rebuilding Place

Under Gail Naughton’s leadership, the National Czech & Slovak Museum & Library preserved its historic building by moving it and preserved institutional wholeness by separating programs, collections, capital, offices, and construction into workstreams that could move on different schedules. That paradox was the central fact of the recovery. Preservation did not mean holding everything still. It meant allowing the parts of a damaged institution to travel different paths without surrendering the mission that made them one institution.

The chapter begins after the building had failed as an operating home. Collections, staff responsibility, public mission, and recovery work continued, but they could not continue in their former arrangement. This was not yet a story of a building returned. It was a business problem with several clocks: materials required specialized treatment; staff needed places to work; the public needed evidence that the institution still existed; donors and agencies required accountable uses for support; engineers and builders faced a physical structure; and leadership had to decide what permanent form all that activity should eventually serve.

Naughton, the museum’s president and chief executive officer from 2002 to 2018, led the choice to keep the institution functioning through displaced programs and offices while a permanent rebuilding program took shape. Her decision was not to perform every recovery task herself, and the museum’s property, funds, liabilities, and contracts were not hers personally. Her responsibility was executive and organizational. She had to hold together work she did not own and could not directly execute: strategy, fundraising, conservation, public programming, relocation, elevation, expansion, and construction.

That distinction matters because flood recovery can collapse into a deceptively simple verb: rebuild. A damaged institution does not rebuild as one object. A book and a building do not recover by the same method. A public program cannot wait on the same schedule as structural engineering. Restricted support cannot necessarily be shifted to whichever need becomes most urgent. A reopening date can restore access without proving that every conservation problem has ended. Naughton’s task was to keep these differences from becoming a breakup.

The analytically safer choices were real. The institution could have remained closed until a complete permanent answer existed. It could have repaired in place without expansion, abandoned the historic structure, or reduced its mission to whatever temporary capacity allowed. Those paths could have reduced coordination or capital exposure. No inspected account establishes that Naughton sat down and listed those options in that form, so they should not be mistaken for a recovered deliberation. They show why the chosen course was consequential: keeping public work alive while pursuing a moved, elevated, expanded home accepted complexity rather than avoiding it.

The pressure did not come from a single antagonist. It came from incompatibility. Damaged holdings required conservators and differentiated treatment. Displaced staff and programs needed usable temporary forms. Capital came with purposes and expectations. A historic building demanded decisions about preservation, movement, foundations, and expansion. Board members, donors, public agencies, architects, movers, contractors, volunteers, and temporary-space providers each retained their own authority, expertise, or obligation. Leadership could coordinate those actors; it could not turn them into one pair of hands.

That is why temporary public presence was not peripheral to the rebuilding. The *Rising Above* exhibition operated in the Kosek Building from 2009 to 2016. Its existence showed that the museum’s mission could remain visible away from the permanent home and continue after the permanent museum reopened. A program in displaced space was not a substitute for the whole institution, but neither was it merely a holding pattern. It kept a public relationship alive while physical and financial work proceeded elsewhere.

For staff, that continuity meant more than an organizational slogan. They needed work, coherence, and a reason for the institution’s many recovery tracks to remain connected. For Czech and Slovak communities and for visitors, access to cultural holdings and interpretation depended on both material conservation and an organization capable of presenting them. For donors and agencies, support carried an obligation: a headline campaign figure could not erase restrictions, approvals, or differences among uses. For volunteers and specialists, the work was physical and technical, not an extension of executive authority.

The materials themselves made the separation visible. Repository evidence summaries describe books being frozen, records being cleaned, and a map surviving in thirty-two pieces. Those details remain publication-level leads rather than fully inspected original-source facts, but they reveal the business logic of fragmented recovery. Different objects could demand different sequences, facilities, expertise, and measures of completion. The institution could not wait for the last damaged item before deciding how to maintain programs, assemble capital, or determine the building’s future.

Naughton’s post-flood decision therefore operated at two levels at once. At the first, she led continuity: public function and staff work would not be suspended until a perfect building answer arrived. At the second, she led reconstruction: the permanent answer would not be limited to putting the old arrangement back where it had been. The two levels depended on each other. Temporary operation kept the mission legible enough to justify a permanent return. The permanent plan gave temporary work a direction beyond indefinite displacement.

The official museum retrospective attributes to Naughton’s leadership a campaign that raised more than $28 million after the flood. That figure establishes scale, but it does not make all money interchangeable or every gift hers to claim. It indicates the capital-organizing burden carried by the institution and its leadership: many sources, purposes, approvals, and promises had to be aligned with a physical and programmatic future. The achievement was not simply that a large number appeared. It was that capital had to meet different workstreams without dissolving their boundaries.

Fundraising also widened the circle of accountability. A donor supporting conservation, a public agency participating in recovery, and a contributor supporting construction might all share an interest in the museum’s return while expecting different evidence of responsible use. Staff and board members had to preserve institutional coherence across those expectations. Naughton’s exposure was professional, strategic, fundraising, schedule-related, and reputational. Exact board delegations, contract signatures, indemnities, and personal financial exposure have not been established. Her courage should not be inflated into ownership; it lies in accepting public responsibility for coordination under dependence.

The physical answer sharpened the chapter’s contradiction. Under Naughton’s leadership, the original building was saved and moved. Official museum language says it was placed on a new foundation three feet above the flood. Other repository summaries report a 480-foot move and conflict over whether elevation amounted to eleven or twelve feet, but those measurements cannot be silently combined. The secure fact is enough: the historic structure survived through displacement and a changed relationship to the flood level.

Moving a building can look like the chapter’s climax because it compresses years of institutional work into one visible act. But treating it as a stunt would distort the decision. The move mattered because it served a larger operating design. Programs had already demonstrated that mission could travel. Collections had followed material-specific recovery routes. Capital had been assembled for differentiated purposes. The building’s movement was another workstream—extraordinary in scale, but not the whole institution and not an engineering act attributable to Naughton alone.

The project also added 30,000 square feet. Expansion changed the proposition from rescue to redesign. The institution was not merely retrieving an artifact from damage; it was deciding what capacity the returning mission would require. That enlarged scope increased dependencies on design, construction, funding, schedules, and public trust. Repair in place might have reduced some of those exposures. Expansion accepted them in exchange for a permanent arrangement intended to support a future larger than survival.

This was entrepreneurship inside a nonprofit institution. Naughton did not possess the control of an owner, and institutional money did not become personal capital because she led its assembly. Yet she faced a recognizable entrepreneurial problem: how to pursue a future that depended on actors she could not command and resources not yet fully organized, while remaining answerable for whether the system cohered. Her work was to make distributed agency productive without claiming it away from the people who carried it.

The board governed. Staff preserved operations and institutional knowledge. Conservators treated holdings. Volunteers contributed labor. Movers and engineers handled the building’s physical demands. Architects and contractors developed and executed the built form. Donors and agencies supplied support under their own constraints. Temporary-space providers made displacement workable. Naughton’s leadership connected these efforts to one mission and one permanent-return strategy. The word *under* in “under her leadership” should bear its proper weight: responsibility above many workstreams, not authorship of every act within them.

The recovery also changed what “place” meant. The museum was rooted in Czech Village, but its public identity could not be reduced to one unusable address. Exhibitions elsewhere, offices elsewhere, collections in treatment, and a building in motion remained connected by institutional purpose. Place became something the organization could preserve through adaptation rather than location alone. The historic building still mattered—enough to save and move—but its value was joined to what the mission required next.

Cedar Rapids supplied the conditions that made such coordination possible and difficult. The institution had staff, volunteers, donors, public attention, temporary space, city recovery planning, construction capacity, and regional links to conservators, universities, agencies, and funders. The recovering city also created competition for money, land, labor, and time. Naughton and the museum did not act outside that environment. Their return depended on civic and specialist capacities shared with many other recovery efforts.

In exchange, the project added something to the city’s repertoire. It demonstrated, in bounded form, that preservation could be organized around future use rather than a demand that every function return unchanged to the same place at the same moment. That claim should not be enlarged into causation. The museum did not single-handedly revive Czech Village, produce nearby investment, or create another organization’s project. Its contribution was a visible institutional method: maintain mission through temporary forms, divide complex work honestly, adapt the physical asset, and reunite the parts around a permanent purpose.

The method required sequence, not simultaneity. A building plan could not dry a wet book; conservation could not secure construction capital; temporary programming could not move a structure. Yet each track could protect the reason the others mattered. Public work kept the mission visible. Material treatment protected what the mission held. Capital and approvals created the possibility of a permanent form. Progress had to be recognized in unlike units—a treatment completed, an office functioning, a grant aligned with its purpose, a plan approved—without allowing any single advance to masquerade as return.

Separation made the institution less vulnerable to one stalled track, but it created another danger: the parts could cease to behave like parts of one organization. Temporary arrangements could become indefinite. Restricted support could be mistaken for general capacity. A visible building project could draw attention away from collections work that lacked a public silhouette. Naughton’s responsibility was not to erase these differences. It was to keep each partial success answerable to the same mission.

The permanent form therefore had to be more than a construction endpoint. Programs, collections, offices, public access, and the historic structure needed a way to function together again. The moved building and 30,000-square-foot addition made that recombination physical. Their scale mattered because of the institutional work they were meant to hold. The building was preserved as part of a living organization, while the organization gave up the fiction that survival required restoring the failed arrangement exactly.

Credit had to remain distributed as carefully as the work. Executive leadership is easy to see; conservation, volunteer labor, temporary-space support, design, engineering, governance, and restricted funding can disappear behind it. But independence was not an inconvenient footnote to the recovery. It was the operating design. Naughton’s leadership became consequential by bringing distinct authority toward convergence without claiming that authority as her own.

By July 2012, four years after the flood, enough physical, financial, programmatic, and organizational capacity had aligned for the expanded museum to reopen. The date marks a consequential return of public access, not a declaration that every object, obligation, staffing disruption, or recovery track was complete. Reopening made institutional wholeness usable again. It did not require every path to have reached its end.

The consequence was physical and organizational. A historic building had been preserved through movement. A larger facility stood in a changed relationship to flood risk. Public programming had survived displacement. Capital, expertise, and labor from many sources had been organized toward a shared return. Naughton’s decision mattered not because it eliminated dependence but because it accepted dependence as the medium of leadership.

That is the chapter’s courage. It is not a claim that Naughton knew the outcome, controlled the board, guaranteed the money, engineered the move, or personally rescued collections. It is the choice to keep the mission operating while the institution was broken into schedules that could have pulled it apart, and to lead those schedules toward a permanent form before their convergence was assured. Waiting would have been simpler. Coordination kept the future open.

What survived, then, was more than memory and more than masonry. The recovery left a method another civic builder could use without any personal handoff from Naughton: assess a damaged structure not only by what it had been, but by the mission it could carry next; organize public and private capacity around adaptation; and let preservation change form when stasis would destroy function.
