# Chapter 26: Highways, Suburbs, and a Moving Market

Cornfield and timber did not become a retail destination simply because automobiles could reach the edge of Cedar Rapids. Land had to enter a transaction; a developer had to organize a project; construction, anchors, stores, roads, utilities, parking, planning, workers, and customers had to converge. The future Lindale site was described as cornfield and timber apparently co-owned by James Faulkes and Thompson, yet Bernard Greenbaum & Associates retains the construction/development credit. Indispensable land association did not make its apparent owners the developers: the outward-moving market required a divided production chain. <!-- EIQ-26-C01 -->

A modern *Gazette* retrospective associates Faulkes and Thompson with ownership of the site, but the published account supplies no inspected deed. The record does not verify which James Faulkes was involved, either apparent owner’s parcel or share, or whether the land was sold, leased, optioned, contributed, retained, or otherwise committed. Apparent coownership is a bounded association, not a transaction history. <!-- EIQ-26-C02 --> The same retrospective credits Bernard Greenbaum & Associates with beginning construction in September 1959. That supports construction/development execution at project level without revealing who selected Greenbaum, approved plans, financed the work, or held final authority. <!-- EIQ-26-C03 --> Land association and development credit must remain separate.

Chapter 25 left Cedar Rapids with technical reach, capital intensity, and productive capability that could persist while founder authority changed. Its inheritance was analytical, not personal: resources could enlarge possibility while distributing authority. No evidence connects Arthur Collins to Faulkes or turns one man’s loss of control into another man’s suburban opportunity. The narrative now rewinds from Collins’s 1971 governance consequence to Lindale’s 1956 announcement.

The outward-moving market made distributed authority spatial. A downtown store could rely on established streets, surrounding commerce, and customers already accustomed to the center. An outlying retail project had to make destination and access together. Edge land was necessary, but customers needed reasons and means to travel there. Anchors, smaller stores, construction, utilities, roads, parking, planning, labor, and customer habits had to form an operating proposition.

The *Gazette* retrospective reports that Younkers and Sears announced in February 1956 a $10 million, seventy-two-acre, fifty-store open-air center. Because the retrospective’s linked contemporary pages remain uninspected, those figures and the announcement remain attributed to the retrospective. They cannot be converted into Faulkes’s investment, debt, guarantee, plan, or promise. <!-- EIQ-26-C04 --> The announcement made the project legible in acreage, cost, stores, anchors, and open-air form before the production chain had made any of them operational.

The same *Gazette* retrospective, citing contemporary coverage, reports that local retailers opposed the proposed center. That establishes project-level conflict, not a confrontation directed personally at Faulkes. <!-- EIQ-26-C05 --> Existing merchants had reason to care about a destination that might reorganize attention and trade, but the retrospective does not supply their complete arguments, identify every opponent, or prove that the project caused a later downtown outcome. Opposition marked uncertainty while the announced destination was still only a proposition.

The project was announced in February 1956. Greenbaum began construction in September 1959. Lindale opened on September 15, 1960. <!-- EIQ-26-C06 --> The interval separated promotional scale from physical commitment. Announced capital was not completed financing. Named anchors did not fill every storefront. Acreage did not establish access. A store count did not produce tenants, workers, inventory, deliveries, or customers. Construction crossed one threshold, but the project still had to become a working destination.

The interval cannot be called Faulkes’s wait, persistence, capital exposure, or ordeal. The published accounts do not establish his identity, authority, capital, retained interest, debt, guarantee, tenant negotiation, personal downside, motive, gain, loss, or consequence. <!-- EIQ-26-C07 --> An edge-land owner might hold, farm, timber, subdivide, sell, lease, option, contribute, or develop; those are structural possibilities, not choices Faulkes is documented as considering. His name belongs at the project threshold beside Thompson, and the evidence stops there.

The project itself continued through divided thresholds. Land had to be available on workable terms. Development organization had to coordinate plans and commitments. Construction had to turn plans into usable space. Anchors had to remain attached strongly enough to support the destination. Smaller stores had to judge whether customer flow justified entry. Workers had to make the place operate. Customers had to travel there and find the arrangement useful enough to return.

Each threshold placed authority somewhere else. An apparent owner could not decide for an anchor. A developer could not decide for every retailer. A retailer could not command roads or customers. Public planning could constrain or enable without operating the stores. Roads could widen the area from which customers and workers traveled, but could not make a tenant sign, stock a store, pay a worker, maintain a utility, or persuade a customer to return. Automobile access also remained unequal for people without equivalent reach.

Parking and utilities belonged to the same chain. Space for automobiles could make an outlying center usable to drivers without producing demand by itself. Utilities could make stores operable; planning and municipal geography could shape permission, service, and access. The published accounts assign none of those functions to Faulkes. Distributed agency was not incidental complexity around the project. It was the means by which the destination could exist.

The opening configuration showed that announced and completed form were not identical. The retrospective reports that Lindale opened with thirty stores and three anchors, rather than the fifty stores named in the 1956 announcement. <!-- EIQ-26-C08 --> The difference is not automatically failure, discipline, triumph, or evidence of one person’s compromise. It shows a project changing as commitments became real. Thirty operating stores and three anchors constituted a completed destination different from the announced scale.

That result belonged to the project. It did not establish what Faulkes received, retained, or lost, Thompson’s transaction, Greenbaum’s capital position, which stores bore what risk, or whether every opening tenant remained. Its supportable consequence was narrower: after opposition and a long interval, an outlying open-air retail center opened with a changed configuration assembled by actors whose authority remained divided.

Cedar Rapids made the undertaking plausible by supplying a growing automobile market, edge land, changing roads and municipal geography, established retailers, utilities, planning, customers, and workers. Those conditions did not compel Lindale or establish which private actor controlled it. The project returned a coordinated retail destination with anchors, multiple stores, and automobile-oriented access. Apparent owners, developer, contractors, anchors, smaller stores, public systems, workers, and customers each occupied a different relation to that result.

The human stakes followed those divisions. Apparent owners faced whatever consequences followed from an unknown transaction. The developer and contractors faced execution obligations whose precise contracts are uninspected. Anchors and smaller stores faced operating and customer uncertainty. Workers depended on jobs and schedules. Existing retailers encountered a new project they opposed. Drivers gained another possible destination, while residents without equivalent automobile access did not receive the same reach. None of those positions can stand for every person in the group.

The completed destination therefore remains a distributed project rather than a Faulkes biography. Apparent ownership placed Faulkes and Thompson at one indispensable threshold; Greenbaum retained construction/development credit; no inspected land instrument, development-entity filing, mortgage, partnership agreement, contract, lease, site plan, or applicant record supplies the missing authority chain. <!-- EIQ-26-C09 --> Lack of an inspected instrument is not proof that none existed, but it prevents land association from becoming development authority, a personal wager, or a recovered consequence.

Lindale made the moving market visible by concentrating dependencies in one place. Customers traveling outward encountered an apparently unified destination. Behind that experience stood land relations, development, construction, retail commitments, public systems, labor, and repeated customer choice. The center could look singular only because separate actors had aligned enough interests and capacities to make it operate.

Outward movement redistributed rather than eliminated dependence. A central location depended on circulation around it; an edge destination depended more visibly on automobiles, roads, parking, and a sufficient reason to make the trip. Anchors could help supply that reason while depending on the larger project becoming usable. Smaller stores could benefit from common traffic without commanding it. Greater individual range rested on deeper institutional coordination.

The project changed the kind of reach Cedar Rapids businesses could use without preserving any particular enterprise. Roads could deliver a customer or employee to a property; they could not maintain orders, credit, supplier cooperation, labor agreement, or plant continuity once those relationships weakened. No personal handoff connects Faulkes, Thompson, or Greenbaum to Keith Barnes. Chapter 27 receives only that project-level limitation.

**The outgoing condition is project-level: dispersed automobile access could widen retail and labor markets, but roads and destinations could not preserve orders, credit, supplier relations, labor agreement, or plant continuity.**
