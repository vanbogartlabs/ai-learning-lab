# Chapter 18: Speeder, Link-Belt, and the Building Boom

Later summaries describe Ronk moving Speeder toward a reported 35,000-square-foot Cedar Rapids plant from a Fairfield operation listed with two lathes, a drill press, a power saw, and a forge; neither the announcement, inventory, nor plant plans have been inspected. <!-- C18-01 -->

The distance between those two descriptions was more than floor area. Five major machines suggest a shop in which each tool, each worker, and each interrupted task mattered immediately. A plant proposal implied more equipment, more people, more work in process, and more obligations that would continue whether orders arrived or not. Ronk was not carrying a plan from one desk to another. He was moving an operating concern whose value depended on keeping machinery, skill, production, customers, and reputation connected while the company changed its physical base. <!-- C18-02 -->

Surviving accounts place Speeder’s move amid Cedar Rapids rail, power, builders, metalworking labor, room, recruitment potential, and neighboring machinery concerns; it does not establish which local inputs Speeder directly used. None of those assets could make Ronk’s decision for him. They could not ensure that Fairfield workers would relocate, that new workers would be ready, that equipment would survive the transfer, or that customers would keep buying through a restart. A capable industrial environment was an opening, not an order book. <!-- C18-03 --> <!-- C18-04 -->

Speeder had already learned that a company’s location was not fixed. Later company histories place Speeder first in Leon and then Fairfield and describe it as small in the mid-1920s; formation dates, ownership, and the mechanics of the earlier move remain unverified. Another move was not effortless continuation; it put continuity at risk for a second time. Staying in Fairfield, adding space incrementally, leasing more room, or waiting for orders and a Cedar Rapids workforce would have contained that risk. No surviving account records Ronk weighing those courses. His action supplies the evidence that matters: in 1926, he chose relocation and enlargement. <!-- C18-05 -->

That choice made Ronk a particular kind of entrepreneur. Howard Hall’s machinery proposition had organized components around a customer’s moving job. Ronk’s problem was different. He had to move the organization that performed the work. A lathe could be shipped. A routine could not. A forge could be installed elsewhere. A machinist’s willingness to move a household could not be packed beside it. Drawings, orders, habits, supplier relationships, and customer confidence had to remain usable across a break in place.

The public proposal made the ambition conspicuous. A summarized 1926 announcement—not yet inspected—described a 35,000-square-foot plant and projected that 150 families would come to Cedar Rapids; neither figure is treated as a completed result. Those were projections, not results, but they reveal the size of the intended break. Ronk was not seeking a modest annex to the Fairfield shop. He had put Speeder’s proposed transformation before workers, customers, and the receiving city while the transfer itself remained unresolved. <!-- C18-06 --> <!-- C18-09 -->

The word *families* exposed what an equipment list could not. A company could announce a move once; every worker still faced a household decision. Following Speeder could mean leaving familiar streets, schools, relatives, housing, and routines. Refusing could mean leaving the employer. The summaries identify no individual choice and support no composite worker’s story. The consequence is visible without one: Speeder’s production knowledge lived partly in people, and Ronk could not ship that knowledge by issuing an instruction. <!-- C18-07 -->

That dependence made the announced scale fragile. A larger building could create room for more production, but it could not instantly create practiced coordination. New workers would have to enter routines that transferred workers knew; transferred workers would have to adapt those routines to a different floor, different equipment placement, and a larger organization. The evidence does not permit that process to be reconstructed. It does establish the burden Ronk accepted: enlargement had to preserve accumulated skill without treating labor as interchangeable. <!-- C18-08 -->

Nor could Speeder solve continuity one department at a time. Equipment without people produced nothing. People without orders generated cost but not revenue. Orders without a dependable restart put customer confidence at risk. A permanent plant without enough of all three turned intended capacity into idle space. Ronk’s decision bound these uncertainties together. Relocation was not a sequence in which each problem could be settled completely before the next began; it was an attempt to keep several incomplete answers aligned long enough for an operating company to emerge in Cedar Rapids.

Cedar Rapids offered a practical way to stage the risk. Later accounts say Speeder first operated in a Cedar Rapids streetcar barn, but the location, tenancy, occupancy dates, and installed equipment remain unverified. Temporary quarters could give machinery a place to work before the proposed plant was ready, but shelter was not continuity. The barn did not guarantee a complete workforce, a smooth restart, sufficient orders, or reliable production. It reduced one kind of delay while leaving the larger wager intact. <!-- C18-10 --> <!-- C18-12 -->

The streetcar barn and permanent plant reveal the chapter’s central contradiction. Ronk pursued scale without requiring every element of final scale before work resumed. Temporary space treated continuity as something to preserve during enlargement; every step toward the permanent works increased fixed commitment. The same decision was cautious in sequence and audacious in destination. <!-- C18-11 -->

Later accounts place Speeder’s permanent works on former circus grounds after the barn stage; deed, parcel, construction, contractor, and occupancy records remain uninspected. The location carried no automatic industrial destiny. Ground, building, equipment, workers, and orders still had to become an operating system. A plant was useful only when production moved through it and customers wanted what emerged. Ronk’s courage did not consist in acquiring a larger address. It consisted in accepting responsibility for reconnecting the pieces of a living business before the larger base had proved itself. <!-- C18-14 -->

Later accounts put numbers around the attempt: about 100 workers transferred, about $40,000 was spent on the works, and about 88 machines were made in the first Cedar Rapids year. The figures are reported outcomes rather than inspected company records, and each answers less than it first appears to. The projected 150 families did not become a documented 150-family transfer. A production count did not establish sales or profit. Plant spending did not reveal Ronk’s equity, guarantees, or possible loss. <!-- C18-13 -->

Within those reported limits, the figures indicate coordinated movement and a Cedar Rapids restart, not uninterrupted production, sufficient orders, customer retention, or profitability. Speeder became more than an industrial-recruitment proposal. Yet restart was not vindication. The surviving evidence does not establish whether customers waited, whether production stopped, whether the first year met expectations, or whether orders comfortably supported the plant. Later activity cannot erase what was uncertain when Ronk committed the company to move. <!-- C18-17 -->

Ronk’s responsibility was organizational before it was financial in any recoverable personal sense. As the leader associated with moving the young company again, he was answerable for continuity and publicly connected to the relocation. The record does not establish his ownership share, cash contribution, salary, debt, or guarantees. Assigning him a private fortune at risk would manufacture a more familiar kind of drama and obscure the one the evidence does support: he placed an operating enterprise in motion and accepted the chance that its connections would break. <!-- C18-15 -->

Customers were part of that continuity. Machinery is a durable product, but a manufacturer’s obligation does not end when metal leaves the floor. Buyers need delivery, setup, parts, repairs, and confidence that the maker will remain available. A relocation could weaken each assurance. If customers treated the move as instability, plant size would not solve the problem. Ronk needed Speeder to become larger without making it less dependable.

The city’s role was similarly concrete and limited. Cedar Rapids could provide transportation, power, industrial room, recruitment, builders, and a labor pool. Temporary use of the streetcar barn could lower the immediate barrier to restarting. The local machinery economy could make specialized work more plausible. Yet the city could not guarantee a transferred workforce or continued demand. Surviving accounts support a bounded reciprocity: Cedar Rapids offered conditions for scale, while Ronk brought an operating concern, products, some workers, and a commitment to restart; specific recruitment promises and local services remain unverified. <!-- C18-16 --> <!-- C18-20 -->

The exchange also divided responsibility in a useful way. Civic recruitment could help open doors, and existing industrial systems could reduce the cost of finding space, transport, and skill. Ronk still had to make Speeder function inside those advantages. If orders failed to arrive, rail connections would not convert capacity into demand. If too few experienced workers came, a labor pool would not automatically preserve Speeder’s routines. The city could make the wager more rational without making its outcome inevitable. That distinction is central to why the move belongs to Ronk rather than to a generalized story of Cedar Rapids growth.

That exchange added another line to the city’s machinery economy. Within the surviving account, Speeder added excavating-equipment capability to Cedar Rapids’s broader machinery economy; specific product lines, suppliers, workforce classifications, and service networks remain unverified. The effect was not Ronk’s sole creation. Machinists, foundry workers, assemblers, suppliers, service workers, carriers, customers, and later managers all contributed to what the enterprise became. The most defensible measure of Ronk’s act is narrower and more consequential: he brought the company to the place where those combined capabilities could accumulate. <!-- C18-18 --> <!-- C18-21 -->

Scale eventually made Speeder attractive to a broader organization. In 1939, Link-Belt announced a combination involving Speeder’s shovel operation. Surviving summaries use several terms for the transaction—combination, purchase, merger, and formation of a wholly owned subsidiary. Without the transaction records, none should be allowed to settle the legal mechanics. The Link-Belt name in the chapter title marks both consequence and a limit on attributing later corporate acts to Ronk. The bounded consequence is enough: the company Ronk had moved and enlarged became part of a wider product and control structure. <!-- C18-19 -->

That later development clarifies the value of the 1926 decision without turning 1939 into Ronk’s second defining choice. A shop-sized concern with five major machines would have offered one kind of capability. A functioning plant, workforce, product line, and customer connection offered another. Relocation helped create a plant-scale platform that later became useful to a broader product structure, although the operational assets and rights valued in 1939 remain unresolved. The value was collective by then, and authority was no longer Ronk’s alone. <!-- C18-23 -->

The limit matters because plant scale has a double edge. Capacity can accept larger demand, support specialization, and make a company valuable to a broader system. It also fixes more of the enterprise in buildings, machinery, payroll, and routines that cannot quickly disappear when demand changes. The permanent plant answered the small shop’s constraint by creating a new exposure. More capability meant more dependence on enough work to keep capability active.

It also changed the meaning of continuity. In Fairfield, continuity meant keeping a small operation alive. In Cedar Rapids, it increasingly meant keeping a larger network of people, machines, space, and customers productive together. Success at relocation did not retire the original problem; it expanded it. Every gain in capacity raised the volume of work needed to sustain the system and the number of livelihoods connected to its operation. The move’s consequence was therefore not simple growth. It was a larger obligation to coordinate growth after the dramatic act of relocation had ended. <!-- C18-22 -->

Outside control added another tension. Cedar Rapids gained jobs, skills, service trades, and production capacity as Speeder grew into a broader corporate structure. But authority over that capacity could move away from the person and place that had assembled it. The later company history supports the bounded conclusion that the local plant could remain while authority moved outside Ronk’s enterprise; the instruments and timing of that shift remain unresolved. That was not necessarily a consequence Ronk foresaw, and it should not be narrated as his intention. It is the institutional inheritance of the scale he pursued: a larger platform can become useful to actors beyond its founder’s control. <!-- C18-26 -->

For workers, the inheritance was equally mixed. The move created the possibility of continued and enlarged employment while tying livelihoods to a fixed operation whose future decisions might be made beyond Ronk and Cedar Rapids. In 1926, workers had faced whether to follow the company. At larger corporate scale, workers could remain in place while authority moved away. The continuity Ronk tried to preserve for the business was never distributed as equal authority over it. <!-- C18-24 -->

Still, the move altered what Cedar Rapids could do. Later operation supports a collective inheritance of excavating and road-construction machinery, technical labor, fabrication, transportation, parts, and field-service capability; the direct local service relationships remain unverified. A machine maker requires more than factory labor. Equipment must be carried, sold, maintained, supplied with parts, and kept productive where customers use it. Each layer creates another business problem and another field for specialized work. <!-- C18-25 --> <!-- C18-27 -->

This is where Ronk’s chapter extends beyond one plant without becoming a city survey. His relocation made one operating company larger by placing it inside an environment of rail, power, labor, room, and related machinery capability. In return, the company added to that environment. Cedar Rapids became more useful to machinery builders because machinery builders had chosen to use it. The pattern was cumulative, but never automatic.

Ronk’s defining decision remains the hinge. He could have protected the small concern by limiting the size of the next step. Instead, he accepted a move that could interrupt production, separate the company from workers, unsettle customers, and burden it with a larger plant before sufficient orders were known. He also staged that commitment through temporary space, showing that courage need not mean refusing every precaution. The streetcar barn and the permanent works belonged to the same wager: preserve enough continuity to reach a scale that continuity alone could not provide.

The safer choice might have preserved more flexibility, but it also would have left Speeder inside the limits of its small base. Ronk chose a harder coordination problem because relocation opened a path that incremental protection might not. That conclusion comes from the structure of his action, not a recovered statement of belief. He accepted responsibility for making a larger future operational before he could know whether the company’s essential relationships would survive the attempt. <!-- C18-28 -->

What survived the move was not simply Speeder’s name. It was an enlarged capacity to build and support machines that changed how other people moved earth, constructed roads, and served equipment away from the factory. That capacity drew together technical labor, fabrication, transportation, and field service. It also carried the costs of fixed plant and the possibility that control would migrate outward. <!-- C18-29 -->
