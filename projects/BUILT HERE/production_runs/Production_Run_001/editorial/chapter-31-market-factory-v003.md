# Chapter 31: The Market in the Factory

Before a merchant could test one small stall, Sarah Ordover publicly led a nonprofit commitment to secure the flood-affected Quality Chef site, preserve threatened industrial buildings, and carry the larger place-and-platform risk. The apparently modest wager at a counter depended on an immodest one upstream: land, preservation, plans, capital, construction, shared services, programming, and public accountability had to be organized before any vendor could make a smaller first move.

The chronology now overlaps the museum recovery and resets to June 2010. Cedar Rapids was developing and practicing that capability across parallel flood-recovery efforts; it was not a completed lesson passed from Gail Naughton to Sarah Ordover. The record establishes no Naughton–Ordover contact or causation. At the Quality Chef site, a new nonprofit faced its own decision: whether to ask the City to entrust it with a flood-affected property before the organization could prove that plans, capital, buildings, merchants, and customers would become a market.

In June 2010, the board of Cedar Rapids City Market Inc. presented a final site plan, business plan, and artist’s conception to the Cedar Rapids City Council. The nonprofit formally requested the Quality Chef site. Contemporaneous reporting identified Ordover as the organization’s president and placed her at the public center of the request and proposed funding explanation. The documents gave the council something defined enough to examine, but paper did not confer land, settle funding, or complete a building. “Final” described the plan presented then; it did not mean that the eventual built form had been settled. At that moment, the concrete act was a public request for control of a place the organization did not yet possess.

The request included a commitment to preserve two flood-affected industrial buildings from demolition during post-flood rehabilitation. Demolition could simplify the property problem. Ordinary leased space could reduce redevelopment complexity. Temporary outdoor markets could continue without concentrating so much responsibility in one site, or private development could carry the land and building risks. Except for the council’s lease-versus-transfer concern, those alternatives are analytical rather than a recovered list voiced by Ordover. They clarify the choice without inventing her deliberation: the nonprofit was making its commercial proposition depend on saving and adapting damaged structures. If the City agreed, the buildings would no longer be only threatened remnants; they would become obligations the organization had publicly claimed for a future use.

Ordover’s role must remain bounded by the organization through which she acted. She was not the sole founder, owner, financier, architect, contractor, or decision-maker. The nonprofit board acted collectively; the City controlled the property question; and Project for Public Spaces had developed the site plan and an early market study. Public and private funders, volunteers, contractors, staff, merchants, and later operators would carry their own parts of the conversion. Ordover’s documented role is narrower and more consequential than a founder legend: as president, she publicly carried the site-and-capital sequence at the moment the organization asked the City to trust a platform that did not yet exist.

That trust was not automatic. Council members explicitly considered the possibility that the enterprise could fail. Their concern does not make them villains; it makes the public stakes visible. The City and taxpayers had legitimate interests in whether property should be leased or transferred, whether support would yield a functioning asset, and what would remain if the nonprofit could not complete or operate the market. A failed effort could leave the organization without a viable site, funders without a functioning public asset, and buildings defended from demolition without a sustainable use.

The failure concern pressed directly against Ordover’s need for agreement. She explained that the organization needed an agreement on the site before it could mount the capital campaign and proceed toward construction. That is the chapter’s one essential sequence: campaign and construction required credible site control, while the City had to consider site control before campaign and construction could make the enterprise credible. The nonprofit was asking public officials to judge an operating proposition before operations could supply their own evidence. A lease or transfer could distribute exposure differently, but the inspected record does not establish the final instrument or its terms. Agreement would not settle the market’s future. It would make the next obligations possible.

Those obligations were large enough to test the public case. Ordover described a proposed $5.8 million plan that included an in-kind site contribution and hoped-for support from FEMA, private sources, and the state. These were proposals, not receipts or expenditures, and the figure cannot be merged with later campaign totals. Its force lies in what remained unresolved. Property value, recovery support, private contributions, and state participation had to be made legible to institutions with different reasons for joining. The proposed package exposed the distance between permission and completion: site control would unlock a campaign, but the campaign still had to align several sources before construction could translate the plan into usable space. The nonprofit was not asking for one check; it was asking permission to begin organizing a public-private commitment around a specific place.

The physical plan was unresolved too. In 2010, it contemplated a new two-story market hall along with reuse of the historic Day Company building and another warehouse-like structure. Later official NewBo history describes the renovated Quality Chef plant becoming Rotary Hall. Between those accounts, the physical answer changed while the preservation obligation remained. A site defended from demolition still had to become a place people could enter, merchants could build within, and a nonprofit could operate. The inspected accounts do not establish who authorized every change or make Ordover the author of the final design. The founding commitment had to survive alteration in form. Preserving industrial structures did not mean freezing the first plan. The coalition had to adapt its design while remaining answerable to the site and public purpose it had defended.

This was the larger wager behind the stall. A vendor entering a shared market would not have to acquire an independent parcel, preserve an industrial building, create common utilities, build a gathering place, or generate all customer traffic alone. Those burdens did not disappear. The nonprofit and its public-private coalition concentrated them upstream, adding fundraising, construction, programming, reputation, and public accountability. Ordover’s supported personal exposure within that institutional wager was executive, fundraising, sequencing, public, and reputational. The record does not show that she guaranteed debt, owned the site, supplied the capital, signed every agreement, held sole voting authority, or faced personal financial loss.

The shift from proposal to working place depended on people whose labor could not be folded into Ordover’s public role. Project for Public Spaces contributed planning and study. The board made organizational decisions. The City negotiated property control and possible support. Funders carried distinct parts of the capital burden. Volunteers, contractors, and staff translated plans into physical and operating capacity. That distribution enabled the platform. A place designed to gather many businesses first had to gather many kinds of authority.

By October 2012, that conversion had entered a different kind of leadership. Ann Poe was executive director during finishing work and merchant move-in. Her role was not an epilogue, and it should not be moved backward. Ordover belongs at the 2010 site-and-capital stage, where the organization made the place-scale commitment. Poe belongs at the opening-period stage, when plans, shared space, and merchant relationships had to become a working environment.

Poe described the market as a conduit and gathering place and expressed the hope that businesses could use it as a launch point toward storefronts. More than 300 merchant applications were reported, while roughly twenty permanent merchants were expected, each responsible for its own build-out. The numerical contrast placed actual people at the far end of the founding sequence and showed both demand for entry and the platform’s limit: shared place narrowed the property commitment, but selection remained a gate, and the market could not make access universal or costless. The record does not establish the criteria, demographics, fairness, costs, or outcomes of those choices. Merchants retained responsibility for spending, building, operating, and attracting customers; customers still had to come; and common areas and programming still had to work across businesses while City and funder expectations extended beyond any one stall. A finished building could house those relationships, but only continuing use could make them a market.

Nor did opening retroactively simplify the 2010 decision. The early mixed design and later Rotary Hall account preserve a project changing across time. Giving every final feature to Ordover would erase the board, consultants, City, funders, builders, and Poe’s opening-period work. Giving the founding choice to Poe would move her backward from the operating threshold where her role is documented. The market emerged through linked responsibilities, with the site request first and merchant entry last, not through one person’s uninterrupted control.

Cedar Rapids supplied both the opportunity and the resistance. Flood recovery produced a damaged industrial site and urgency around demolition or reuse. The City could negotiate property control and possible in-kind support; local vendors, volunteers, consultants, and funding networks offered capacity beyond one leader. The same setting imposed public scrutiny, district-wide competition, and a legitimate fear of committing public assets to an enterprise that might fail. In return, the nonprofit and its coalition defended useful structures, organized plans and proposed capital around them, and converted a preservation claim into shared commercial infrastructure. No one leader owns that exchange.

Ordover’s exposure was public rather than private. Her courage cannot be measured by imagined confidence, private fear, or personal ownership. It lies in accepting visible responsibility for a sequence whose first condition—the site agreement—opened larger exposure instead of closing it. The council’s failure concern made the downside speakable. The proposed $5.8 million plan showed the size of the unresolved obligation. A changing design required collective adaptation. By opening, hundreds of applications competing for roughly twenty places showed why the platform mattered and why it could never remove judgment, scarcity, or risk.

What passed forward was therefore neither Ordover’s authority nor NewBo’s story. It was a city capability made usable through the work of a nonprofit, public officials, planners, funders, builders, operators, and merchants: damaged industrial space could become a common place where an entrepreneur’s first premises did not have to be an entire independent property. That condition cannot decide any later company’s actions or guarantee any merchant’s result. It simply changes the scale at which a next test can begin.
