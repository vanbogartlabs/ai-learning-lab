# Narrative Discovery Record — Chapter 4: David W. King

## The One Truth

David W. King paired a temporary ferry privilege with David and Mary King’s platting and expansion of Kingston, linking passage to a west-bank destination before traffic or settlement demand was proven. The privilege’s bridge exception—and the later turn to public bridges—show that replacement was built into the proposition’s limits; King did not build those bridges.

## Why It Changes Understanding

King’s enterprise was not merely a ferry and not a bridge venture. Its force came from coupling two uncertainties: Kingston gave people reasons to cross, and the crossing made Kingston more usable. The bridge exception exposed the private privilege’s possible obsolescence from the beginning; later public bridges show that connection became a need larger than one temporary franchise.

## Courageous Decision

> Despite uncertain traffic and settlement demand, river interruption, a ten-year term, and possible bridge replacement, King chose the protected crossing opportunity alongside David and Mary King’s platting of Kingston because his documented actions indicate that passage and destination could make one another more useful.

## Ten-Year Reader-Retell Sentence

> David W. King paired a ten-year ferry privilege—one that already allowed a future bridge—with David and Mary King’s platting of Kingston, making connection a business whose success pointed beyond the ferry itself.

## Placement

Put the ferry-and-Kingston pairing on the opening page and reveal the bridge exception within the first 10 percent. Reserve later public bridges for a brief closing consequence and limit, not a second narrative.

## Evidence Boundary

Supported in repository summaries: King received a ten-year ferry privilege, exclusive within one mile upstream and downstream; the law preserved future bridge construction; David and Mary King share credit for Kingston platting and expansion; later bridges belonged to other actors. The coordinated ferry-and-land strategy and built-in obsolescence are bounded structural interpretations. Traffic, tolls, financing, returns, exact plat date, and division of work between David and Mary remain unresolved.

## What Must Never Be Lost

- The temporary ferry privilege and its bridge exception.
- David and Mary King’s joint credit for Kingston platting.
- The mutual dependence of passage and destination, followed by connection becoming public infrastructure.

## What Must Never Be Added

- Any claim that King built, financed, owned, or directed the later bridges.
- Sole credit to David for Kingston, or an invented division of labor with Mary.
- Invented motives, dialogue, traffic, tolls, losses, causation, or a guaranteed plan linking ferry and plats.
