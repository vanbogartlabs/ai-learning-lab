# Narrative Discovery Record — Chapter 11: John T. Liddle

## The One Truth

John T. Liddle's housing enterprise did not end when he erected and sold small homes to wage earners: when purchasers missed scheduled payments, he reportedly carried the delay rather than press forfeiture, and the source attributes home retention to that forbearance.

## Why It Matters

Liddle made creditor response part of housing production. A completed house and signed sale did not secure a wage earner's place; retention still depended on income timing and the seller's choice after default. Liddle could protect capital by pressing forfeiture, yet accepted delayed or uncertain receipts. Another builder should care because responsibility can continue after delivery, when rigid terms meet a customer's unstable capacity.

## Entrepreneurial Meaning

> Despite construction exposure and missed payments that made forfeiture the safer protection of capital, Liddle chose to give purchasers more time because his conduct supports the inference that completed payment and home retention were worth carrying beyond the original schedule.

## Reader-Retell Sentence

> Liddle built and sold small homes to wage earners, then carried buyers who missed payments instead of immediately taking the homes back.

## Mandatory Placement

Open with the missed-payment fork and state the full build-sell-carry chain on the opening page and within the first 10 percent. Anna Vild may appear only later as restriction context.

## Evidence and Confidence

A 1911 Linn County history says Liddle invested in real estate, erected and sold small homes for wage earners, allowed added time after missed payments, and thereby preserved homes. **Confidence: Moderate, attributed Tier 3 local history.** Houses, buyers, prices, instruments, terms, extension lengths, losses, and long-term outcomes are unknown.

## Omission Risk

Without this truth, the chapter becomes neighborhood survey or benevolence claim. It loses Liddle's complete production-and-credit agency, explicit safer alternative, accepted payment risk, and household consequence.

## Frozen Inheritance and Transition

Receive wages, land, credit, restrictions, and housing need as city conditions. Leave Chapter 12 a city where some wage earners had a more durable relationship to place, while residential-industrial separation made scheduled movement more consequential. Do not imply Liddle caused transit or knew its next builder.

## What Must Never Be Added

Do not call the homes affordable, charitable, subsidized, paternalistic, or universally successful. Do not invent a buyer, house, contract, motive, scene, amount, or loss. Anna Vild's $1,600 restriction is context only: do not claim she built or financed a house or dealt with Liddle.
