# Narrative Discovery Record — Chapter 4: David W. King (v002)

## The One Truth

David W. King paired a temporary ferry privilege with David and Mary King’s platting and expansion of Kingston before traffic or settlement demand was proven. The privilege already preserved a future bridge, so the crossing proposition contained its own possible replacement; King did not build, finance, own, or direct the later bridges.

## Why It Matters

King’s enterprise was neither merely a ferry nor a bridge venture. Passage made west-bank land more usable, while Kingston created reasons to cross. Its success exposed a need larger than one temporary franchise: dependable connection beyond the local crossing, especially to external markets.

## Entrepreneurial Meaning

King accepted coupled land-and-access uncertainty rather than operating only a temporary ferry or waiting for durable infrastructure. His actions support a bounded structural interpretation that passage and destination could reinforce one another; they do not establish his private motive or a guaranteed plan.

## Reader-Retell Sentence

King paired a ten-year ferry privilege that allowed a future bridge with Kingston’s development, making local connection valuable enough to reveal the need for a more dependable external link.

## Mandatory Placement

Put the ferry-and-Kingston pairing on the opening page and the bridge exception within the first 10 percent. Keep later bridges brief and explicitly outside King’s ownership or construction.

## Evidence and Confidence

**Confidence: Moderate/qualified.** Repository summaries support King’s ten-year ferry privilege, its one-mile exclusivity and bridge exception, David and Mary King’s joint Kingston platting credit, and later bridges under other actors. Traffic, tolls, financing, returns, exact plat date, and division of work between David and Mary remain unresolved.

## Omission Risk

Without this truth, the chapter becomes ferry chronology, erases Mary, or falsely turns King into a bridge builder. It also loses the chapter’s essential shift from local passage to the need for dependable external connection.

## Frozen Inheritance and Transition

Receive Chapter 3’s activity on both banks, which makes division costly and creates King’s crossing market. Leave Chapter 5 exactly this condition: **Local crossing exposes the need for dependable connection to external markets.** George Greene receives that need for his rail decision; do not hand it to Young or imply personal contact or causation.

## What Must Never Be Added

No sole credit for Kingston; no invented motive, dialogue, traffic, tolls, losses, or guaranteed ferry-and-land plan; and no claim that King built, financed, owned, directed, or caused later bridges, Greene’s rail promotion, or later growth.
