# Narrative Discovery Record — Chapter 6: James C. Young

## The One Truth

Young put a reported 883-lot outer district before buyers even though a plat could fix lot lines but could not supply the transportation, streets, drainage, utilities, buildings, institutions, neighbors, or buyer commitments that made those lots useful.

## Why It Matters

The scale turns Young from a parcel seller into a promoter of coordinated possibility. He received a city made more credible by rail-enabled circulation, yet local access and land usability remained incomplete. His proposition depended on many actors he did not control. Another builder should remember that defining a product can precede—and cannot guarantee—the system that gives it value.

## Entrepreneurial Meaning

> Despite a crowded land market and unproved access, services, occupancy, and demand, Young chose to promote a reported 883-lot Third Addition because his actions support the inference that attention and early commitments could make distance more commercially negotiable.

## Reader-Retell Sentence

> Young promoted a reported 883-lot district before the systems that could make any one lot useful existed within his control.

## Mandatory Placement

State the One Truth on the opening page and within the first 10 percent, before brass-band or food detail. Scale and dependence—not promotional spectacle—must govern the chapter.

## Evidence and Confidence

Repository summaries report an 883-lot Third Addition laid out and promoted in 1883; a later account recalls brass-band music and free food. **Confidence: Moderate and qualified** for the addition and count; **Low to Moderate** for the promotion detail. No reliable sales result is preserved. Streetcar service reportedly followed in 1884, but Young's role is unproved.

## Omission Risk

Without this truth, the chapter becomes colorful promotion or generic growth history. It loses Young's district-scale exposure, the buyer's uncertainty, and the gap between divisible inventory and a functioning place.

## Frozen Inheritance and Transition

Receive Chapter 5's **rail-enabled circulation**, which makes outer land more credible without completing local access. Leave Chapter 7 **expanded land and circulation** on which households, merchants, and institutions can act beyond the old center, without claiming Young recruited them, designed their communities, or caused later growth.

## What Must Never Be Added

No invented sales success, finances, motive, dialogue, crowd, promised improvements, streetcar control, or causation linking Young to transit, population growth, neighborhood institutions, or later industry.
