# Narrative Discovery Record — Chapter 18: George T. Ronk

## The One Truth

George T. Ronk moved Speeder toward a reported 35,000-square-foot Cedar Rapids plant from a Fairfield operation whose summarized equipment list held only two lathes, a drill press, a power saw, and a forge—risking a living company’s continuity to pursue plant scale.

## Why It Matters

The physical contrast makes “relocation” concrete. Ronk was not moving an idea onto empty ground: equipment, production, workers, households, customers, and reputation all had to survive the break between a small operating base, temporary quarters in a streetcar barn, and a permanent plant.

## Entrepreneurial Meaning

Ronk chose another move and enlargement rather than remaining in Fairfield, expanding incrementally, or waiting for orders and workforce certainty. The decision exposed the company to restart and retention risk before growth was assured; Ronk’s personal equity, guarantees, and loss ceiling are unknown.

## Reader-Retell Sentence

Ronk left a shop summarized by five major machines for a reported 35,000-square-foot plant, betting that Speeder could survive the move required to become larger.

## Mandatory Placement
