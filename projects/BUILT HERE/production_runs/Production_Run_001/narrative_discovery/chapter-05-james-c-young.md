# Narrative Discovery Record — Chapter 5: James C. Young

## The One Truth

Young put a reported 883-lot outer district before buyers even though a lot’s practical value depended on transportation, streets, drainage, utilities, buildings, institutions, neighbors, and buyer commitments that its lines could neither provide nor keep under his sole control.

## Why It Changes Understanding

The scale turns Young from a seller of distant parcels into a promoter of coordinated possibility. He could define the product, but the product could work only if many other actors completed the place around it. His courage was not certainty that expansion would succeed; it was exposing a district-scale proposition to public judgment before the systems that could make it useful were proved.

## Courageous Decision

> Despite a crowded land market and an outer district whose access, improvements, occupancy, and buyer response remained unproved, Young chose to promote a reported 883-lot Third Addition publicly because his actions support the inference that attention and early commitments could make distance more commercially negotiable.

## Ten-Year Reader-Retell Sentence

> James C. Young promoted a reported 883-lot district before the systems that could make any one of its lots valuable existed within his control.

## Placement

State the One Truth on the opening page and within the first ten percent, before any promotional detail, so scale and dependence—not spectacle—govern the chapter.

## Evidence Boundary

The repository’s present evidence summaries report 883 lots in Young’s Third Addition and an 1883 public promotion. A later real-estate account reports brass-band music and free food; use that detail only with this provenance and only as evidence of attempted attention, never of attendance, sales, or success. The lot count remains reported pending verification of the original plat.

## What Must Never Be Lost

Keep the reported 883-lot scale, the distinction between fixed lot lines and future-dependent value, Young’s lack of sole control over the required systems, and the closing inheritance of expanded operating ground for later builders without beginning Chapter 6.

## What Must Never Be Added

Do not add sales success, precise finances, private motive or emotion, promised improvements, streetcar control, invented scene detail, or causation linking Young’s promotion to transit, growth, neighborhood development, or later industry.
