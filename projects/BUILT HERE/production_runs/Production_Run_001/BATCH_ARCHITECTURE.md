# Production Run 001 — Chapters 1–5 Delivery-Batch Architecture

**Status:** Superseded as a production-scope artifact by `BOOK_ARCHITECTURE_35.md`. Retained as the first human-review delivery-batch view.

## Book-Level Question

Why did generations of entrepreneurs repeatedly choose to build in Cedar Rapids, and how did their choices create conditions later builders could inherit?

## Opening-to-Ending Design

The book begins with an entrepreneur making possibility usable before success is visible. The eventual ending must return to that act of inheritance: every builder receives an unfinished opportunity and becomes responsible for what the next builder will receive.

Chapters 1–5 form one escalating sequence:

> **Define the opportunity → own the uncertainty → make the river work → connect the divided market → connect the city to external markets.**

The chapters must not read as five origin biographies. Each chapter inherits a practical problem created or exposed by the prior entrepreneur.

## Chapter Sequence

### Chapter 1 — Brown’s Proposition

- **Entrepreneur:** Nicholas B. Brown
- **Defining courage:** Help organize Rapids City into a surveyed commercial proposition before control, demand, or dependable power was proven.
- **Narrative priority:** Slightly more than sixty blocks and 805 planned lots made uncertainty concrete enough for another person to enter.
- **Inheritance:** George Greene receives a defined but unsecured proposition and must decide whether it is worth owning.

### Chapter 2 — Greene’s Buy-In

- **Entrepreneur:** George Greene
- **Defining courage:** Accept an interest in the Rapids City proposition while its legal character, value, participants, and economic engine remained uncertain.
- **Narrative priority:** Greene’s investment mattered not because the terms are recoverable—they are not—but because joining converted Brown’s plan from solitary possibility into shared commitment.
- **Inheritance:** Capital and participation increase the pressure to make the river provide productive value; Brown’s problem returns at a larger scale.

### Chapter 3 — Brown Against the River

- **Entrepreneur:** Nicholas B. Brown
- **Defining courage:** Recommit to dams and productive enterprise after early solutions failed and the river remained beyond dependable control.
- **Narrative priority:** Brown’s importance lies in returning to an unsolved system, not in conquering it.
- **Inheritance:** Productive activity on both banks makes the river’s division more expensive, creating David W. King’s market for connection.

### Chapter 4 — King’s Crossing

- **Entrepreneur:** David W. King
- **Defining courage:** Pair a time-limited ferry privilege with the platting and expansion of Kingston before traffic, tolls, continuous passage, settlement demand, and return could be guaranteed.
- **Narrative priority:** King recognized that connection itself could be a business—and that making the two banks usable together could enlarge the city’s market. Later bridges are the consequence and limit of his crossing proposition, not structures attributable to him.
- **Inheritance:** Once local crossing becomes more usable, George Greene can address the larger problem of dependable connection to external markets.

### Chapter 5 — Greene’s Iron Bet

- **Entrepreneur:** George Greene
- **Defining courage:** Commit to rail as a more dependable connection after river transport had exposed its limits, before route, capital, construction, and traffic were assured.
- **Narrative priority:** Greene’s second chapter is not another entry decision. It asks whether a builder already exposed to local uncertainty will accept responsibility for connecting the proposition to external markets.
- **Inheritance:** Rail-linked access gives industrial builders a larger field of suppliers, workers, sites, and customers.

## Arc Requirements

### Entrepreneurial Arc

Each builder makes a new kind of enterprise possible: planned inventory, shared ownership, productive power, physical connection, and dependable external connection.

### Emotional Arc

The sequence moves from possibility to commitment to failure-tested persistence to local connection to external connection. Confidence must not rise in a straight line; every gain creates a larger exposure.

### Historical Arc

Treaty-conditioned settler opportunity and contested claims establish the moral and legal ground. History appears only where it changes an entrepreneur’s options, risk, or consequence.

### Courage Arc

Courage escalates from acting before proof, to joining another person’s unproven proposition, to recommitting after failure, to building temporary local connection, to committing to a capital-intensive external connection.

### Inheritance Arc

Every chapter ends with a specific usable condition—not a generic lesson—that the next entrepreneur receives.

## Continuity Controls

- Brown’s Chapters 1 and 3 must form one evolving character story without repeating the same decision.
- Chapter 2 must belong to Greene, not to land-title history or Brown.
- Chapter 3 must belong to Brown, not to the Cedar River or a chronology of dams.
- Chapter 4 must belong to King, not to bridge engineering or municipal growth.
- Chapter 5 must belong to Greene’s railroad decision, not repeat Chapter 2 or become a transportation chronology.
- James C. Young’s current run-specific Chapter 5 artifacts are preserved but architecturally reclassified as Chapter 6 candidate material. They require re-indexing and upstream revision after this architecture gate; this file does not authorize manuscript edits.
- Treaty and Indigenous history must remain accurate and ethically necessary without displacing the entrepreneur.
- Source uncertainty must be stated once where material, then carried through precise prose rather than repeated archival commentary.
- No chapter may claim sole founding, private motives, exact investments, clean ownership, or causal certainty that the repository evidence does not support.
