## 1. Scope and authority

This review covers Chapters 1–12 in `assemblies/Enriched_Manuscript_With_Evidence_Placeholders_v001.md`. It treats Chapters 1–6, 9, and 11 as ready prose and Chapters 7, 8, 10, and 12 as nonblocking Evidence Resolution Queue placeholders. `BOOK_ARCHITECTURE_35.md` remains frozen, and `EVIDENCE_QUEUE.md` controls placeholder boundaries.

## 5. Major reader issues

### R-MAJ-01 — Chapter 1 explains the same proposition after the reader has understood it

- **Enriched artifact:** `enriched/chapter-01-browns-proposition-v002.md`
- **Exact draft:** “The survey did not eliminate that demand for courage; it organized it. It let uncertainty pass from one participant to the next in identifiable form.” Later: “The survey converted a generalized gamble into many particular decisions.” Later again: “Brown helped move the venture from possibility without measure to exposure that could be counted.”
- **Reader consequence:** The 805-lot opening, compromised land context, and question of ownership are compelling, but the chapter repeatedly translates them into versions of “commercial form before proof.” The reader stops discovering and starts being instructed. Brown recedes behind the thesis, and the opening chapter delays the book's first decisive forward move.
- **Revision instruction:** Preserve the 805-lot opening, collective-agency limits, Indigenous/settler ethical ground, Brown's analytical alternative, and final Greene question. Keep one developed passage explaining that survey lines made uncertainty usable and one late passage explaining accountability; cut or combine the intervening restatements so each remaining paragraph adds a new consequence, participant, or decision pressure.
- **Must remain unchanged:** Brown must not become sole founder, owner, surveyor, or financier; no motive, personal financial exposure, or cleaner treaty geography may be invented; the chapter must still end before Greene makes his decision.

### R-MAJ-02 — Chapter 2 leads with the archive and then restates relational exposure

- **Enriched artifact:** `enriched/chapter-02-greenes-buy-in-v002.md`
- **Exact draft:** “The surviving repository record cannot tell us exactly what he acquired. A later account supplies the entry; no inspected deed or contract in the approved research fixes the asset, parcel, seller, date, price, fraction, legal character, or Greene’s formal capacity.” Later: “Greene joined dependence.” Later again: “His participation helped make their unfinished condition his problem too.”
- **Reader consequence:** The missing instrument is a legitimate trust boundary, but the opening makes the reader process repository method before fully feeling the danger of entry. Once “Greene joined dependence” lands, multiple later paragraphs re-explain entanglement, shared risk, and limited control. The choice remains clear while momentum stalls.
- **Revision instruction:** Open on Greene's reported entry into 805 unproved lots and prospective power, then state the missing-instrument boundary once in a compact paragraph. Retain “Greene joined dependence” as the chapter's governing turn; consolidate the later shared-risk, obligation, and unfinished-condition passages into one escalating consequence sequence that ends on the missing productive engine.
- **Must remain unchanged:** Keep modern archival uncertainty distinct from uncertainty Greene historically faced; preserve Greene's bounded participant role, collective credit, unknown terms and motives, ethical land context, and exact productive-power inheritance.

### R-MAJ-03 — Chapter 3's operating-system explanations overwhelm its excellent return-after-failure story

- **Enriched artifact:** `enriched/chapter-03-brown-against-the-river-v002.md`
- **Exact draft:** “Water alone cut no board and ground no grain. A mill needed a structure to direct power and machinery to receive it, but productive use also required labor, maintenance, material arriving for processing, finished goods moving outward, and enough demand to justify keeping the parts together.” Later: “He was working inside a system whose usefulness had to be renewed continually.” Later again: “A dam therefore concentrated uncertainty rather than ending it.”
- **Reader consequence:** The failed early dam, changing mills, steam and rail alternatives, and 1870 duration marker form a strong escalating story. Repeated inventories of machinery, labor, maintenance, supply, transport, demand, and interruption flatten those stages into the same analytical beat. The reader understands the system before Brown reaches the chapter's most revealing late decision.
- **Revision instruction:** Build the chapter around four beats only: failed first answer; renewed productive ventures and their human dependencies; the arrival of steam and rail as real alternatives; Brown's reported 1870 association as the duration test. Keep one full operating-chain explanation near the first mills and one later sentence showing how dependence enlarged; remove the repeated maintenance/dependence inventories between them.
- **Must remain unchanged:** Preserve conflicting dates, collective roles, enterprise-cost versus personal-cost distinction, no claim of wisdom or uninterrupted control, the 1870 marker, and the two-bank crossing condition.

### R-MAJ-04 — Chapter 5 substitutes a rail-system lecture for escalating proximity to Greene's decision

- **Enriched artifact:** `enriched/chapter-05-iron-connection.md`
- **Exact draft:** “A rail proposition had to collect commitments in advance of operation. Capital, route, and rights-of-way came before traffic could justify them. Track, bridges, depots, and equipment had to become an operating chain through labor and maintenance.” Later: “The facilities reveal that connection is never just a line between two points.” Later again: “Rail offered greater regularity than favorable-water transport, but its reliability was organizational rather than automatic.”
- **Reader consequence:** The chapter's central contradiction—fixed iron still requiring contingent organization—is strong, but it is explained in several dependency lists and abstractions. Greene appears mainly as a bounded role inside a system rather than as the person repeatedly choosing participation before proof. The reader can paraphrase the thesis but has little new desire from one section to the next.
- **Revision instruction:** Center three progressive turns: water transport's demonstrated limits; Greene's bounded choice to promote fixed external connection before route/capital/traffic were secure; June 1859 as collective consequence rather than vindication. Retain one complete rail dependency list, the qualified steamer example, and one concrete account of the later facility system; compress the remaining track-is-not-operation explanations and return Greene's renewed participation to the start of each major movement.
- **Must remain unchanged:** Do not make Greene sole builder, negotiator, financier, street-rights grantor, or operator; preserve the distinction from Chapter 2, collective recruitment, qualified arrival, distributed labor, and exact rail-enabled inheritance to Chapter 6.

## 8. Cross-range reader findings

### Reader desire and comprehension

The movement's strongest hooks are numerical or physical: 805 lots, a failed brush-and-rock dam, a ten-year ferry privilege vulnerable to flood and replacement, 883 lots promoted with remembered music and food, 15,039 hogs before a sixteen-acre commitment, and missed payments carried rather than forfeited. Comprehension is generally high. Desire falls when chapters continue to paraphrase an already-clear mechanism instead of advancing to a changed pressure, consequence, or choice.

### Emotional and courage stakes

The chapters consistently distinguish courage from confidence and avoid invented interiority. Chapters 4, 6, 9, and 11 turn that discipline into felt stakes through washout, buyer refusal, fire and labor exposure, death and succession, or loss of a home. Chapters 1, 2, 3, and 5 more often state the courage interpretation than let changing circumstances renew it.

### Trust and evidence language

The assembly is unusually trustworthy about unknown ownership, authority, terms, motives, and outcomes. The problem is not qualification itself but repeated source-method narration after a boundary has already been established. One clear qualification followed by bounded nouns and verbs will increase trust more than recurring references to “the repository,” “approved research,” or what the record “does not establish.”

### Unforgettable truths

Every ready chapter has a viable retellable truth. The four failures arise because the manuscript surrounds those truths with too many interpretive echoes, not because the chapters lack a memorable core.

### Cedar Rapids reciprocity

All eight ready chapters show a two-way exchange rather than founder worship: Cedar Rapids supplies compromised ground, river motion, shared participants, crossing demand, rail possibility, a competitive connected market, labor and infrastructure, or wage-earner housing demand; the builders return legibility, participation, productive persistence, passage, external connection, market-tested land, industrial capability, or forbearance. This is a section strength and should remain explicit but need not be restated more than once per chapter.

### Endings and forward pull

All eight ready chapters end on a usable condition, unresolved obligation, or direct question. Chapters 6, 9, and 11 cross adjacent queue slots without narrating placeholder protagonists. The repeated bold terminal-condition styling sometimes reads as production markup rather than organic cadence, but the underlying forward logic is sound; wording governed by frozen transition controls must remain exact unless separately authorized.
