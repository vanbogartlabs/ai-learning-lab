# Character Brief — Arthur A. Collins

## Entrepreneur

Arthur A. Collins

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> **Arthur A. Collins was driven, as his repeated commitments indicate, to make specialized technical knowledge capable of operating at ever-greater scale—even as each enlargement transferred more of the outcome to customers, workers, lenders, shareholders, and governors beyond his control.**

This thesis integrates one person-level arc across three chapters. It is an interpretation of documented formation, capacity expansion, technical investment, and the 1971 financing-and-governance transaction, not a recovered statement of Collins's private motive.

## Courage Profile

Collins's arc contains three distinct decisions that must not collapse into generic growth:

1. **Chapter 23 — Depression-era formation:** convert personal technical practice into a formal operating company during 1931–1937.
2. **Chapter 24 — wartime scaling:** enlarge fixed and leased capacity in 1942 under customer-shaped procurement rules.
3. **Chapter 25 — capital/control tradeoff:** allow the 1971 North American Rockwell financing agreement to proceed, exchanging reported working capital and debt restructuring for majority-board rights and effective control.

The sequence matters. Chapter 23 creates an entity. Chapter 24 teaches that entity to reproduce capability through many people and locations under a dominant customer's rules. Chapter 25 confronts the financing and governance consequence of a valuable, capital-intensive institution whose continuity and founder independence have separated.

### The Impossible Bet

- **Chapter 23 — formation:** Collins treated specialized radio skill as the basis for a durable operating entity during the Depression. Success required more than invention: naming, chartering, bidding, selling, collecting, employing, recordkeeping, customer service, and legal obligations had to become repeatable beyond his own hands.
- **Chapter 24 — wartime scaling:** Collins committed a young company to added plant sections and leased locations around Cedar Rapids in 1942. The future he accepted required many workers and systems to reproduce technical quality while public procurement shaped specifications, materials, prices, facilities, labor matters, inspection, completion, and termination.
- **Chapter 25 — capital/control tradeoff:** Collins allowed a reported 1971 convertible-stock financing and debt restructuring to proceed so the enterprise could obtain working capital and continue operating, even though North American Rockwell received the right to elect a board majority. The attempted future preserved the technical institution by accepting that its founder might no longer govern it.

None of these bets was the same as confidence in inevitable success. Each decision changed the form of dependence Collins accepted.

### The Uncertainty

**Chapter 23:** Customers, cash collection, product-market fit, staffing, capitalization, and institutional survival were uncertain during economic contraction. Several dates—1931, late 1932, 1933, and 1937—mark different thresholds rather than one clean founding moment. Family-business failure supplied surrounding pressure without proving that Arthur assumed his father's liabilities.

**Chapter 24:** Wartime demand supported expansion but did not guarantee materials, prices, inspection outcomes, labor coordination, contract continuation, or postwar usefulness. Added sections and leased spaces created fixed and administrative burdens that could outlast the customer regime that justified them.

**Chapter 25:** By 1971, continued operation depended on capital, debt restructuring, corporate approvals, and governance rights. The record does not establish every alternative, Arthur's exact legal power to refuse, personal share position, negotiation authority, or whether a different transaction could have preserved both continuity and control. The consequence—his later removal and resignation—must not be written as an outcome he knew was inevitable when the agreement proceeded.

The modern record's gaps are separate from contemporaneous uncertainty. Missing ledgers, contracts, minutes, and securities records do not prove Collins lacked information at the time.

### The Safer Alternative

- **Chapter 23:** remain an amateur or consulting practitioner, limit sales to work Collins could perform personally, delay incorporation, or take employment within an established concern.
- **Chapter 24:** accept less wartime work, subcontract more, expand less, or keep operations concentrated at the existing plant.
- **Chapter 25:** reduce or stop C-System spending earlier, narrow product lines, sell assets, seek capital without majority-board rights, accept another investor or merger proposal, or enter court-supervised restructuring.

These alternatives clarify the choices analytically. No source records Collins enumerating all of them. For Chapter 25 especially, the record does not establish that every alternative remained available in May 1971 or that any would have safely preserved the company.

### The Personal Risk

**Chapter 23:** Collins attached his professional name and standing to a formal enterprise responsible for customers, employees, bids, receipts, records, and legal obligations. Supported exposure is professional, organizational, customer, and reputational. Exact capitalization, ownership, loans, guarantees, household contribution, profit, and losses are unknown.

**Chapter 24:** As founder and executive, Collins accepted accountability for greater fixed capacity, multiple locations, hiring, training, quality, coordination, and public-contract dependence. Exact board authorization, lease signatures, costs, financing, personal guarantees, contract values, and personal cash exposure are not established.

**Chapter 25:** The transaction directly exposed Collins's governance authority and founder identity. North American Rockwell reportedly received majority-board election rights; Collins initially remained president and chief executive, was removed in November, and resigned on December 7. His exact shareholding, voting power, personal guarantees, negotiation authority, and financial consequence remain unresolved.

Across the arc, the most defensible personal stake is the progressive separation of Collins's name, technical purpose, and formal authority. Do not invent family sacrifice, personal ruin, private fear, or a quantified fortune at risk.

### The Opposition

No single opponent defines all three chapters. Opposition changes with the enterprise:

- **Chapter 23:** Depression-era contraction, uncertain customers and cash, the administrative burden of formal enterprise, and the gap between technical skill and repeatable business operation.
- **Chapter 24:** scarce materials, inspection and procurement requirements, distributed locations, hiring and training burdens, fixed overhead, and the dominant public customer's power over products, prices, facilities, labor matters, and termination.
- **Chapter 25:** capital intensity, continued development costs, reported heavy bank borrowing, falling sales and market weakness, the end of large Apollo-era work, lender pressure, supplier and payroll obligations, and corporate governance mechanisms that could preserve operations while displacing founder control.

Bankers, managers, public agencies, employees, shareholders, and North American Rockwell must not be flattened into villains. They had distinct roles and interests. Likewise, conflicting or incomplete later sources are evidence problems, not contemporaneous opposition.

### The Courageous Decision

> **Chapter 23: Despite Depression-era contraction, uncertain customers and cash, and the organizational demands beyond engineering, Arthur Collins chose to formalize specialized radio work into an operating company because his repeated commercial and legal commitments indicate that he believed technical capability could become durable only through an entity larger than personal practice.**

> **Chapter 24: Despite fixed-capacity exposure, distributed operations, procurement control, and uncertainty about postwar demand, Arthur Collins chose to enlarge plant and leased capacity in 1942 because his commitment indicates that he believed the young organization could reproduce technical quality through many people and places.**

> **Chapter 25: Despite the direct threat to founder authority, Arthur Collins allowed the 1971 North American Rockwell financing agreement to proceed because his conduct indicates that he believed continued operation and working capital had become worth accepting governance dependence, even though effective control could pass from him.**

Each “believed” clause is an inference from action, not a quotation. Chapter 25 must retain the evidence qualification that the transaction chain comes from a detailed later synthesis pending primary corporate, bank, shareholder, and securities verification.

### Why It Matters

The three decisions transform Collins and the institution in different ways. Formation makes knowledge reusable through a company. Wartime scaling makes the company capable through collective systems but increasingly dependent on a powerful customer. The 1971 transaction preserves operating possibility while changing who can govern the enterprise. The arc is therefore not “a visionary kept choosing growth.” It is a progression in the price of making capability durable.

For Cedar Rapids, Collins's importance lies in the technical organization that remained after his authority ended: people, facilities, administrative routines, systems knowledge, and national reach. The legacy is intentionally double-edged. Productive capability can persist under outside governance, but local continuity and founder control are not the same outcome.

## Core Identity

Collins is best understood as a technical practitioner who repeatedly became an organizational builder, then confronted the limits of founder authority over what he had built. His early radio capability mattered because he translated it into commercial routines. His wartime leadership mattered because he accepted that quality had to be reproduced by many people under rules he did not set. His later decision mattered because the institution's capital requirements made continuity contingent on governance concessions.

He must not be portrayed as a solitary inventor. Employees, managers, customers, public procurement officials, landlords, suppliers, inspectors, lenders, shareholders, and outside directors all acquire greater agency as the company grows. The arc's human meaning comes partly from that redistribution of agency.

## What They Wanted

The documented decisions point toward one evolving concrete aim: make communications capability reliable and reusable beyond Collins's own bench, then preserve the organization capable of delivering it.

- In Chapter 23, that meant a named, chartered, operating company with customers, employees, records, and obligations.
- In Chapter 24, it meant added and distributed capacity able to perform wartime communications work at organizational scale.
- In Chapter 25, it meant working capital and continued operation for a broad, capital-intensive technical institution, even when obtaining them changed control.

The record does not authorize generic claims that Collins wanted greatness, the moon, unlimited growth, personal fame, or control at any cost.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

Private compulsion is not recoverable from the run evidence. Repeated commitments provide the safer answer. Collins moved technical activity through successive commercial and legal thresholds during 1931–1937, enlarged capacity under wartime demand, sustained postwar technical reach, continued supporting the capital-intensive C-System, and ultimately permitted a transaction that separated institutional continuity from his own control.

That pattern supports the interpretation that he valued the continuation of technical capability beyond any one product or personal task. It does not prove obsession, certainty, or that he preferred loss of control. Chapter 25 must distinguish accepting the reported instrument from desiring its later governance consequence.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Collins's actions suggest three recognitions:

1. technical skill becomes economically durable only when converted into an organization with records, roles, customers, and legal commitments;
2. complex communications work becomes repeatable only when quality is distributed through facilities, labor, administration, and coordinated systems; and
3. a technically valuable institution can demand more capital than founder authority alone can supply.

These are interpretations from the arc, not claims that Collins alone recognized them or foresaw every consequence. The third recognition must be presented as the business reality embodied by the 1971 transaction, not as proof that he anticipated loss of control years earlier.

## Defining Character Traits

- **Organizing:** converted specialized practice into company forms, customer routines, records, and obligations.
- **Technically ambitious:** supported increasingly complex communications work and later integrated computer-communications development.
- **Scale-accepting:** enlarged facilities and organizational systems under wartime demand.
- **Dependence-tolerant:** repeatedly accepted that customers, employees, public rules, lenders, and governors would shape what the company could do.
- **Persistent:** continued technical and organizational commitment across Depression, war, postwar markets, and capital crisis.
- **Control-vulnerable:** built an institution whose financing requirements eventually exceeded the founder's ability to preserve effective authority.

“Visionary,” “fearless,” and “obsessed” are not supported substitutes for these action-based traits.

## Greatest Risk

Across the integrated arc, Collins's greatest risk was that making technical capability durable would make it less his to direct. Formation attached his name to obligations. Wartime scale distributed execution among many people and under customer rules. Postwar capital dependence culminated in a reported transaction that preserved working capital while granting majority-board rights to North American Rockwell.

This is a governance, professional, organizational, and identity risk. Exact personal financial loss or wealth exposure is unknown.

## Greatest Obstacle

The greatest obstacle was the widening gap between technical possibility and organizational endurance. Engineering achievement required sales, cash, labor, materials, inspection, administration, facilities, customers, debt, and governance. Every solution to one limit created another dependency: entity after personal practice, systems after entity, and outside capital after systems became too expensive for existing resources.

## Defining Decision

### Chapter 23 — Depression-Era Formation

- **Options:** remain an amateur or consultant; limit work to personal capacity; delay formal commitments; join an established firm; or build an operating company.
- **Choice:** Collins moved specialized radio work through successive commercial and legal forms from 1931 through 1937, including use of the Collins Radio name and corporate chartering and reincorporation thresholds.
- **Cost:** his professional name and organizational responsibility became attached to customers, sales, collections, bids, employees, records, and entity-level obligations during contraction.
- **Consequence:** a personal technical practice became a durable young organization capable of receiving larger customer demands. The later dedicated plant is a threshold into Chapter 24, not the Chapter 23 decision.

### Chapter 24 — Wartime Scaling

- **Options:** limit wartime work, subcontract more, add less capacity, remain concentrated in the existing plant, or enlarge fixed and leased operations.
- **Choice:** in 1942 Collins enlarged the Thirty-Fifth Street plant and occupied leased locations around Cedar Rapids so the company could perform communications work at organizational scale.
- **Cost:** more fixed overhead, sites, workers, training, quality controls, administrative systems, and dependence on procurement rules and orders.
- **Consequence:** Collins Radio gained distributed technical capacity—facilities, workforce, organization charts, compensation and labor administration, quality routines, spare-parts support, and customer coordination—that had to survive the wartime demand regime.

### Chapter 25 — 1971 Capital/Control Tradeoff

- **Options:** reduce development, narrow products, sell assets, obtain different financing, accept another investor or merger, restructure through court, or permit the reported North American Rockwell agreement. The record does not establish that all options remained open or safe.
- **Choice:** Collins allowed the agreement to proceed, reportedly bringing a $35 million convertible-stock investment, debt restructuring, working capital, and North American Rockwell's right to elect a majority of the board.
- **Cost:** effective founder control. After shareholder action and the reported September closing and board election, Collins initially remained president and chief executive, was removed in November, and resigned December 7.
- **Consequence:** technical capability and Cedar Rapids operations could continue under outside governance. The 1973 merger is a later bounded consequence, not the defining decision itself.

## Contradiction

Collins's defining contradiction is that his success in making technical capability larger and more durable steadily reduced his individual command over it. Formal organization freed the work from his own time; wartime systems freed it from one site and one set of hands; outside capital freed continued operation from existing resources. Each form of durability also transferred authority.

A second contradiction is that dependence produced both capability and vulnerability. Government customers helped force organizational sophistication while setting rules and creating termination exposure. National markets and space work displayed technical reach while increasing capital intensity. North American Rockwell financing supplied working capital while changing governance. The arc should preserve both sides rather than treating dependence as either corruption or rescue.

## Human Stakes

**Chapter 23:** early customers depended on functioning equipment and delivery; employees depended on the young firm's ability to turn work into cash; Collins's professional name stood behind the entity. Family-business collapse and citywide contraction formed context, but Arthur's assumption of family debts is not established.

**Chapter 24:** employees and households depended on recruitment, classification, training, pay, hours, and continuing orders. Managers, suppliers, landlords, inspectors, and public agencies carried independent responsibilities. Customers depended on equipment meeting requirements. Archive folder titles do not establish individual wages, injuries, experiences, or equality.

**Chapter 25:** employees depended on payroll and continued work; suppliers had bills; lenders and shareholders faced loss; customers depended on continuity; Collins faced removal from authority over the company bearing his name. The instrument affected many constituencies, so the chapter must not reduce the transaction to one founder's tragedy.

## Emotional Arc

### Integrated Arc

- **Ambition:** Make specialized communications knowledge durable beyond personal practice.
- **Pressure:** Economic contraction, then customer-shaped wartime demand, then postwar capital intensity and lender pressure.
- **Decision:** Create the entity; enlarge its collective capacity; accept financing whose governance rights could displace the founder.
- **Consequence:** Capability becomes progressively more durable, distributed, and valuable while Collins's individual control becomes progressively less sufficient.
- **Legacy:** Cedar Rapids retains technical reach and institutional skill after founder authority changes, proving that continuity and control are separate outcomes.

### Chapter-Specific Movement

- **Chapter 23:** possibility becomes obligation. The reader moves from recognizable technical skill to the less glamorous but consequential burden of building a company.
- **Chapter 24:** obligation becomes dependence. The reader experiences scale as more people, sites, rules, and customer power—not as a celebratory workforce montage.
- **Chapter 25:** dependence becomes governance consequence. The reader sees working capital arrive attached to board rights, followed by the founder's removal and resignation.

No private emotion should be assigned to Collins without stronger evidence. The emotional force belongs in the changing relationship between name, institution, capability, and authority.

## Memorable Details

### Chapter 23

- Collins was a licensed amateur radio operator while young and had recognized radio capability before company formation.
- The formation sequence includes Arthur Collins Radio Laboratories in 1931, use of Collins Radio by late 1932, a Delaware charter in 1933, and Iowa reincorporation in 1937; each date marks a different threshold.
- The surviving corporate archive includes early cash-sale receipts, cash-drawer slips, advertising, attorney, employment, customer, bid, government-work, and equipment record classes.
- Collins equipment was associated with the 1933–1934 Byrd Antarctic expedition; transaction terms and causal importance remain unverified.
- Merle Collins's mortgage and farm businesses failed in 1934; that context does not establish Arthur's liabilities.

### Chapter 24

- Collins Radio had recently crossed into dedicated capacity at 855 Thirty-Fifth Street NE before wartime expansion.
- In 1942 the company added plant sections and occupied leased locations around Cedar Rapids.
- The archive inventory identifies 1941–1942 building-addition files and a 1942 plant survey.
- Organization charts, salary materials, a women's-earnings file, first-aid reports, War Labor Board matters, Navy price adjustment, Treasury rulings, spare-parts files, and mobilization schedules make the administrative burden of scale tangible.
- Folder names prove record classes exist; they do not prove specific wages, injuries, decisions, or outcomes.

### Chapter 25

- Collins Radio participated in aviation, military, satellite, and space communications work; exact roles must remain contract-specific and bounded.
- A Summer 1969 company publication described North American Aviation's Apollo communications-and-data subcontract, nearly 600 company employees involved at peak, five key subcontractors managed, and a fourteen-station unified S-band network role. It is a company representation, not an independent audit, and does not place every worker in Cedar Rapids.
- The C-System sought to integrate computers and communications for large organizational activity.
- Stearns reports more than $100 million in bank borrowing in 1969 and a $35 million May 1971 North American Rockwell convertible-stock investment; both figures require primary verification.
- The reported instrument included Rockwell's right to elect a board majority and further restructuring of bank debt.
- Reported shareholder action occurred August 31; investment, stock issuance, and election of seven Rockwell directors followed September 2.
- Collins remained president and chief executive immediately after the investment, was removed in November, and resigned December 7.

These details support bounded summary. They do not support invented workshops, contract negotiations, factory shifts, inspections, boardroom dialogue, banker threats, moon-landing reactions, or resignation scenes.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids gave Collins a home base, technical and amateur-radio context, customers and professional relationships, legal and commercial institutions, utilities, transport, industrial skills, labor, suppliers, and space. During wartime, its mix of a dedicated plant and additional leasable locations made distributed expansion possible. In the postwar period, its accumulated engineering, production, and administrative capability gave the company a platform for national and international systems work.

The city did not remove uncertainty or supply unlimited capital. No evidence establishes that a particular Cedar Rapids bank financed, denied, or saved the early company. By 1971, local technical capability was not sufficient to preserve founder governance.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

Collins gave Cedar Rapids a formal radio and communications enterprise whose employment, records, facilities, production routines, engineering culture, and systems reach broadened the city's industrial vocabulary beyond food and machinery. Wartime expansion created distributed technical and administrative capability. Postwar work connected that capability to aviation and space systems.

The contribution was collective. Collins did not personally perform every design, fabrication task, inspection, sale, or administrative function. The institution's persistence after his authority ended is part of what he gave Cedar Rapids and part of the warning embedded in the gift.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

Collins leaves a durable but young technical organization: formal identity, commercial routines, early customers, records, roles, and a local base approaching dedicated plant capacity. Chapter 24 receives that entity and asks whether it can enlarge plant and workforce inside procurement rules shaped by wartime customers. It must not replay invention or formation as its defining decision.

### Chapter 24 → Chapter 25

Collins leaves distributed technical capacity: facilities, workforce, administration, quality routines, and customer coordination. Chapter 25 receives an institution that must survive the demand regime that forced its creation. Postwar aerospace reach supplies scale and consequence, but the defining question is the specific financing and governance rights Collins accepted in 1971.

### Chapter 25 → Chapter 26

Collins leaves technical reach, capital intensity, and proof that productive capability can persist after founder authority changes. Chapter 26 receives an analytical city condition: assets and markets increasingly depend on networks and capital beyond one person's control. No personal transfer from Collins to James Faulkes may be invented, and Chapter 25 must not preview a Faulkes decision as established while Chapter 26 agency remains separately bounded.

## Evidence Boundary

### Supported within the run research

- Collins had recognized radio capability before company formation and was personally associated with the company bearing his name.
- The 1931–1937 record supports successive commercial and legal organization thresholds.
- The corporate archive contains record classes relevant to customers, products, finances, organization, employees, law, buildings, labor, procurement, and C-System development.
- In 1942 Collins Radio added sections to the Thirty-Fifth Street plant and occupied leased Cedar Rapids locations under Collins's founder leadership.
- Wartime scale involved facilities, workforce, administration, quality, and procurement dependence beyond Collins's individual work.
- Collins Radio developed broad postwar communications capability and participated in bounded aviation and space-system roles.
- A detailed later synthesis attributes continued C-System commitment and bank-funded development to Collins.
- That synthesis reports a May 1971 convertible-stock investment agreement, majority-board election rights, debt restructuring, later shareholder action and closing, Collins's November removal, and December 7 resignation.

### Qualified or interpretive

- “Skill converted into reusable organization,” “customer-shaped scale,” and “working capital purchased with governance rights” are evidence-bounded interpretations.
- Formation dates come from repository and retrospective synthesis and require original charter and filing verification.
- Archive inventory labels establish source classes, not the contents or outcomes of uninspected files.
- The 1971 transaction amount, debt figure, dates, and rights are supported at Research stage by a detailed later company/oral-history synthesis but require annual reports, agreements, minutes, proxy materials, bank records, and securities filings before publication.
- Collins's personal motive, exact option analysis, and knowledge of later consequences are unknown.
- C-System commitment contributed to capital dependence but is not established as the sole cause of distress; market weakness, the end of large Apollo work, lenders, managers, and broader company conditions also mattered.

## Chapter North Star

> **Chapter 23 is the story of Arthur Collins converting specialized radio skill into an operating company during the Depression, accepting commercial and legal obligations before the organization was secure.**

> **Chapter 24 is the story of Collins committing that young company to wartime capacity large enough to reproduce technical quality through many people and places under rules set by powerful customers.**

> **Chapter 25 is the story of Collins accepting a specific 1971 capital-and-governance bargain that could keep the technical institution operating while effective control passed beyond its founder.**

Together, the chapters tell one story of capability made durable at a progressively higher price in dependence and control. No chapter may substitute the integrated thesis for its distinct decision.

## Opening Possibilities

### Chapter 23 — Depression-Era Formation

1. **A decision:** Open on one clearly defined formation threshold—such as the move from Arthur Collins Radio Laboratories toward the Collins Radio name—while immediately signaling that organization occurred through several dates rather than one ceremonial birth.
2. **A moment of pressure:** Place cash-sale receipts, cash-drawer slips, bids, and attorney files against the Depression economy to show that technical skill had to pass through ordinary business routines.
3. **A vivid business problem:** A radio could work and still fail to create a company; the harder question was whether sales, collections, employment, records, and legal obligations could repeat.

### Chapter 24 — Wartime Scaling

1. **A decision:** Begin with the 1942 commitment to add plant sections and occupy leased locations, not with a recap of childhood radio or company formation.
2. **A moment of pressure:** Use the plant survey and scattered-space problem to show that every additional location increased both capacity and coordination burden.
3. **A vivid business problem:** Wartime orders required more than units; the company had to align people, materials, specifications, prices, inspections, records, and spare parts while the customer could shape or end the demand.

### Chapter 25 — Capital/Control Tradeoff

1. **A decision:** Open with the reported May 1971 terms—a convertible-stock investment and working capital attached to majority-board election rights—qualified once as a later synthesis pending transaction files.
2. **A moment of pressure:** Place supplier bills, payroll, lender pressure, and continued operation on one side of the agreement, founder control on the other, without reconstructing a boardroom.
3. **A vivid business problem:** Capital was not neutral cash. The right to elect a board majority converted financing into authority, and the later removal made that allocation personally consequential.

## Closing Handoff

### Chapter 23 → Chapter 24

Close on a company that now exists beyond Collins's individual practice but remains young: it has a name, legal forms, customers, commercial routines, records, and approaching dedicated capacity. Chapter 24 receives that organization in 1942 and asks whether it can become many sites and many workers without losing technical reliability. Do not preview C-System finance or 1971 control.

### Chapter 24 → Chapter 25

Close on distributed technical capability whose greatest customer also demonstrated the danger of dependence. Facilities, workforce, administration, and quality routines survive as an institution that must find postwar purposes and capital. Chapter 25 may use aerospace reach to show what the organization became, but it must drive toward the 1971 instrument rather than repeat a story of choosing scale.

### Chapter 25 → Chapter 26

Close on the separation between capability and control. The enterprise continues with valuable technical reach and Cedar Rapids capacity, but founder governance has changed. That condition—productive assets tied to larger networks and outside capital—hands the book to the automobile-oriented market problem of Chapter 26 without implying Collins transferred anything personally to Faulkes or that the next chapter's separate agency gate has already been resolved.
