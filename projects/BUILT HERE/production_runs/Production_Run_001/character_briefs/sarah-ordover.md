# Character Brief — Sarah Ordover

## Entrepreneur

Sarah Ordover

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> **Sarah Ordover was driven, as her documented founding leadership indicates, to carry the large place-and-platform risk that could let smaller entrepreneurs test their work through staged commitments.**

This is an interpretation of Ordover's public 2010 actions as Cedar Rapids City Market president. It preserves the board's authority, collective founding effort, and Ann Poe's later opening-period operating role.

## Courage Profile

### The Impossible Bet

In 2010, Ordover publicly led the nonprofit's effort to secure the flood-affected Quality Chef site, preserve threatened industrial buildings, assemble funding, and sequence site control, capital campaign, and construction for a public market. The attempted future was a shared platform combining food access, public gathering, community uses, and lower-threshold vendor entry before the site, capital, construction, or merchant demand had become an operating market.

### The Uncertainty

Site control, council approval, preservation feasibility, capital assembly, construction, final design, vendor participation, customer traffic, and enterprise viability were unresolved. Council members explicitly considered failure. The group could abandon the site, accept demolition, lease ordinary space, continue temporary outdoor markets, or wait for private development. Only lease versus transfer is documented as a contemporaneous alternative; the others are analytical.

### The Personal Risk

Ordover accepted executive, public, fundraising, sequencing, and reputational responsibility as nonprofit president. The record does not establish that she owned the site, personally guaranteed debt, supplied the projected capital, signed every agreement, held sole board authority, or bore personal financial loss.

### The Opposition

Structural opposition came from flood damage, threatened demolition, uncertain site control, council concern about enterprise failure, capital dependence, adaptive-reuse complexity, and the sequence problem that fundraising and construction could not proceed without a site. No council member, city official, consultant, or later leader should be cast as a villain.

### The Courageous Decision

> **Despite flood damage, demolition pressure, uncertain site control, an unassembled proposed funding package, and explicit concern that the enterprise could fail, Sarah Ordover chose to publicly lead the nonprofit's commitment to secure and preserve the Quality Chef site because her insistence on the site-to-campaign-to-construction sequence supports the inference that she believed smaller entrepreneurs needed a shared platform before they could make survivable first bets.**

The belief clause is an inference from documented action and platform purpose, not a recovered private motive.

### Why It Matters

Ordover's decision makes visible who accepted the risk behind the later “small first bet.” The nonprofit and its partners carried site, preservation, funding, and public-space exposure so vendors would not each need an independent property before testing demand. The resulting platform was collective, and its eventual form differed from the first inspected plan.

## Core Identity

Ordover was the documented founding president and public platform organizer at the 2010 site-and-capital stage. She was not the sole founder, owner, financier, architect, contractor, or opening operator. Ann Poe belongs as the opening-period executive director who managed merchant-facing operations and articulated the incubator purpose.

## What They Wanted

The concrete outcome was site control and a financed path toward a public market that preserved useful industrial structures and offered shared commercial place, fresh-food access, gathering, community uses, and lower-threshold entry for vendors.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

Private compulsion is unknown. Ordover's public presentation of plans, funding logic, preservation request, and insistence that site control precede fundraising and construction supports sustained commitment to making the platform executable rather than merely aspirational.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Her actions suggest she saw that entrepreneurs could make smaller, staged bets only if another institution first carried the larger risk of land, buildings, shared infrastructure, funding, and gathered traffic. The record does not establish that she alone conceived that insight.

## Defining Character Traits

- **Platform-oriented:** organized shared conditions for other businesses to enter.
- **Sequence-conscious:** publicly tied site control to fundraising and construction.
- **Preservation-minded in action:** sought to keep flood-affected industrial buildings from demolition.
- **Publicly accountable:** answered council concern about enterprise failure.
- **Collaborative:** acted through a nonprofit board, city negotiation, consultants, funders, and later operators.

## Greatest Risk

The greatest supportable risk was public responsibility for committing a nonprofit to a site-dependent platform whose land, preservation, funding, construction, and enterprise viability remained unresolved.

## Greatest Obstacle

The greatest obstacle was dependency sequence: without site control there could be no credible campaign or construction, yet securing the site required public confidence in a market that did not yet exist.

## Defining Decision

- **Options:** abandon the site, accept demolition, lease ordinary space, continue temporary markets, wait for private development, or seek control and preservation of the Quality Chef site.
- **Choice:** as Cedar Rapids City Market president, Ordover publicly led the 2010 site request, preservation commitment, proposed funding explanation, and sequence toward campaign and construction.
- **Cost:** nonprofit execution exposure, fundraising dependence, adaptive-reuse complexity, public accountability, and Ordover's leadership reputation.
- **Consequence:** the founding organization advanced site and business plans that ultimately led, through collective work and later design development, to the 2012 NewBo City Market opening.

## Contradiction

The platform sought to lower place risk for individual vendors by concentrating larger place risk in a nonprofit and its public/private coalition. Public support reduced some exposure while increasing accountability. Preserving industrial identity required adapting rather than freezing the initial plan.

## Human Stakes

A failed platform could leave the nonprofit without a viable site, funders without a functioning public asset, threatened buildings without a future, and entrepreneurs without promised lower-threshold entry. Vendors later accepted their own build-out and operating risks. The city and taxpayers had interests in property transfer and public support.

## Emotional Arc

- **Ambition:** create shared commercial infrastructure from a damaged industrial site.
- **Pressure:** threatened demolition, uncertain control, unassembled capital, and explicit failure risk.
- **Decision:** secure the site first, then organize campaign and construction around it.
- **Consequence:** collective work produced an adaptive-reuse market and smaller vendor entry points.
- **Legacy:** staged-risk infrastructure allows future entrepreneurs to test without carrying a whole storefront first.

The arc does not assign private feelings to Ordover.

## Memorable Details

- In June 2010, the nonprofit board presented a final site plan, business plan, and artist's conception to City Council.
- Ordover was identified contemporaneously as Cedar Rapids City Market president.
- The group formally requested the Quality Chef site and preservation of two buildings threatened by demolition.
- Ordover explained a proposed $5.8 million plan; proposed sources must not be treated as awarded or spent funds.
- The initial plan included a new two-story market hall and reuse of existing structures; it was not identical to the final factory conversion.
- Project for Public Spaces developed the site plan and an early market study.
- By October 2012, Ann Poe was executive director during finishing work and merchant move-in.
- More than 300 merchant applications were reported, with about twenty permanent merchants expected and responsible for their own build-outs.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids gave Ordover and the founding organization a flood-recovery opening, a valuable industrial site, possible in-kind property support, public negotiation, local vendors, civic volunteers, funding networks, and damaged structures capable of reuse. It also imposed demolition pressure, uncertain control, public scrutiny, and district-wide recovery constraints.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

Ordover and the organization advanced a specific public-market proposition, defended keeping industrial structures, and organized plans and proposed funding around the site. The larger coalition and later leaders, including Poe, translated that foundation into opening operations. No one leader owns the whole conversion.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

The founding effort left staged-risk infrastructure: shared place, gathered traffic, selection, utilities, programming, and smaller spaces through which entrepreneurs could test products without first carrying a whole storefront.

## Evidence Boundary

The PASS Research identifies Ordover as the strongest documented founding leader at the 2010 site-and-capital stage. It supports her presidency, public site request, preservation commitment, funding explanation, and sequencing logic. It also establishes that the board, City, Project for Public Spaces, funders, contractors, volunteers, and later leaders retained independent agency.

Do not call Ann Poe the founder or assign her the 2010 site choice. Do not call Ordover sole founder, owner, financier, architect, contractor, or guarantor. Do not merge the proposed $5.8 million plan with later campaign totals, claim she authorized every final feature, or erase the change from the 2010 mixed plan to the finished form. Do not use John Pinkerton's later stall as the platform-creation decision or claim merchant success without evidence.
