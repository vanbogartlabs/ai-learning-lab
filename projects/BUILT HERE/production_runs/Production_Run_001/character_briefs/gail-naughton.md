# Character Brief — Gail Naughton

## Entrepreneur

Gail Naughton

## One-Sentence Character Thesis

> **Gail Naughton was driven, as her post-flood leadership demonstrates, to preserve an institution's mission by separating its damaged whole into coordinated workstreams that could survive on different schedules and later converge in a safer permanent form.**

This thesis is limited to the PASS Chapter 30 rebuilding record. It does not use blocked Chapter 29 to assign Naughton an unverified emergency decision or private motive.

## Courage Profile

### The Impossible Bet

After flood damage made the museum's former operating arrangement unusable, Naughton led NCSML in maintaining displaced programs and offices while coordinating conservation, capital raising, strategy, movement and elevation of the historic building, expansion, construction, and eventual reopening. The future she pursued was an operating institution before all of its physical parts had returned.

### The Uncertainty

The institution faced uncertain conservation outcomes, restricted funding, donor confidence, engineering and construction execution, schedules, temporary operating capacity, and the future form of the building. Waiting for one permanent answer, repairing in place, abandoning the historic structure, or reducing the mission were structurally safer in some respects. No inspected source records Naughton enumerating those options.

### The Personal Risk

Naughton's supported exposure was executive, strategic, fundraising, professional, schedule, donor-trust, and reputational. The museum's assets, campaign funds, contracts, and liabilities were not hers personally. Board authority, contract signatures, indemnity, delegated powers, and personal financial exposure remain unestablished.

### The Opposition

No single opponent defines the rebuilding. Structural opposition included damaged collections and facilities, displaced staff and programs, incompatible recovery schedules, restricted capital, engineering complexity, competition for land, labor, funding, and speed, and the need to preserve public trust while permanent access remained unavailable.

### The Courageous Decision

> **Despite damaged facilities, fragmented recovery schedules, restricted capital, and dependence on boards, staff, specialists, donors, agencies, and builders, Gail Naughton chose to keep NCSML functioning while leading a permanent rebuilding program because her repeated executive commitments support the inference that she believed the mission could survive only if continuity and reconstruction proceeded together rather than waiting on one complete answer.**

The belief clause is inferred from attributed post-flood leadership, not from blocked Chapter 29 or a recovered statement of private thought.

### Why It Matters

The decision changed rebuilding from repair into institutional design. Programs, collections, staff work, capital, the historic building, and new construction could move separately, then recombine. The museum returned in July 2012 in a moved, elevated, and expanded form without erasing the distributed work that made that return possible.

## Core Identity

Naughton was an institutional entrepreneur: NCSML's president and CEO from 2002 to 2018, responsible for coordinating mission, public presence, strategy, fundraising, and rebuilding. She was not the museum's owner, sole governor, conservator, engineer, mover, designer, fundraiser, or builder.

## What They Wanted

The concrete outcome was a functioning NCSML that remained publicly legible during displacement and returned to a permanent form capable of supporting collections, programs, staff, and future mission more safely than the failed arrangement.

## Why They Could Not Let It Go

Private compulsion is not documented. Naughton's sustained leadership over temporary operations, a campaign of more than $28 million, preservation and movement of the original building, expansion, and reopening supports institutional commitment. This brief does not backfill a Chapter 29 motive or decision.

## What They Saw Before Others Did

Her attributed actions support the interpretation that preservation could require movement and separation rather than restoration of every function in one place on one schedule. Collections, programs, offices, capital, and buildings could travel different paths while remaining parts of one mission.

## Defining Character Traits

- **Coordinating:** aligned distinct workstreams without claiming sole execution.
- **Mission-continuity oriented:** maintained public programming before permanent reopening.
- **Capital-organizing:** led a large campaign while funding uses remained differentiated.
- **Adaptable:** treated movement, temporary space, elevation, and expansion as forms of preservation.
- **Publicly accountable:** carried executive and donor-trust responsibility over a multi-year return.

## Greatest Risk

The greatest supportable risk was that the institution could fragment faster than leadership could coordinate it: collections, staff, programs, capital, construction, and public trust might fail on different schedules before they could be reunited.

## Greatest Obstacle

The greatest obstacle was synchronized rebuilding under distributed authority. Each mission component required different specialists, restrictions, timing, approvals, and measures of completion.

## Defining Decision

- **Options:** remain closed pending one permanent answer, repair in place without expansion, abandon the historic building, reduce the mission, or keep operating while rebuilding through multiple coordinated tracks.
- **Choice:** Naughton led NCSML in displaced operations while assembling conservation, capital, strategy, relocation, elevation, expansion, and construction work for a permanent return.
- **Cost:** executive and reputational accountability, fundraising and coordination exposure, donor-trust risk, schedule complexity, and dependence on many independent actors.
- **Consequence:** under her leadership, NCSML raised more than $28 million, saved and moved the original building, added 30,000 square feet, and reopened in July 2012.

## Contradiction

Naughton preserved place by moving it. She protected institutional wholeness by dividing recovery into separate tracks. The leadership achievement depended on distributed agency rather than personal control over every task.

## Human Stakes

Staff needed work and an operating institution. Czech and Slovak communities and visitors depended on continued access to nonreplaceable holdings. Donors and public agencies needed accountable use of restricted support. Volunteers and specialists carried physical and technical labor that executive summaries can obscure.

## Emotional Arc

- **Ambition:** preserve mission and public access beyond the failed operating arrangement.
- **Pressure:** damaged materials, displacement, restricted capital, and incompatible schedules.
- **Decision:** keep programs and work moving while building a permanent answer on separate tracks.
- **Consequence:** the historic building moved, the institution expanded, and public access returned.
- **Legacy:** Cedar Rapids gains a recovery repertoire based on future use rather than restoration in place alone.

No private emotional state is assigned to Naughton.

## Memorable Details

- Naughton served as NCSML president and CEO from 2002 through 2018.
- The *Rising Above* exhibition operated in the Kosek Building from 2009 to 2016.
- Under her leadership, the institution raised more than $28 million.
- The original building was saved and moved, placed on a new foundation three feet above the flood, and joined by 30,000 square feet of expansion.
- The expanded museum reopened in July 2012, four years after the flood.
- Repository summaries mention frozen books, a map in thirty-two pieces, differentiated conservation, and a reported 480-foot building move; each detail requires its stated qualification and source verification.
- Accounts differ on eleven versus twelve feet of elevation; do not silently harmonize the measurements.

## What Cedar Rapids Gave Them

Cedar Rapids gave Naughton and NCSML a rooted institution in Czech Village, staff, volunteers, donors, temporary space, public attention, city recovery planning, construction capacity, and regional links to universities, conservators, agencies, and funders. The recovering city also created competition for money, land, labor, and speed.

## What They Gave Cedar Rapids

Under Naughton's leadership, NCSML remained publicly legible during displacement and returned in a moved, elevated, and expanded form. The project showed that preservation could adapt buildings and sequence functions around future mission. It did not alone revive Czech Village or cause nearby investment.

## What They Left for the Next Builder

Naughton and NCSML left a Cedar Rapids capability: damaged structures could be evaluated by the future mission they might support, and public/private coalitions could preserve useful space through adaptation.
