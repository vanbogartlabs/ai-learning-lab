## Executive Finding

The complete 35-slot macro-arc is viable:

> possibility → connection → community → industrial systems → labor and civic capacity → Depression/war technology → decentralization and industrial loss → access and inclusion → disaster recovery → adaptation → inheritance

It is not yet production-ready.

The validation found:

- **19 chapter slots with viable person-centered architecture**
- **16 blocked chapter slots:** 7, 8, 10, 11, 12, 20, 21, 22, 25, and 29–35
- a pronounced protagonist/agency trough in Chapters 7–12;
- an ending sequence that has the correct themes but lacks a verified series of increasingly consequential human decisions;
- duplicate or insufficiently distinct uses of Anna Jacobs, Gail Naughton, Arthur Collins, and John Pinkerton;
- person-level authority gaps for Alois Blaha, Caroline Sinclair, Anna Vild, W. J. Greene, Grant Wood, Chris Cuddy, Nathan Boelkins, and Sydney Rieckhoff;
- one broken Chapter 30→31 protagonist handoff;
- chronology braids that must be deliberate and explicit rather than presented as linear inheritance.

The corrected map now aligns Chapter 5 with George Greene and Chapter 6 with James C. Young, preserves the approved 35 slots, marks every chronology braid, and records an exact upstream correction for each blocked slot. It does not manufacture replacement entrepreneurs or decisions where the repository does not yet support them.

## Validation Categories

### 1. Complete Chapter Sequence — PASS

- Exactly 35 approved slots are present, numbered 1–35 without gaps.
- The runtime backlog controls numbering and subjects.
- Each slot now has a candidate protagonist, chronology control, candidate defining decision or explicit deficiency, inherited condition, and gate result.
- The sequence is organized into six cumulative movements.
- Chapters 1–5 are recognized as a human-review delivery batch, not the production scope.

### 2. Entrepreneur Chronology — PASS

- Every slot has a chronology anchor or an explicit unresolved-date warning.
- The sequence preserves the approved backlog's thematic braids rather than silently pretending strict linear chronology.
- Required resets are registered at Chapters 7–9, 13, 14→15, 18→19, 27→28, 33→34, and 35.
- Chapter 35 is defined as an emblematic coda rather than the next chronological event.
- This is an architecture chronology pass; exact dates still require upstream evidence validation.

### 3. Book-Wide Courage Arc — FAIL

- The intended six-stage courage escalation is coherent in outline.
- Sixteen slots do not yet establish a named person making a distinct consequential decision while accepting responsibility for the outcome.
- Chapters 7–12 lose agency in topic-led material about commerce, mutual aid, wealth, housing, and infrastructure.
- Chapters 29–35 repeat recovery, small-bet, and inherited-capability themes without a verified escalation of personal responsibility.
- Architecture cannot pass the Courage Principles by treating association, public spokesperson status, property acquisition, artistic participation, or broad civic context as entrepreneurial courage.

### 4. Inheritance Chain — FAIL

- A specific inheritance is defined or proposed for all 35 slots.
- The chain nevertheless fails because several inherited conditions depend on unverified protagonists or decisions.
- The historical Chapter 30 handoff promised Ann Poe while Chapter 31 delivered John Pinkerton; this mismatch has been rejected but the correct NewBo founding protagonist remains unresolved.
- Chapter 21 lacks a nonduplicative woman entrepreneur.
- Chapter 34 lacks a verified disaster-era small-business decision capable of handing responsibility to Chapter 35.

### 5. Narrative Escalation — FAIL

- Chapters 1–6 provide a strong escalation: plat → participation → power → local crossing → external rail → outward market.
- Chapters 13–28 contain strong industrial, catastrophe, rebuilding, machinery, labor, control, market, failure, and access movements.
- The Chapters 7–12 agency trough interrupts escalation.
- The Chapters 29–35 ending engine has the right thematic order but not yet the required person-level decisions.
- Full escalation cannot pass until the blocked slots become indispensable human stories rather than thematic containers.

### 6. Foreshadowing Opportunities — PASS

The architecture now controls seven motif lines:

1. river;
2. lines and maps;
3. temporary forms before durable institutions;
4. systems beyond one person's control;
5. doors and unequal access;
6. rebuilding as preservation of future choice;
7. stewardship and inheritance.

Each motif has an early setup, middle complication, late return, and required final payoff. These are editorial contracts, not permission to repeat vocabulary or invent scenes.

### 8. Chapter Transitions — FAIL

- The map now records a specific transition for every slot and identifies all chronology resets.
- Chapter 5 correctly passes rail-enabled circulation to Young in Chapter 6.
- The Chapter 30→31 transition remains blocked until an actual NewBo founding decision-maker is identified.
- Chapters 21, 22, 25, 29–35 cannot supply approved transitions until protagonist agency and decisions are validated.
- Transitions may pass city conditions, but may not invent personal contact, sole causation, or inevitability.

### 9. Final Chapter Payoff — FAIL

- The final contract is structurally strong: receive → choose → reinterpret → assume responsibility → hand off.
- Sydney Rieckhoff remains a candidate, not an approved final protagonist.
- Current evidence must establish storefront chronology, financing or lease exposure, operating obligations, alternatives, setbacks, consequences, and a concrete larger commitment.
- If that evidence is insufficient, a stronger contemporary next builder must replace her as protagonist; she may remain a bounded illustrative echo.
- The ending cannot pass merely because booth-to-storefront movement provides a pleasing callback.

### 10. Consistency with `Book_Philosophy.md` — FAIL

- The macro-arc correctly treats Cedar Rapids as opportunity, constraint, and inheritance.
- Nineteen slots meet the people-first standard.
- Sixteen slots currently lack a defensible person-centered entrepreneurial decision or duplicate another chapter.
- Anna Vild's lot restriction, Chris Cuddy's public statement, Nathan Boelkins's objective, and topic-led chapters cannot be treated as sufficient protagonist action without additional evidence.
- Therefore the architecture does not yet satisfy “the protagonists are always people” and “every chapter must earn its place” across the complete manuscript.

### 11. Consistency with `Courage_Principles.md` — FAIL

- The architecture correctly defines courage as action under uncertainty with responsibility, not optimism or hindsight.
- Blocked slots fail because they do not yet answer who acted, what they risked, why waiting was safer, which decision changed the story, and what was inherited.
- Repeated protagonists pass only when later decisions materially deepen uncertainty and responsibility.
- Brown and George Greene pass; Collins Chapter 25, Naughton Chapters 29–30, and Pinkerton Chapters 31/34 require correction or consolidation.

### 12. Consistency with `Publishing_Operating_System_v2.0.md` — PASS

- No downstream production was performed.
- Missing person-level decisions were not invented.
