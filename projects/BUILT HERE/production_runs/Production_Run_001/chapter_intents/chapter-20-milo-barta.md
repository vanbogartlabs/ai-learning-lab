## One-Sentence Chapter Promise

> This chapter is the story of Milo Barta and Lewis Clark co-founding Federal Labor Union 18530 inside Cedar Rapids's packinghouse economy, creating durable worker authority without ownership while leaving Barta's personal livelihood risk and consequence unresolved.

## Why This Chapter Exists

Chapter 19 leaves mobility infrastructure, specialized work, and increasingly concentrated employer authority. Chapter 20 expands the book's definition of building: workers who did not own the industrial enterprise could create a durable institution able to pool participation, make rules, and counter unilateral authority.

The chapter is necessary because its defining act is institutional and shared. Barta and Clark created the named vehicle; later workers, organizers, affiliates, negotiators, and members developed its recognition, agreements, grievances, strikes, and arbitration. The chapter must show durable counterpower without converting structural worker exposure into a manufactured personal-sacrifice story about Barta.

## Central Business Problem

How can workers build durable countervailing authority inside an enterprise they do not own when individual access to work and pay remains under employer control and a new institution has no guaranteed participation, recognition, or staying power?

## Defining Qualified Action

On August 23, 1933, Milo Barta and Lewis Clark co-founded Federal Labor Union 18530 at Wilson's Cedar Rapids packinghouse, creating a named institution through which workers could build collective authority.

Avoiding a named role, supporting organization privately, relying on individual complaint, waiting for others, leaving for other work, or joining a temporary protest were structural alternatives, not options Barta is documented as weighing. His cataloged packinghouse-worker status makes livelihood exposure plausible at a structural level, but the evidence does not establish that he worked for Wilson on that date, knowingly wagered his job, anticipated retaliation, or experienced a personal consequence. Never manufacture courage from that gap.

## Stakes

- **For Barta:** a named co-founding role and packinghouse-worker identity are established; date-specific employer, job, wage, standing, knowingly accepted downside, retaliation, and personal consequence are not.
- **For Clark:** his equal co-founding role must remain visible; the record does not authorize sole credit to Barta.
- **For workers and households:** dependence on employer-controlled work and pay created structural livelihood exposure, while a durable institution could provide representation, rules, collective leverage, grievances, and procedures.
- **For management and the enterprise:** organized worker authority could constrain unilateral action and create the possibility of coordinated interruption, but no Barta-specific confrontation or response is documented.
- **For later members:** institutional durability depended on collective action beyond the founders; later affiliations, agreements, strikes, grievances, and arbitration belong to later actors.
- **For Cedar Rapids:** industrial capacity now included countervailing worker authority, not only owner and management power.

## Emotional Arc

**Beginning — An organized company and dispersed workers:** Set capital, facilities, management, and rules against workers' need for a numbered institution, then state the co-founding act with Barta and Clark equally present.

**Middle — Authority without ownership:** The founding creates a vehicle but guarantees neither participation, recognition, agreement, enforcement, nor survival. Structural livelihood dependence makes the act consequential without proving Barta's private fear or sacrifice.

**End — The institution exceeds its founders:** Later workers develop durable collective capacity. The consequence is institutional continuity, while Barta's personal outcome remains unresolved.

This arc describes institutional pressure, collective action, and reader experience—not Barta's undocumented emotion, conscious risk acceptance, or retaliation.
