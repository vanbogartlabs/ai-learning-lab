## Why This Chapter Exists

This chapter advances the flood story from loss into institutional design. Rebuilding was not synonymous with repairing walls or waiting until every part could return at once. Mission, collections, staff work, public programs, restricted capital, engineering, and construction moved on different schedules and under distributed authority.

Naughton's decision is distinct from Chapter 29's unresolved emergency gate. Chapter 30 begins after damage and displacement are documented enough to become a portfolio of projects. It asks whether leadership will keep the institution functioning while deliberately rebuilding its permanent form rather than merely wait, repair in place, abandon the historic structure, reduce the mission, or close.

## Central Business Problem

How can a damaged institution remain publicly legible and operational when its collections, staff, programs, capital, historic building, and construction cannot recover together or under one person's control?

The problem is coordination under distributed authority: every mission component has different specialists, restrictions, approvals, schedules, and definitions of completion.

## Defining Decision

After flood damage made NCSML's former operating arrangement unusable, Gail Naughton led the choice to keep the institution functioning through displaced programs and offices while assembling the separate conservation, capital, strategy, relocation, elevation, expansion, and construction work required for a permanent return.

Remaining closed pending one complete answer, repairing in place without expansion, abandoning the historic building, or reducing the mission were structurally safer in some respects; no inspected source records Naughton enumerating them. This post-damage operating-and-rebuilding choice is the chapter's sole defining decision. Emergency actions assigned to blocked Chapter 29 are outside scope.
