# Chapter Intent — Chapter 21: Women in the Business of Building

## Chapter Title

**Women in the Business of Building**

## Entrepreneur

**Mary Snyder** — use the approved Character Brief at `../character_briefs/mary-snyder.md`. Snyder is the protagonist; Anne Jordan must remain visible as the essential partner in the later two-vehicle company and joint property commitments.

## One-Sentence Chapter Promise

> This chapter is the story of Mary Snyder turning her own automobile into licensed work during the Depression, then making that personal market entry durable through partnership and coordinated service.

## Why This Chapter Exists

This chapter gives the book a documented woman whose authority rests in a specific asset, license, operating choice, and continuing responsibility rather than association or symbolism. It shows that Snyder did not escape dependence when wage work disappeared: she changed its terms by making one car carry the burdens of capital, workplace, and service delivery.

The chapter advances the movement from industrial authority to women’s operating authority. Because Chapter 20 remains upstream-blocked at its personal-risk gate, this chapter may receive only the book-level condition that concentrated work created a need for organized authority; it must not present Milo Barta, or any unvalidated Chapter 20 protagonist, as Snyder’s predecessor or personal influence.

## Central Business Problem

How can Snyder turn a personally owned car into dependable paid service when demand, vehicle reliability, customer acceptance, safety, and continued access to the market remain uncertain?

A car is not yet a taxi business. Snyder must obtain a license, attract fares, complete rides safely, maintain the asset, and repeat the service. Partnership with Jordan later adds capacity while also adding coordination across two drivers, two vehicles, schedules, customers, territory, and property.

## Defining Decision

After losing her Marion Ford-garage job and failing to find another, Snyder chooses in 1932 to obtain a chauffeur’s license and put her own automobile into commercial taxi service rather than preserve it for private use or continue waiting for wage employment.

The decision is Snyder’s personal market entry. The subsequent formation of Mari-An Cab Company with Anne Jordan is the consequence that makes the service more durable; it must not be retroactively credited to Snyder alone.

## Stakes

- **For Snyder:** Her own car becomes both working capital and workplace, exposed to commercial mileage, maintenance, breakdown, accident, theft, uncertain fares, and interruption of income.
- **For Jordan:** The later two-vehicle company and office commitment create shared operating responsibility; exact ownership shares and division of authority are unknown.
- **For customers:** Service depends on a licensed driver and functioning vehicle meeting routes and schedules, including Marion depot traffic and regional trips.
- **For the business:** One failed asset or unsafe encounter can threaten both service capacity and livelihood.
- **For the chapter’s ethics:** Women’s authority must be shown through documented operation without making Snyder representative of all women or erasing the municipal boundary between Marion and Cedar Rapids.

## Emotional Arc

**Beginning — One asset under pressure:** Rewind explicitly from the unresolved 1933 labor-authority chapter to Snyder’s 1932 entry. Put the own-car/licensed-entry truth on the opening page and within the first ten percent, before longevity, mileage, or sale.

**Middle — Possession becomes operation:** The car accumulates obligations: license, fares, fuel, maintenance, routes, strangers, safety, and schedules. Jordan’s partnership expands capability but also makes coordination indispensable.

**Decision — Commercial use before proof:** Snyder puts the car and her own labor into the market without dependable demand.

**End — Independence through coordination:** Mari-An becomes repeatable through partner labor, vehicles, customer trust, depot schedules, regional reach, and property. The emotional force lies in the asset’s changing burden and sustained operation, not invented desperation, confidence, or triumph.

## Reader Takeaway

> Mary Snyder made her own car carry two jobs at once: it was both her capital and her workplace.

A builder can gain more control over work by accepting direct responsibility for the dependencies that make a service repeatable.

## Historical Material Required

1. **Opening-page truth:** In 1932 Snyder obtained a chauffeur’s license and converted her own car into a taxi after losing wage work and failing to find another job.
2. **Precise geography:** Snyder grew up in Cedar Rapids, but the garage job and the Marion-based Mari-An Cab Company were in Marion; service belonged to a connected regional market.
3. **The personal asset:** The first car was Snyder’s own, making the market-entry exposure attributable without inventing its make, value, financing, or insurance.
4. **Anne Jordan’s authority:** Snyder and Jordan formed the two-vehicle Mari-An Cab Company together; later company and office-building commitments must remain joint unless stronger records allocate them.
5. **Operating coordination:** Licensing, vehicles, drivers, customers, routes, safety, Marion’s passenger-train schedule, and regional trips should show what converted entry into an operating business.
6. **Continuity:** Jordan retired in 1942 and Snyder continued driving the remaining cab; Snyder sold the business in 1964. These are consequences, not proof that success was visible in 1932.
7. **Evidence readiness:** Research, Character Brief, and NDR are PASS/approved for intent use at moderate retrospective-source confidence. Mileage, routes, property, and sale details require contemporaneous verification before publication and must be attributed or qualified as directed upstream.

## Connection to Cedar Rapids

Cedar Rapids gave Snyder a documented upbringing and a nearby urban market connected to Marion through roads, passengers, destinations, and taxi-territory boundaries. That adjacency created opportunity without erasing the city line.

Snyder and Jordan gave the Cedar Rapids–Marion area woman-operated passenger mobility and demonstrated authority over licensed vehicles, schedules, customers, safety, property, and continuity. Their contribution is regional exchange, not an invented Cedar Rapids headquarters.
