# Chapter Intent — Chapter 4: Two Banks, One City

## Chapter Title

**Chapter 4: Two Banks, One City**

## Entrepreneur

**David W. King**, with Mary King’s joint platting credit preserved.

Approved Character Brief: `../character_briefs/david-w-king.md`

## Driving Ambition

King’s documented actions support the chapter-specific inference that passage and a west-bank destination could make one another more useful. This is a structural interpretation of the ferry privilege and David and Mary King’s Kingston platting, not a recovered statement of King’s motive or a documented master plan.

## One-Sentence Chapter Promise

> This chapter is the story of David W. King pairing a temporary crossing privilege with David and Mary King’s west-bank development before traffic, continuous passage, settlement demand, or return was secure.

## Why This Chapter Exists

Chapter 3 leaves productive activity on both banks, making division costly. Chapter 4 shows King treating that division as a business problem: a ferry could make Kingston more usable, while Kingston could create reasons to cross. The chapter advances the book from productive activity to local connection without turning King into a bridge builder.

The chapter is indispensable because King’s proposition contains its own limit. His legal advantage was temporary and expressly allowed a future bridge; if local passage became important, the city would need connection beyond one private privilege and, eventually, beyond the local market.

## Central Business Problem

How can King make passage commercially useful while helping make the west bank worth reaching when traffic, river continuity, settlement demand, and the privilege’s useful life remain uncertain?

## Defining Decision

King chose to pursue the protected ferry opportunity alongside David and Mary King’s platting and expansion of Kingston rather than operate only a temporary crossing, hold land without organizing a settlement, or wait for durable public infrastructure.

The safer alternatives are analytical, not a recovered deliberation. The decision couples access and land before either can guarantee the other, while accepting that flood, expiration, and a future bridge can interrupt or replace the proposition.

## Stakes

- **For King:** a ten-year privilege, limited to one mile upstream and downstream, is exposed to uncertain traffic, physical interruption, and legal obsolescence. His exact capital, revenue, profit, and loss are unknown.
- **For Mary King:** her joint platting credit must remain visible; the record does not allocate ownership, authority, labor, or exposure between the Kings.
- **For buyers and residents:** west-bank land is less useful if passage fails, while those who commit to lots depend on access the river can remove.
- **For merchants and travelers:** a working crossing enlarges the reachable local market, but one temporary ferry cannot guarantee dependable movement.

## Emotional Arc

**Beginning — Opportunity already limited:** The reader meets a protected ferry privilege with an expiration date and a future-bridge exception already inside it.

**Middle — Coupled uncertainty:** Passage needs a destination and Kingston needs passage. The reported 1851 washout reveals how quickly the river can weaken both propositions.

**End — Usefulness reveals insufficiency:** The crossing helps make two banks more usable together, yet its temporary private form exposes a larger unmet need for dependable external connection.

This movement describes the business proposition and the reader’s experience, not King’s undocumented private emotion.

## Reader Takeaway

A temporary crossing and an unproved destination could reinforce one another, but their usefulness made the limits of local connection impossible to ignore.

A reader should be able to repeat:

> King paired a ten-year ferry privilege that allowed a future bridge with Kingston’s development, making local connection valuable enough to reveal the need for a more dependable external link.

## Historical Material Required

1. The ten-year ferry privilege opposite Cedar Rapids and its one-mile upstream-and-downstream exclusivity.
2. The requirement for a suitable landing and the privilege’s express preservation of future bridge construction.
3. David and Mary King’s joint credit for platting and expanding Kingston.
4. The disputed first-plat date—1850 in one later account and 1852 in preservation studies—qualified once without taking over the chapter.
5. The ferry-and-Kingston pairing as a bounded structural interpretation, not a recovered agreement or private plan.
6. The reported 1851 ferry washout as concise evidence of physical fragility, without invented damage, duration, or response.
7. Later bridges only briefly, as evidence that the crossing need exceeded one temporary privilege and as an explicit limit on King’s credit.
8. The move from an enlarged two-bank market to the unmet need for dependable external connection.

## Historical Material To Avoid

- Bridge chronology, engineering, commissioners, contractors, companies, votes, bonds, annexation procedure, or municipal expansion except for one concise consequence if essential.
- Any claim that King built, financed, owned, directed, or caused later bridges. He receives no bridge-builder credit.
- Sole credit for Kingston or an invented division of action and exposure between David and Mary King.
- Ferry traffic, tolls, revenue, workforce, financing, exact landing, lot totals, sales, returns, or quantified personal loss.
- King’s undocumented biography, motive, beliefs, words, emotions, negotiations, dialogue, or scenes.
- Any claim that King caused Greene’s rail promotion, personally handed him an opportunity, or guaranteed later growth.

## Connection to Cedar Rapids

**What Cedar Rapids gives King:** A growing but divided market in which activity on both banks makes passage valuable. The Cedar creates scarcity for the crossing while flood, ice, and open water prevent legal protection from becoming dependable service.

**What King gives Cedar Rapids:** A bounded crossing-and-land proposition that helps make the two banks more usable together, with Kingston’s platting credited jointly to David and Mary King. He does not give the city its later bridges.

## Opening Strategy

Put the Narrative Discovery Record’s One Truth on the opening page and the bridge exception within the first 10 percent:

> David W. King paired a temporary ferry privilege with David and Mary King’s platting and expansion of Kingston before traffic or settlement demand was proven. The privilege already preserved a future bridge, so the crossing proposition contained its own possible replacement; King did not build, finance, own, or direct the later bridges.

Open with the two clocks inside the opportunity: ten years of legal protection and possible bridge replacement from the beginning. Establish Mary King’s joint credit and King’s non-bridge-builder boundary immediately. Do not open with biography, annexation, bridge history, or an invented ferry or flood scene.

## Evidence Readiness

**Ready to draft:** The approved record supports the temporary ferry privilege, its territorial and bridge limits, David and Mary King’s joint Kingston platting credit, the reported washout, the coupled access-and-land interpretation with qualification, and the outward business inheritance.

**Prohibited in draft:** Bridge-builder credit; sole Kingston credit; invented motives or scenes; unverified tolls, traffic, finances, losses, lot performance, or role allocation; and personal or causal links to Greene’s rail work.

## Transition
